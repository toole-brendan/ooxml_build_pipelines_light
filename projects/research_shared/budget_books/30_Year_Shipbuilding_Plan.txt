FOREWORD FROM THE

SECRETARY OF THE NAVY                                                             (ACTING)
                                      Since I first raised my right hand and swore an oath to defend this nation,
                                      my focus has been on the warfighter. Today, as the Acting Secretary of the
                                      Navy, that commitment remains my North Star.
                                      We are at a strategic inflection point. For decades, while America’s
                                      attention was elsewhere, our adversaries built fleets at a pace not seen
                                      in generations. During this time, our own maritime industrial base was
                                      weakened by inconsistent demand and misaligned priorities. This left
                                      our Fleet smaller, our shipyards reduced in numbers and atrophied in
                                      capability, and our people facing unacceptable risk. We will not allow
                                      these conditions to continue.
                                      Over a century ago, President Theodore Roosevelt sent our fleet of
                                      battleships and their escorts on an unprecedented voyage around the
                                      globe to demonstrate America’s interests, industrial might and resolve.
                                      The Great White Fleet was a clear, unmistakable signal of peace through
                                      strength.
Today, under the leadership of President Trump, we will launch the modern successor to that vision: the
Golden Fleet. This is not merely a course correction; it is a generational undertaking to restore America’s
position as a seapower state. It requires a clear-eyed strategy, sustained investment, and a direct commitment
to the American worker who builds the ships and the Sailor and Marine who sail them into the fight.
This Shipbuilding Plan is our roadmap. It is built on three clear, enduring principles:
 • Change How We Do Business: We are shifting from a slow, compliance-based bureaucracy to an
   accountable, warfighting enterprise. We will demand performance, reward speed, and empower our
   partners to deliver. We will no longer tolerate the backlogs that put our mission and America’s sons and
   daughters at risk.
 • Enhance Maritime Dominance: We will build a larger, more lethal, and more balanced fleet. The Golden
   Fleet will deliver a high-low mix of advanced combatants, cost-effective frigates, and unmanned systems,
   giving our commanders the combat mass and flexibility to win any fight, any time.
 • Revitalize Our Industrial Base: In accordance with the President’s Executive Order, we will “Restore
   America’s Maritime Dominance” and ignite a renaissance in American shipbuilding. Through a stable,
   long-term demand signal, we will unleash private investment, expand our manufacturing capacity across
   the nation, and create thousands of high-skill American jobs. A strong industrial base is not just an
   economic goal; it is a national security imperative.
This plan outlines how we will deliver the capability our warfighters deserve. Our success will be measured
by one metric: a larger, more capable Fleet – manned and unmanned – ready to defend our homeland and
project power globally.
To our Sailors, Marines, and the American workers who support them: I will always be beside you. Now, we
have work to do.


Hung Cao
Acting Secretary of the Navy

FOREWORD FROM THE

CHIEF OF NAVAL OPERATIONS
                                      The United States Navy exists to fight and win our Nation’s wars at sea. The
                                      Fleet is our most decisive instrument of national power, and its strength
                                      begins in the Foundry, is delivered through the Fleet, and is proven in
                                      the Fight. This 30-year shipbuilding plan reflects the Navy’s warfighting
                                      framework as articulated in the US Navy Fighting Instructions in order to
                                      execute the Navy Warfighting Concept, and it aligns our investments to
                                      ensure the Fleet we build is ready for the conflicts we may face, not just the
                                      ones we expect.

                                      We are operating in an era defined by persistent competition, rapid
                                      technological change, and adversaries who are expanding their naval
                                      forces at scale. Because the future fight cannot be predicted with certainty,
                                      our approach is guided by the Hedge Strategy that builds a balanced,
                                      adaptable force designed to maintain decision advantage, preserve
                                      escalation dominance, and prevail across the full spectrum of operations,
                                      from daily campaigning to high-end combat.

Therefore, to execute the Golden Fleet Initiative, this plan delivers strength in all three elements of naval power.

The Foundry is our industrial base: shipyards, suppliers, maintainers, and the maritime workforce that make
naval power possible. Continued stable demand, on-time and at-cost production, disciplined requirements,
and long-term investment by industry, are essential to restoring the capacity to build and sustain the Fleet at the
speed and scale required.

The Fleet must be tailorable, upgradeable, and ready today while prepared for tomorrow. We will field a high-low
mix of platforms, integrate unmanned and autonomous systems, increase payload capacity, and ensure the
power and digital architecture needed for future weapons and networks. Leveraging a smart mix of capabilities
allows us to solve the most pressing operational problems faced by Combatant Commanders globally,
delivering scalable, tailorable forces that can be applied across the full range of military operations.

The Fight is the purpose of it all. We build ships to generate and execute combat power. This plan delivers a Fleet
able to distribute, concentrate, and sustain force globally, providing Combatant Commanders credible options
at our timing and tempo and ensuring the Navy remains ready to fight and win.



Admiral Daryl Caudle
34th Chief of Naval Operations

FOREWORD FROM THE

COMMANDANT OF THE
MARINE CORPS
                                    The Marine Corps is the nation’s premier expeditionary force-in-readiness,
                                    designed to operate as an integrated element of naval power. Our distinct
                                    contribution to the Joint Force is the ability to project power from the sea,
                                    assuring allies and partners while providing a persistent, stabilizing forward
                                    presence. In an era of emerging great power competition, the Navy-Marine
                                    Corps team is a critical tool for controlling the maritime domain, shaping
                                    the behavior of adversaries, and responding to crises with speed and
                                    decisiveness.

                                     Our strategy is centered on maintaining a decisive expeditionary
                                     advantage, ensuring our forces can gain and maintain access in the world’s
                                     most contested littorals. This approach leverages the unique capabilities
                                     of the Amphibious Ready Group and Marine Expeditionary Unit (ARG/
                                     MEU), the premier force offering of the Marine Corps. Forward deployed,
                                     the MEU provides our national leadership and Combatant Commanders
                                     with combat credible forces that are persistently on-scene and contribute
to deterrence, campaigning, crisis response, and combat operations. A continuous 3.0 ARG/MEU presence is
required to provide Geographic Combatant Commanders, and our Nation, with the forces they need to meet the
challenges presented across the globe.

This Shipbuilding Plan significantly improves our posture and marks a historic and unprecedented commitment
to our amphibious forces, representing the most significant fleet recapitalization effort in decades. Through
committed procurement of modern platforms, including LHA, LPD, and Medium Landing Ships, we are making
a generational investment in our capacity for naval campaigning and crisis response. This undertaking will
guarantee that your Marines and Sailors remain forward postured, holding potential adversaries at risk and
standing ready to answer the nation’s call with overwhelming force.



Gen. Eric M. Smith
39th Commandant of the Marine Corps

APPENDIX 3: ANNUAL FUNDING FOR SUSTAINMENT
The FY19 NDAA directed reporting cost considerations of owning and operating a larger force. The priorities
stated in the body of this report require the DoN to ensure operations and sustainment accounts are funded
properly to achieve a ready and capable force.

Operations and sustainment funding to support the size of the fleet is essential to equip, maintain and repair
the battle force. Appropriately phased sustainment funding must be consistent with the size of the battle
force. To be capable, ready, and lethal, the Navy must remain balanced across the elements of readiness,
modernization, and force structure. When the life of a ship is extended, the sustainment requirement grows
as the age of the ship increases. Moreover, sustainment resources programmed to shift from a retiring ship
to a new ship must now stay in place for the duration of the extension. The sustainment requirement grows
until equilibrium is reached at the desired higher force inventory, when deliveries match retirements and all
resourcing accounts reach steady state at a higher, enduring cost.

The sustainment cost in Figures A3-1 represents the funding programmed in the FYDP with FY31 funding
levels inflated forward using Office of the Secretary of War inflation indices applied to the inventory shown
in Appendix 1, Table A1-5. Included in this sustainment estimate are personnel, planned maintenance, and
baseline operations, which represent those costs tied directly to owning and operating a ship. Funding is
shown in then-year dollars (TY$). Figure A3-1 does not capture all costs. For example, long-range costs such
as modernization and ordnance (threat and technology driven), infrastructure and training (services spread
across many ships), and aviation detachments are not included. Similar to procurement, estimates become
less accurate further into the future.

capability vessels providing cargo transfer and support functions. With an average vessel age over 40 years,

The sealift force consists of two primary elements: prepositioning (PREPO) and surge sealift vessels.

•       PREPO Fleet: Operating under the Military Sealift Command (MSC), the PREPO fleet supports the Marine
        Corps’ requirement for 2.1 million square feet of cargo capacity. The FY27 fleet is comprised of seven

        retirement of current vessels starting in the early FY30s.

•       Surge Fleet: The surge sealift fleet, managed by MSC and the Maritime Administration (MARAD),
        supports broader joint requirements with a mandated capacity of 10.5 million square feet. In FY27, this
        fleet includes 43 RORO vessels and six special capability ships. Recapitalization for this component is
        measured by total cargo capacity rather than a specific number of hulls.

To sustain these capabilities, the PB27 budget continues the Navy’s dual strategy of acquiring used
commercial RORO ships and initiating a new construction program. Through FY25, the Navy procured seven
large used vessels. The plan is to purchase up to four more in FY26 and a final one in FY27, reaching the
congressionally authorized limit of twelve used ships. This approach is designed to replace cargo capacity lost
as older ships are retired, ensuring the total required sealift capability is consistently met.



PROCUREMENT ACTIVITY
To recapitalize the sealift fleet, the Navy is funding MARAD to acquire used commercial vessels and design
new ones. MARAD has contracted a Vessel Acquisition Manager (VAM) for procurements and a design firm for
two new vessels, with delivery expected in the early FY30s.

The offshore petroleum distribution (AG) capability will be replaced by a network of systems integrated into the
Joint Petroleum Over-the-Shore network, rather than a direct one-for-one vessel replacement.

Following an FY24 analysis, the crane ship (T-ACS) fleet will be recapitalized with seven new ships from two
variants: new feeder container ships and conversions of two T-ESDs. This approach is designed to maximize
capability in austere ports, at-sea transloading, and VLS reloads. T-ESD conversions will start in FY27, with
feeder ship procurement beginning in FY30 to align with current T-ACS retirements.

UNMANNED SURFACE VESSEL (USV) FAMILY OF SYSTEMS (FOS) STRATEGY
The United States faces a strategic inflection point where peer adversaries have achieved naval mass that
the U.S. cannot match with traditional shipbuilding alone. To counter this and deliver warfighting capability
fast, the USV FoS strategy outlines a rapid, competitive path to deliver affordable, scalable, and adaptable
unmanned surface capabilities in response to rapidly evolving Navy requirements. This initiative is a strategic
effort to:
•   Augment the Main Battle Force: Serve as a direct, dual-use supplement to existing ready forces,
    providing a flexible “tailored force” to enhance the nation’s maritime posture without disrupting Tier 1
    shipyard capacity.
•   Create Strategic Complexity and Deterrence: Increase the quantity and distribution of assets within
    the battlespace to complicate and confuse adversary targeting and decision-making in support of critical
    Naval missions.
•   Strategic Industrial Base Revitalization: Strengthen our sovereign industrial capability by revitalizing the
    nation’s Tier 2 and Tier 3 commercial shipbuilding industrial base to produce vessels now while building
    the capacity to support and expand traditional shipbuilding.
This USV FoS acquisition strategy is aligned to a variety of Combatant Commander priorities including
Electronic Maneuver Warfare, massed OWA, persistent Maritime Domain Awareness, distributed Contested
Logistics, Maritime governance, partner support, gray zone deterrence, Exclusive Economic Zones monitoring,
partner sustainment, low signature presence, chokepoint defense, infrastructure protection, and coastal
denial.

This acquisition strategy accelerates the delivery of meaningful capability to the warfighter by creating
a dynamic, mission-driven marketplace for USVs. By prioritizing demonstrated operational outcomes,
modularity, and agility, the approach ensures the fleet is equipped with the most current technology.

This is not a traditional acquisition and will not follow a standard framework. Instead, projects will be
executed under the Other Transaction authority of 10 U.S.C. § 4022. This approach accelerates capability
delivery by enabling rapid prototyping with a broad range of industry partners. To incentivize industry’s internal
investment, payments will only be made for demonstrated operational success, with opportunities for non-
competitive, follow-on production agreements.

The marketplace is built on a foundation of sustained competition. The Government will not fund bespoke
prototype design or fabrication. Instead, industry partners will be invited to present their existing capabilities
as part of a four-criteria qualification process that assesses their business model, platform design,
manufacturing readiness, and on-water autonomy performance. Through this repeatable approach of
“mission-driven on-ramps,” the Navy can continuously assess and qualify partners, allowing the acquisition of
versatile, production-ready vessels designed to carry a wide range of payloads.

The financial cornerstone of this strategy is a public-private partnership that leverages commercial investment
to deliver capability at a fraction of the traditional cost. Based on the operational timeline and expected threat
level, the program management office will determine the most appropriate contract vehicle and ownership

UNDERSEA ROBOTIC AND AUTONOMOUS SYSTEMS FAMILY OF SYSTEMS
(U-RAS FOS)
Due to the rapidly changing, complex undersea warfighting environment, the Navy is integrating Unmanned
Undersea Vehicles (UUVs) into our fleet at a rapid pace. These UUVs are contributing to how we are changing
the character of undersea warfare. The U-RAS FoS is essential to the Navy’s path to deliver affordable,
scalable, and adaptable unmanned undersea capabilities in response to rapidly evolving Navy requirements.

In the U-RAS FoS, XLUUVs are pier-launched vessels with a modular design that can accommodate a variety
of payloads. XLUUVs will have a modular payload bay, with defined interfaces that will support current and
future payloads. The XLUUV effort will include support of future systems and integration of current and
future payloads. Providing the Navy with unprecedented optionality for scalable payloads and sensor suites
tailored for missions ranging from surveillance to strike. As the requirements and acquisition strategy are
further refined, this approach will support future budget and procurement decisions for increasing undersea
capability.
