## Title

**Two Routes to Revenue** | A new entrant reaches a federal program two ways — as a prime,
or through the prime's supply chain — and the structure of the award record determines which
route is open.

## Purpose / takeaway

Give leadership one disciplined read of any federal program: **is the revenue reachable, and
by which route — prime or subcontract?** A large or recurring award is not, by itself, an
addressable opportunity; addressability turns on whether — and how — a new entrant may legally
reach the work. The structure of the award record answers that. The governing lesson:
**"closed as a prime" is not "no revenue"** — when the prime role is committed or restricted,
the addressable demand moves down to the supply chain, and the next two slides work each route
in turn.

## The argument

Separate two things the eye conflates:

- **Recurrence** — *will the Government buy this requirement again?*
- **Addressability** — *can this company reach and win the action, and by what route?*

A near-certain follow-on on a vehicle the company cannot access as a prime is not a direct
prime opportunity — but it is frequently a **subcontract** opportunity, and the same record
says so. There are exactly **two routes to the revenue**:

- **Prime** — a direct contractual relationship with the Government (privity with the
  Government). The company holds the award and is paid by the Government.
- **Subcontractor** — a supplier to the entity that holds the prime award (privity with the
  prime). The company is paid by the prime, not the Government.

Which route is open is **dictated by the structure of the award record** — extent of
competition, award structure (single- vs. multiple-award), vehicle type and subtype,
fair-opportunity posture, and whether the requirement is still forming (transitioning out of
RDT&E) or locked to incumbents (multiyear/block buy, sole-source). The upper indicators
(object type, obligations vs. ceiling, extent competed, award structure) are hard data and
decide quickly; the lower judgments (timing, ability to perform) are where the analyst adds
value.

## Reading the route from the award record — the core mapping

The heart of the slide. Each posture is read from named fields and resolves to one route.

| Read off the award record | What it indicates | Route it opens |
|---|---|---|
| Open / full-and-open solicitation or recompete (`extentCompeted`) | Any responsible offeror may compete | **Prime** — submit a proposal |
| Multiple-award IDC, orders on FAR 16.505 fair opportunity (`multipleOrSingleAwardIdc`, `fairOpportunity`) | Competition limited to vehicle holders | **Prime** — seat onto the pool at re-solicitation (on-ramp); or supply a current holder |
| Other Transaction / consortium / prototype (`awardOrIDVType` = *Other Transaction Agreement*) | Direct, non-FAR agreement; performer holds it | **Prime-like** — join the consortium and compete for projects |
| Requirement transitioning out of RDT&E into procurement / O&M | A program of record is forming | **Prime** — position ahead of the production solicitation |
| Definitive multiyear or block-buy prime, dual-source (`multiyearContract`, definitive contract) | Prime role committed years out | **Subcontractor** — supply the established primes |
| Single-award IDC, or sole-source incumbent (`extentCompeted` = not competed) | One holder receives the work | **Subcontractor** — qualify as a second source; supply the holder |

**Single-award ≠ sole-source:** a fully competed vehicle can have a single winner. Conversely,
an order under a competed multiple-award vehicle can be directed to one holder under an
exception to fair opportunity. Read the structure, not the headline.

## The two routes — slide content

### Route 1 — Prime (privity with the Government) → expanded on slide 02

- **Available when:** an open solicitation or full-and-open recompete; a multiple-award
  vehicle with an on-ramp; an Other Transaction / consortium; or a capability moving out of
  RDT&E into a program of record.
- **How a new entrant enters:** submit a proposal to an open solicitation; compete for a seat
  when a multiple-award pool re-solicits (**on-ramp**); join the consortium and compete for
  prototype projects under an OT; shape and position ahead of a forming requirement.
- **Timing signal:** the **ordering-period end** (`lastDateToOrder`) of a multiple-award
  vehicle; an approaching recompete; the **RDT&E → procurement/O&M transition** that signals a
  production competition forming.
- **Folds together** the open-competition, on-ramp, and OT/consortium entry modes with the
  pre-recompete and program-expansion signals that tell a new entrant a prime competition is
  forming — i.e. *which* prime mode applies and *when* it opens.

### Route 2 — Subcontractor (privity with the prime) → expanded on slide 03

- **Available when:** the prime role is committed or restricted — a definitive multiyear or
  block-buy contract, a single-award vehicle, or a sole-source incumbent.
- **How a new entrant enters:** qualify and sell to the holder as a subcontractor/supplier —
  *competing into* **fragmented** work-types, or offering a **second source** where one supplier
  dominates a critical one; team under a FAR 9.6 contractor team arrangement. *(Both angles are
  worked on slide 03.)*
- **Timing signal:** the prime's **re-procurement cadence** — each new block or lot re-opens
  first-tier sourcing — and the front-loading of subaward dollars after each prime award.

## Annotations — the two reframes

**A closed prime is not a closed market** *(feeds the subcontractor route; → slide 03).* When
the prime role is committed years ahead — multiyear procurement (FAR 17.1), block/lot buy, or a
major quantity-adding modification — the addressable demand sits *below* the prime: components,
castings, electronics, software, test equipment, engineering changes, depot/sustainment. It
releases as **AP/LLTM** (advance procurement / long-lead-time material) and **EOQ** (economic
order quantity) buys, placed by the prime or an upper-tier supplier, **not** by the Government.
First-tier (FFATA) subaward reporting is a **floor, not a census**.

**Enter before the requirement closes** *(feeds the prime route's OT mode).* Other Transactions
and SBIR/STTR are **entry authorities, not pricing types**. A competitively awarded prototype
OT can lead to a **production follow-on without new competition** (DFARS 206.001-70 exception);
an SBIR/STTR **Phase III** can be awarded sole-source to the firm whose earlier work it derives
from. Enter at the topic, prototype, consortium, BAA, or CSO stage — before the production
follow-on closes to later entrants.

## Portal-visibility overlay (a property of the set, not a route)

Most addressable awards were **never advertised on SAM.gov Contract Opportunities** — FAR 16.5
orders and OTs are synopsis-exempt by law and never appear. Award-data analysis runs on
**SAM.gov Contract Awards, USAspending, FPDS, and SAM.gov Subaward Reporting**, not the
opportunities portal. Set this apart beneath the routes; do **not** wire it into the route
logic.

## Right-rail commentary

A bold finding sentence, then non-bold support, beside the route logic rather than interleaved.

- **Finding:** A large award is a posture to classify, not an opportunity to assume. The
  structure of the record names the route — prime or supply chain.
- The upper indicators are hard data — object type, obligations vs. ceiling, extent competed,
  award structure — and resolve quickly; the judgment is in timing and ability to perform.
- The decision is **route, not bid / no-bid:** compete as prime, on-ramp onto the vehicle,
  join the consortium, or supply the prime.
- A **closed prime is not a closed market** — multiyear and block buys release demand below the
  prime (AP/LLTM, EOQ, first-tier subcontracts).
- Most addressable work was never on **SAM.gov Contract Opportunities** — it is in Contract
  Awards / USAspending / FPDS / Subaward Reporting.

## Emphasis intent (intent, not mechanism)

The **two routes** are the focal pair and should read as the payoff. The **landscape → route
mapping is the pivot** — the turn from "a large award" to "your route in" — and should read as
the slide's hinge. Everything upstream is classification logic and should recede. State the
intent; the build stage chooses how to express it.

## Quantification discipline

This is a **generalized method slide — no program names and no dollar figures on the face.**
Keep every posture conceptual. The market-sizing reconciliation, the named programs, and the
award data (ceilings, obligations, TAS) live on the worked-example slides (02, the prime route;
03, the subcontractor route) — this placement also satisfies the request to carry the hard
contract data on slides 02–03 rather than here. If an illustrative posture is shown, name it in
real terms (e.g. "a multiple-award IDC on FAR 16.505 fair opportunity") — never a specific
program or dollar.

## Accuracy guardrails

- An **IDV is not necessarily an IDIQ** (BPA, BOA, FSS are IDVs too) — name the subtype.
- **Single-award ≠ sole-source** — a fully competed vehicle can have one winner.
- A **modification is not a new award** — a quantity-adding mod is committed demand; an
  administrative mod or deobligation is not.
- An **unexercised option is not guaranteed revenue.**
- A **first-tier subaward report is a floor, not a census** — FFATA captures qualifying
  first-tier reports only. Reserve **"subaward"** for the reporting context; use
  **subcontractor / supplier** for the commercial relationship.
- An **OT is an entry authority, not universally open** — and a production follow-on may be
  sole-source to the prototype awardee.
- **Never sum** a parent IDV ceiling with child-order obligations, or a shared multiple-award
  ceiling across holders.
- Award data is **evidence and inference, not certainty** — records lag, names change, lower
  tiers are incomplete, and requirements migrate to different vehicles.
