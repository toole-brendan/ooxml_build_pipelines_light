# Slide Spec — Addressability (Generalized Decision Cascade) — Slide 01

Type: Body. **Method opener for slides 02–03.** This slide is the generalized read; slide 02

## Title

**Addressability** | A large award isn't an opportunity until it clears five gates — and the
gate it fails as prime names the route in.

## Purpose / takeaway

Teach the reader to read *any* federal award and answer one question — **is there reachable
revenue here, and by what route?** — by classifying the record, then walking five gates:
**demand × access × timing × ability to perform × economics**. The lesson: "not addressable as
prime" is **not** "no money." A failed gate *redirects* to a non-prime route — on-ramp,
subcontract/teaming, supply chain, or consortium/OT — rather than ending the opportunity.

## The argument

Open by separating two things the eye conflates:

- **Event probability** — *will the Government buy this again?*
- **Addressability** — *can this company reach and win the action?*

A near-certain follow-on on a vehicle the company cannot access is not a direct opportunity.
Addressability is then a product of five gates; if any gate is zero as prime, the apparent
opportunity is not directly addressable *as prime* — but it may redirect down the performance
chain or toward a later contracting action. The upper gates (object type, obligations vs.
ceiling, extent of competition) are hard data and decide quickly; the lower gates (timing,
ability to perform) are judgment, where the analyst adds value. *Route* is treated here as the
**output** (the leaves), not a gate; **timing** is promoted to its own gate because for federal
work the contracting-action calendar is often the binding constraint.

## The decision cascade — slide content, top to bottom

**Precondition — classify the record.** Name the object before gating it: definitive (standalone)
contract / IDV (and its subtype — IDIQ, BPA, BOA, FSS) / task or delivery order / modification or
option / first-tier subaward / Other Transaction. These name *different dimensions* — an IDIQ
governs **access** to future orders, contract type (FFP, CPFF, T&M) governs **payment risk**,
single- vs. multiple-award governs **how many holders**. Mislabel the record and every gate below
is wrong.

**Gate 1 — Demand, not capacity.** Obligations on orders and quantity-adding modifications =
committed demand; ceiling / total base and all options value (TBAOV) / unexercised options =
capacity. *Fail →* a ceiling, an empty BPA/BOA, or an unexercised option is not yet revenue —
track for actual orders.

**→ ADDRESSABLE MARKET (waypoint).** What remains is real, in-scope, committed demand — the
denominator, and the first focal stop.

**Gate 2 — Access (the pivot).** Who may legally compete, and therefore by what route? Read
**extent of competition** (`extentCompeted`), **award structure** (single- vs. multiple-award),
and **order route** (FAR 16.505 fair opportunity). *Fail as prime →* a multiple-award IDIQ
(holders only), a single-award IDIQ (sole holder), or a sole-source action redirects to a
non-prime route. *Pass →* an open standalone / full-and-open recompete: you can be prime.

**Gate 3 — Timing.** Is there an upcoming contracting action to target? Distinguish **period of
performance** (current vs. ultimate completion date), the **ordering-period end** (last date to
order) of an IDV, and **option** dates. *Fail →* an approaching completion date that is only an
**option exercise** (FAR 17.207), or a vehicle mid-ordering-period → monitor, don't act. *Edge →*
a sole-source **bridge** (FAR 52.217-8 extension of services / 6.302 urgency) means the
recompete/follow-on is **delayed, not abandoned** — read the acquisition forecast, sources-sought,
and draft solicitation.

**Gate 4 — Ability to perform.** Can the company win and execute? Responsibility determination,
past performance, **facility clearance (FCL)**, **CAS-compliant / DCAA-auditable** accounting where
required, and subcontract flowdowns. *Fail →* not yet qualified: qualify, team, or subcontract.

**Gate 5 — Economics.** Accessible and winnable is not the same as worth it. Contract type and
payment risk (FFP vs. cost-reimbursement vs. T&M), contract financing (progress / performance-based
payments), **data rights** (DFARS 252.227-7013 / -7014), and the compliance and security burden.
*Fail →* economics don't fit: pass, or pursue a different tier.

**→ ROUTE LEAVES (the payoff).** What survives is named by route — the second focal stop:

- **Bid the recompete** — open standalone / full-and-open recompete; compete as prime.
- **On-ramp** — multiple-award IDIQ; seat as a holder when the pool re-competes *(worked on slide 03)*.
- **Subcontract / teaming** — closed prime, single-award, or sole-source; FAR 9.6 CTA or prime-sub.
  A committed MYP/block prime is enterable *below* the prime *(worked on slide 02)*.
- **Consortium / OT** — innovation pathway; enter at the prototype/consortium stage.

## Access posture → route (the core mapping)

The heart of the slide. Each posture answers "prime opportunity now?" and names the route that
survives it.

| Award posture | Prime opportunity now? | Route to revenue |
|---|---|---|
| Open standalone solicitation / full-and-open recompete | Potentially yes | Bid the recompete as prime |
| Multiple-award IDIQ (FAR 16.5), orders on fair opportunity | Holders only | On-ramp at the next pool; or subcontract/team to a current holder |
| Single-award IDIQ | Sole holder receives all orders | Subcontract / supplier to the holder; position for the vehicle recompete |
| Sole-source action (`extentCompeted` = not competed) | Usually no open prime | Supply chain / second-source qualification; shape the next requirement |
| Set-aside (8(a) / SDVOSB / HUBZone / WOSB) | Only if eligible for that procurement | Qualify under the right program, or team compliantly |
| BPA / BOA with no calls or orders | Not yet revenue | Track actual calls/orders before treating as demand |
| OT consortium / prototype | Open via consortium or direct solicitation | Join the consortium; enter at prototype (production follow-on may be sole-source to the prototype winner) |

**Single-award ≠ sole-source:** a fully competed vehicle can have a single winner. Conversely, an
order under a competed multiple-award vehicle can be directed to one holder under an exception to
fair opportunity.

## Annotations — the two reframes

**Closed prime ≠ closed market** *(feeds the Subcontract/teaming leaf; → slide 02).* When the
prime role is committed years ahead — multiyear procurement (FAR 17.1), block/lot buy, or a major
quantity-adding modification — the addressable demand sits *below* the prime: components, castings,
electronics, software, test equipment, engineering changes, depot/sustainment. It releases as
**AP/LLTM** (advance procurement / long-lead-time material) and **EOQ** (economic order quantity)
buys, bought by the prime or an upper-tier supplier, **not** the Government. First-tier subaward
(FFATA) reporting is a *floor, not a census*.

**Enter early or the follow-on is closed** *(feeds the Consortium/OT leaf).* OT and SBIR/STTR are
*entry authorities, not pricing types*. A competitively awarded prototype OT can lead to a
**production follow-on without new competition** (DFARS 206.001-70 exception); an SBIR/STTR
**Phase III** can be awarded sole-source to the firm whose earlier work it derives from. The lesson
is to enter at the topic, prototype, consortium, BAA, or CSO stage — before the production
follow-on closes to later entrants.

## Portal-visibility overlay (a property of the set, not a gate)

Most addressable awards were **never advertised on SAM.gov Contract Opportunities** — award-data
analysis runs on **SAM.gov Contract Awards, USAspending, and FPDS**, not the opportunities portal.
Set this apart beneath the route leaves; do **not** wire it into the cascade.

## Right-rail commentary

A bold finding sentence, then non-bold support, sitting beside the cascade rather than interleaved
with it.

- **Finding:** A large award is not an opportunity — it is a posture to classify. Walk the gates;
  the gate it fails as prime names the route in.
- The upper gates are hard data — object type, obligations vs. ceiling, extent of competition — and
  decide quickly; the lower gates (timing, ability to perform) are judgment.
- The decision is **route, not bid/no-bid**: bid the recompete, on-ramp onto the vehicle,
  subcontract/team to a holder, or join the consortium/OT.
- A **closed prime is not a closed market** — multiyear and block buys release demand below the
  prime (AP/LLTM, EOQ, first-tier subcontracts).
- Most addressable awards were never on **SAM.gov Contract Opportunities** — the work is in Contract
  Awards / USAspending / FPDS.

## Emphasis intent (intent, not mechanism)

Two stops carry the argument and should read as the focal pair: the **ADDRESSABLE MARKET waypoint**
(where capacity, mislabeled records, and out-of-scope demand have been stripped and what remains is
real, reachable, committed demand) and the **ROUTE LEAVES** (the named routes to revenue). The
**Access gate is the pivot** between them and should read as the turn of the argument. Everything
upstream is filtering logic and should recede. State the intent; the build stage chooses how to
express it.

## Quantification discipline

This is a **generalized method slide — no dataset figures on the face.** Keep every stage
conceptual. The market-sizing reconciliation and named vehicles live on the worked-example slides
(02, supply the prime; 03, become a holder) and in the wiki, not here. If an illustrative posture is
shown, mark it as an example and name it in real terms (e.g., "a multiple-award IDIQ on FAR 16.505
fair opportunity") — never a specific program or dollar figure.

## Accuracy guardrails

- An **IDV is not necessarily an IDIQ** (BPA, BOA, FSS are IDVs too) — name the subtype.
- **Single-award ≠ sole-source** — a fully competed vehicle can have one winner.
- A **modification is not a new award** — a quantity-adding mod is committed demand; an
  administrative mod or deobligation is not.
- An **unexercised option is not guaranteed revenue.**
- A **supplier purchase order is not always a public subaward** — FFATA captures qualifying
  first-tier reports only. Reserve **"subaward"** for the reporting context; use
  **subcontractor/supplier** for the commercial relationship.
- An **OT is not universally open or easy.**
- **Never sum** a parent IDV ceiling with child-order obligations, or a shared multiple-award
  ceiling across holders.
- Award data is **evidence and inference, not certainty** — records lag, names change, lower tiers
  are incomplete, and requirements migrate to different vehicles.
