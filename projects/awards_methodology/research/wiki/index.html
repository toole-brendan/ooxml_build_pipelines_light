Structure and terminology of United States defense contracting

ArticleTalkReadEditView history

This article is a structural reference for the vocabulary of United States federal defense contracting — how a single acquisition can be, at the same time, an indefinite-delivery indefinite-quantity (IDIQ) vehicle, a multiple-award contract, a firm-fixed-price task order, an incrementally funded action, and a multiyear procurement, because each label answers a different question. It describes terms as dimensions rather than as competing categories. For the analysis of a specific weapon program's supplier base, see the companion article on outsourced work on U.S. Arleigh Burke-class destroyer construction.

Defense contract structure is the system of legal instruments, ordering mechanics, pricing arrangements, competition methods, time and funding rules, performance relationships, and data-reporting categories through which the United States Department of Defense (DoD) and other federal agencies acquire supplies and services under the Federal Acquisition Regulation (FAR) and its Defense FAR Supplement (DFARS). The subject is widely perceived as a tangle of overlapping acronyms — IDIQ, IDV, MAC, GWAC, BPA, BOA, CLIN, MYP, FFP, CPFF, T&M, JEFO — but those acronyms become tractable once each is assigned to the single dimension of an award that it describes. None of the labels in the sentence "a multiple-award IDIQ, with firm-fixed-price task orders, a five-year ordering period plus options, incremental funding, and a large-business prime contractor using several subcontractors, supporting a weapon program acquired through multiyear procurement" contradicts another. They answer different questions: what legal vehicle, how many holders, how the contractor is paid, how time is authorized, how money is obligated, who performs, and how the federal data systems represent the result.

This article is organized around that dimensional principle. The central analytical claims it develops are that "award amount" is dangerously ambiguous — the estimated value, the vehicle ceiling, the minimum guarantee, the obligation, and the outlay are five different numbers, and federal spending must never be computed by adding a parent vehicle's ceiling to its child orders' values — and that "in scope" is necessary but not sufficient to validate a contract action, which must also be timely, within the ceiling, properly competed or justified, funded from a legally available appropriation, and signed by a warranted contracting officer. A recurring secondary theme is the gap between what is provable from public data (the funding account and awarding office) and what generally requires the underlying procurement file (the exact Program Element or budget line item, and the named source-selection authority).

A version caveat is structural to the subject as of mid-2026. As of June 25, 2026, DoD contracting officers operate under a class deviation effective March 16, 2026 requiring them to use the Revolutionary FAR Overhaul rewrite of FAR Part 16, together with the revised DFARS Part 216 and PGI 216, in place of the corresponding codified text. This matters at the level of citations: older and still-codified materials place many order procedures at FAR 16.505, while the current DoD deviation places fair-opportunity procedures primarily at FAR 16.507. Every section of this article therefore carries, or inherits from this front matter, an "as of" date and identifies whether it describes codified FAR, the DoD deviation, or historical practice. Thresholds (the $15,000 micro-purchase threshold and the $350,000 simplified acquisition threshold, both effective October 1, 2025) and small-business goals are stored as dated facts separate from the enduring conceptual explanations, because they change on their own cycles while the concepts do not.

The dimensions of a federal contract

The single most useful idea for reading United States defense contracting is that the subject's vocabulary is not a list of mutually exclusive contract types but a set of dimensions, each of which answers a different question about the same award. An acquisition does not have to be either an indefinite-delivery indefinite-quantity (IDIQ) contract or a firm-fixed-price contract or a multiple-award contract; it is routinely all three at once, because "what ordering structure," "how is the contractor paid," and "how many companies hold the vehicle" are independent questions. Most confusion in reading contract announcements, federal spending data, and trade-press reporting comes from collapsing two or more of these dimensions into one — for example, treating IDIQ as though it were a pricing type, or treating a vehicle's ceiling as though it were money that has been spent. This chapter sets out the ten dimensions used throughout this reference and then walks a single example through all of them.

Why a dimensional model

Consider the following description of one acquisition:

A DoD multiple-award IDIQ, with firm-fixed-price task orders, a five-year ordering period plus options, incremental funding, and a large-business prime contractor using several subcontractors, supporting a weapon program acquired through multiyear procurement.

Read as a list of competing categories, that sentence appears self-contradictory: how can something be both an IDIQ and firm-fixed-price, both multiple-award and have a single prime performing, both incrementally funded and multiyear? Read as a set of dimensions, there is no contradiction at all. Each emphasized term names a value along a different axis. The IDIQ label describes the ordering structure; firm-fixed-price describes payment and risk; multiple-award describes the number of vehicle holders; the ordering period describes time; incremental funding describes how money is obligated; prime and subcontractor describe the performance chain; and multiyear procurement describes the funding-and-commitment method of the program the work supports. A reader who keeps the axes separate can classify almost any award correctly; a reader who does not will repeatedly mistake one dimension for another.

The following ten questions classify any defense award. Each later chapter of this reference develops one or more of these dimensions in detail.

Two pairings cause most of the trouble and are worth naming at the outset. First, the instrument dimension and the pricing dimension are fully independent: an IDIQ may issue firm-fixed-price orders, cost-reimbursement orders, or both, and the same is true of a standalone contract or a multiyear contract. The compact rule, developed in Pricing types and cost risk, is that vehicle type determines how work is accessed, while pricing type determines how the contractor is paid and who bears cost risk. Second, the award structure dimension (single versus multiple award) and the competition dimension (full and open, set-aside, or sole source) are independent, so that a single-award contract may be the result of vigorous competition and is not the same thing as a sole-source award; that distinction is the subject of Single-award, single-source, and sole-source.

A complete worked example

A single hypothetical connects most of the vocabulary. Suppose the U.S. Army needs engineering support over ten years but cannot predict in advance which specific projects will arise or in what quantity.

Each feature of this structure sits on one of the ten axes:

  • The IDIQ instrument is chosen because the quantity is uncertain and work will be bought through future orders rather than at award. The contract states a binding minimum and a stated maximum, as required for indefinite-quantity contracts under FAR 16.504.115
  • Multiple award is the award-structure value: five separate companies received five parallel prime contracts for the same scope. They do not jointly hold one contract, and none of them owns the entire ceiling.
  • The vehicle's ceiling is the maximum aggregate value of orders the terms authorize; it is a contractual boundary, not money already obligated. This distinction is the whole subject of Awards, ceilings, obligations, and spending.
  • When a specific need arises, the Army runs an order competition among the eligible holders — a fair opportunity to be considered, not a reopening of full and open competition to the whole market. Fair opportunity is governed by FAR Subpart 16.5 (in the current DoD deviation, primarily FAR 16.507) and is developed in Indefinite-delivery contracts.1
  • The winning company receives a task order, which is itself a binding contract within the parent framework even though it is not a separate prime contract with the Government.
  • The order may use several pricing types at different contract line-item numbers (CLINs) — here a firm-fixed-price CLIN alongside a cost-reimbursement CLIN — making the order a hybrid. CLIN structure is covered in CLINs, SLINs, and funding traceability.
  • The order may carry options of its own, separate from any options on the parent IDIQ; an order-level option and a parent-level option are different things, as Options and award terms explains.
  • Subcontractors perform portions of the order, but the prime remains responsible to the Army, which generally has privity of contract only with the prime — the subject of Prime–subcontractor structure, privity, and flowdowns.
  • Actual funding is recorded through obligations on the orders and their modifications, not by reading the parent ceiling. Tracing those obligations is covered in How awards appear in federal data.

That one example exercises the instrument, award-structure, ordering, pricing, competition, time, funding, performance-chain, and data-classification dimensions simultaneously, which is precisely why no single label captures it.

The two analytical warnings that follow from the model

The dimensional model produces two warnings that recur throughout this reference and are stated here so that later chapters can refer back to them.

The first concerns money. Because the instrument and funding dimensions are separate, the phrase "award amount" is ambiguous to the point of being dangerous: the estimated or potential value, the vehicle ceiling, the minimum guarantee, the obligation, and the outlay are five distinct numbers that can differ by orders of magnitude. The most consequential consequence is the prohibition on adding a parent IDV ceiling to the values of its child orders, which double-counts; expenditure analysis must follow the obligation transactions. This is developed in Awards, ceilings, obligations, and spending.

The second concerns validity. Because competition, time, funding, and authority are separate dimensions, being within an instrument's stated scope is necessary but never sufficient to make a contract action lawful. The action must also be timely (within the ordering period), within the ceiling, properly competed or justified, funded from a legally available appropriation, and executed by a warranted contracting officer. The interaction of these axes is the subject of Period of performance and scope and Contracting authority and who makes the award.

The core vocabulary

The terms used to describe United States defense contracting are widely misread because nearly every one of them belongs to a particular dimension — instrument, award structure, ordering, pricing, time, or performance chain — and answers only that dimension's question. The reliable way to misinterpret an award is to take a term from one dimension and treat it as though it answered another, for instance reading IDIQ (an instrument) as if it named a pricing type. This chapter is a glossary: it reproduces the core vocabulary in a single reference table and then expands, in short encyclopedic entries, the pairs that are confused most often. Deep treatment of each concept is deferred to the dedicated chapter, with cross-links provided. The dimensional framework these terms hang from is the subject of The dimensions of a federal contract.

IDV versus IDIQ

An Indefinite Delivery Vehicle (IDV) is principally a federal contract-data umbrella: it is the category, used in award reporting, for parent instruments against which orders or purchases may later be placed. It belongs to the data-classification dimension. An IDIQ — an indefinite-delivery, indefinite-quantity contract — is one specific instrument that contains a binding minimum and a stated maximum, with work purchased through orders; it belongs to the instrument dimension. The misconception is to treat the two as synonyms. They are not: IDIQ is one species within the broader IDV genus. For federal reporting purposes an IDV may include indefinite-delivery contracts, Federal Supply Schedules, blanket purchase agreements, basic ordering agreements, and other agreements that contain ordering provisions, only some of which are IDIQ contracts.3 The full taxonomy is developed in IDV, IDIQ, MAC, GWAC, and Schedules, and the ordering mechanics of indefinite-delivery contracts are covered in Indefinite-delivery contracts.

The two meanings of MAC

The acronym MAC denotes two unrelated concepts that share three letters, and conflating them is a frequent source of error.

A multiple-award contract is an award-structure value: several sources receive parallel prime contracts for the same or similar scope, after which specific work is competed among the holders. The defining facts are that each award is a separate contract and that no holder owns the entire ceiling; the awardees do not jointly hold one instrument. The common misunderstanding is to imagine a single shared contract or to attribute the full vehicle ceiling to each company.

A multi-agency contract is a different thing entirely: a contract established by one agency for use by multiple agencies. It is a single contract whose distinguishing feature is who may order from it, not how many holders it has. The misconception here is simply to confuse it with "multiple-award contract." Both senses, together with government-wide acquisition contracts (GWACs) and Schedules, are distinguished in IDV, IDIQ, MAC, GWAC, and Schedules.

Task order versus delivery order

A task order is an order for services placed under a parent contract; a delivery order is an order for supplies placed under a parent contract. Both belong to the ordering dimension, and each is a binding contract within the parent framework even though neither is a separate prime contract with the Government. Two misconceptions recur. The first treats an order — particularly a task order — as a mere administrative request rather than the binding instrument it is. The second assumes that "task order" and "delivery order" name two different vehicle types; they do not. They are the same kind of thing distinguished only by what is being bought, services or supplies, under the same kind of parent vehicle. The placement, competition, and identification of orders are treated in Indefinite-delivery contracts, and how orders surface in reporting data is covered in How awards appear in federal data.

Option

An option is, by definition, a unilateral Government right to purchase additional supplies or services or to extend the term of the contract during a specified period.4 It belongs to the time dimension and, crucially, it is not itself a contract but a contractual right held by the Government. The dominant misconception is to treat unexercised options as guaranteed or already-awarded work: an option represents potential, not commitment, and its value should not be counted as obligated until the Government actually exercises it. Because the right is the Government's alone, the contractor cannot compel its exercise. Options, including the distinction between parent-level and order-level options, are the subject of Options and award terms.

MYP

MYP — multiyear procurement, or multiyear contracting — is a funding-and-commitment method that covers more than one program year, not a contract type. It is implemented through a contract but is not the contract itself, and it differs from an ordinary contract carrying annual options precisely in its commitment structure: a multiyear arrangement commits the Government across program years rather than re-deciding each year through a unilateral option. The misconception is to treat MYP as merely an ordinary contract with annual options. Multiyear procurement and its application to major weapon production are developed in Multiyear procurement and major weapon production, and its relationship to options specifically is discussed in Options and award terms.

Subcontract versus subaward

A subcontract is a contract under which a prime contractor, or a higher-tier subcontractor, purchases supplies or services needed to perform the prime contract. It is a real contract, but not one with the Government; the relationship runs between private parties. The misconception is to treat the subcontractor as a direct federal contractor — it is not, and the Government generally has no privity of contract with it. This legal structure is the subject of Prime–subcontractor structure, privity, and flowdowns.

A subaward is a different kind of term, drawn from the transparency and federal-assistance vocabulary. For procurement awards it usually refers to a reported first-tier subcontract — that is, the subset of subcontracts that are captured under federal subaward-reporting requirements. The misconception is to assume that every commercial vendor purchase a prime makes is a reportable subaward; many ordinary vendor purchases are not. The reporting regime, including what counts as a first-tier subaward, is covered in Subawards, subcontracts, and first-tier reporting.

BPA versus BOA

A blanket purchase agreement (BPA) is a repetitive-purchase arrangement, but its precise legal effect depends on the authority under which it is established — whether it is a FAR Part 13 (simplified acquisition) BPA, a Schedule BPA, or another authorized BPA. The misconception is to assume that all BPAs operate under the same rules; they do not, and the governing authority changes the analysis. Importantly, a BPA is often not, by itself, a contract.

A basic ordering agreement (BOA) is not a contract at all. It is an instrument that establishes terms and pricing methods for future orders or contracts; the actual contracts come into being only when orders are placed against it. The misconception is to read a BOA's stated ceiling as an awarded contract amount — it is neither awarded money nor an obligation. Both arrangements appear as IDVs in federal reporting and are addressed alongside the other "not a contract type" traps in Common "not a contract type" traps, with the dollar-figure pitfalls covered in Awards, ceilings, obligations, and spending.

How orders are identified in the data

Several of the terms above interact with how an award is identified in federal contract data, and a single rule prevents much confusion. For reporting, an IDV may include indefinite-delivery contracts, Schedules, BPAs, BOAs, and other agreements with ordering provisions. An order placed against one of these parents generally carries its own Procurement Instrument Identifier (PIID) while also identifying the parent instrument, so that the order is both a thing in its own right and a child of a named umbrella.3 The same vocabulary that distinguishes parent from child in prose — IDV versus order, definitive contract versus task or delivery order — is what the data systems use to keep awards, ceilings, and obligations from being double-counted. The reporting structure is developed in How awards appear in federal data.

The throughline

Every term in this glossary belongs to a dimension, as set out in The dimensions of a federal contract. The fastest way to misread an award is to treat a term from one dimension — for example IDIQ, which names an instrument — as if it answered another dimension's question, such as how the contractor is paid. That is why this reference repeatedly separates the instrument question from the pricing question, developed in Pricing types and cost risk; the ceiling question from the obligation question, developed in Awards, ceilings, obligations, and spending; and the award-action question from the modification question, developed in Contract modifications. A term that is correctly assigned to its dimension is rarely misread; a term that is borrowed across dimensions almost always is.

Awards, ceilings, obligations, and spending

The single most consequential source of error in reading United States defense contract data is treating the phrase "award amount" as though it named one number. It does not. A defensible reference must distinguish at least seven money concepts — an estimated or potential value, a vehicle ceiling, a minimum guarantee, an obligation, an outlay, the current-versus-potential value reported in a database field, and the shared-versus-individual ceiling of a multiple-award vehicle — each of which can differ from the others by orders of magnitude. These amounts all sit on the funding dimension introduced in The dimensions of a federal contract: they describe when and how much money is committed or paid, a question that is entirely independent of the instrument dimension (whether the award is a standalone contract, an indefinite-delivery vehicle, or an order). Reading a press-release headline dollar figure without knowing which of these amounts it represents is the most common single mistake in interpreting defense contract announcements, federal spending data, and trade-press reporting. This chapter defines each amount, assembles them into a comparison table, and closes with the one rule that follows from all of them.

Why "award amount" is ambiguous

A contract announcement that reports a dollar figure is reporting a value somewhere along a spectrum that runs from "the most that could ever be spent if everything goes a certain way" to "money that has actually left the Treasury." The two ends of that spectrum are not close together, and many widely cited figures sit at the speculative end. A headline that a company "won a $20 billion contract" may describe a ceiling that will never be reached, an estimate of potential value that assumes every option and every contemplated quantity is exercised, or — far less often — money that has genuinely been committed. The figure means nothing analytically until it is assigned to one of the concepts below.

The ambiguity is not a defect of any one announcement; it is structural, because the same acquisition legitimately carries several different true numbers at once. A multiple-award indefinite-delivery indefinite-quantity (IDIQ) vehicle can simultaneously have a very large ceiling, a very small guaranteed minimum, a modest amount obligated to date, and a still smaller amount actually paid out. None of those numbers is wrong; they answer different questions. The danger compounds with the IDIQ and multiple-award contract (MAC) vehicles described in IDV, IDIQ, MAC, GWAC, and Schedules, where the ceiling can be enormous relative to the guaranteed minimum, and again with the modifications described in Contract modifications, where a single modification action can carry most of a program's value.

The distinct money concepts

Estimated or potential value

The estimated or potential value is the possible value of an award if all contemplated work, quantities, and options are used. It is a planning and capacity figure — the size of the box, not its contents. Critically, it does not mean that the Government has committed that amount, promised it to the awardee, or in any sense spent it. On a multiple-award vehicle the announced "value" is frequently this number, and frequently it is the aggregate potential across all holders rather than anything any one company will receive.

Vehicle ceiling or maximum quantity

The vehicle ceiling (also the maximum, or maximum quantity when stated in units) is the upper contractual boundary on the value or quantity of orders that may be placed under an IDIQ. It is a limit, not a forecast and not a commitment: a $20 billion ceiling does not mean that $20 billion was obligated, that $20 billion was spent, or that $20 billion was promised to each holder. The ceiling is the figure most often misread as "the size of the contract." Under the current DoD deviation, indefinite-quantity contracts must state a maximum, expressed as the most the Government may order over the life of the vehicle.5

Minimum guarantee

The minimum guarantee is the minimum dollar amount or quantity the Government agrees to order over the life of an IDIQ. This minimum — not the ceiling — is the basic Government purchase commitment, and it is the only amount the Government is actually obligated to buy. The revised FAR 16.504 applied by DoD under the class deviation requires an indefinite-quantity contract to state a minimum greater than a nominal quantity, together with a stated maximum; the minimum must be more than a token amount so that the contract is supported by consideration.5 In practice the guaranteed minimum is often a tiny fraction of the ceiling, which is precisely why the two must never be confused: the large number is a boundary, while the small number is the promise.

Obligation

An obligation is a legal commitment of federal funds — the action by which the Government binds an appropriation to pay for goods or services. Obligations are the correct basis for almost all spending analysis. They may be recorded at award, or, more often on indefinite-delivery vehicles, later: through the placement of task or delivery orders, through modifications, through incremental-funding actions, and through the exercise of options, each of which can add to the obligated total over time. Because obligations accrue through these downstream actions, the amount obligated on a vehicle at any moment generally bears no fixed relationship to its ceiling. Options and their exercise mechanics are treated in Options and award terms, and the line-item structure that ties an obligation to a specific appropriation is treated in CLINs, SLINs, and funding traceability.

Outlay

An outlay is an actual payment — money disbursed from the Treasury to the contractor. Outlays generally trail obligations, often by months or years, because contractors invoice only after performing services or delivering supplies, and payment follows acceptance. The gap between obligations and outlays is normal and expected; it is not evidence that funds are missing. For most purposes the obligation is the better measure of Government commitment, while the outlay is the better measure of cash actually spent in a period.

Current versus potential contract value

Many databases expose both a current value and a potential value for the same award, and the two are routinely separated by unexercised options. The current value is the reported value of the presently awarded work — the base plus any options already exercised. It is a contract-value field, not a spending measure: it can differ from the amount actually obligated, and it must never be read as the money committed or paid. The potential value adds the unexercised options and other contingencies and is therefore a ceiling-like figure. Federal contract data in fact carries three value concepts that are easily conflated — the action and total obligations (the only dependable measure of funds committed), the base-and-exercised-options value (the current value), and the base-and-all-options value (the potential value) — of which only the obligations represent a commitment.6 The exact meaning of any field depends on the specific field consulted, so the field definition, not the label alone, controls the interpretation. How these values are represented as records in the systems of record is the subject of How awards appear in federal data.

Shared versus individual ceiling

For a multiple-award contract, the word "ceiling" is itself ambiguous because the ceiling may be structured in more than one way. A MAC may have a single aggregate ceiling shared by all holders; a separate ceiling for each holder; pools of holders, each pool with its own separate ceiling; or some combination of these. Whether a given dollar figure is a shared programwide ceiling or an individual holder's ceiling cannot be inferred from the announcement; the solicitation and the signed contracts control. This distinction matters whenever a per-company figure is compared against a programwide one, and it interacts with the single-award-versus-multiple-award distinction developed in Indefinite-delivery contracts (FAR 16.5).

The essential data rule

All of the foregoing reduces to one operational rule, the single most important rule for working with federal contract data:

Never calculate federal spending by adding a parent indefinite-delivery vehicle (IDV) ceiling to the values of its child orders. For expenditure analysis, follow the obligation transactions, and understand the parent–child relationship between the vehicle and its orders.

The rule exists because the two quantities live on different levels of the same hierarchy and overlap. A parent IDV is a framework; its ceiling is the maximum that orders placed under it may total. The child orders are the actual instruments through which money is obligated within that framework. Adding the parent ceiling to the child-order values therefore double-counts — it sums a boundary with the very transactions the boundary was meant to bound — and inflates apparent spending, sometimes massively. The correct unit of expenditure analysis is the obligation recorded on the orders and their modifications, not any ceiling, estimate, or potential value. This is also why the parent–child data model and the avoidance of double-counting are central to How awards appear in federal data: a vehicle and its orders are distinct records, and only one of them carries the money. USAspending exposes obligation and outlay data at the transaction, award, and federal-account levels precisely so that spending can be traced through these obligation transactions rather than estimated from ceilings.7

The same caution applies to modifications. Because an obligation can be recorded on a modification, and because one modification action can carry most of a program's value, expenditure analysis that ignores modification transactions will understate spending just as surely as summing ceilings overstates it. The reliable path is always the same: identify the obligation transactions, attribute each to its appropriation and its place in the parent–child structure, and sum those — never the boundaries, the estimates, or the potential values that announcements tend to headline.8

How awards appear in federal data

The last of the ten dimensions introduced in The dimensions of a federal contract is data classification: how the federal systems of record represent a given acquisition as one or more discrete records. The same award that the preceding chapters classify by instrument, pricing, and funding is, inside the data systems, a typed record with an identifier, a place in a parent–child hierarchy, and a set of value and obligation fields. The principal values of this dimension are definitive contract, indefinite-delivery vehicle (IDV), parent award, order, transaction, and subaward. Confusing these record types — most damagingly, adding a parent vehicle's ceiling to the values of the orders placed beneath it — is the most common source of error in interpreting federal spending data. This chapter describes how SAM.gov, USAspending.gov, and the Federal Procurement Data System (FPDS) represent an award; it defers the definitions of the underlying money concepts to Awards, ceilings, obligations, and spending and defers the detailed budget-line and accounting-classification tracing to CLINs, SLINs, and funding traceability, keeping the money-tracing workflow here only at the level of which records carry which numbers.

The systems of record

Three public systems hold most of what is provable about a federal contract action. The Federal Procurement Data System (FPDS) is the contract-action system of record: contracting officers report each award and each subsequent action there, and its data model — typed records, instrument identifiers, and parent–child links — is the model the other systems inherit. USAspending.gov is the public spending portal that draws procurement records from FPDS and adds federal-account, transaction, and subaward layers, exposing the same data through a documented application programming interface (API).9 SAM.gov, the System for Award Management, is the registration and reporting hub; among other functions it now hosts first-tier subaward and subcontract reporting, which moved there when the legacy FFATA Subaward Reporting System (FSRS) was retired on March 8, 2025.10 These systems share a common vocabulary of record types, so that a record described as an IDV in one is the same kind of object in the others.

The public-data reporting delay

Public contract-action data is not real-time. Contracting officers report actions to FPDS on a lag, and for the Department of Defense the public release of contract-action data is further delayed — DoD-funded and DoD-awarded actions are generally withheld from the public systems for roughly 90 days after the date the action is signed. The consequence for any analysis built on public data is that recency is lagged: the absence of a recent action does not establish that none occurred, and a monitoring process must treat the latest public picture of DoD work as on the order of a quarter stale.11 This currency limit is one of the reasons the inference of future acquisition events from award history, developed in Recompetes and opportunity intelligence from awards data, yields probabilities and lead times rather than certainties.

The record types

Definitive contract

A definitive contract is a standalone award that is complete in itself: it states the work, the price or pricing arrangement, and the period of performance at the time of award, and it does not exist to host later orders. In the data it appears as a single award record carrying its own identifier and its own obligation history. The full vocabulary distinction between a definitive contract and an ordering vehicle is drawn in The core vocabulary; for data purposes the salient point is that a definitive contract is a leaf, not a framework — it has obligations of its own but no child orders placed against it.

Indefinite-delivery vehicle (IDV)

An indefinite-delivery vehicle (IDV) is the data category for an ordering framework against which later orders are placed. For federal reporting the IDV category is broad: it covers indefinite-delivery contracts, Federal Supply Schedules, blanket purchase agreements (BPAs), basic ordering agreements (BOAs), and other agreements that carry ordering provisions, as contemplated by the contract-reporting rules of FAR Subpart 4.6.12 An IDV record typically reports a ceiling and other framework-level values, but the money the Government actually commits is generally recorded not on the IDV itself but on the orders beneath it. The distinction between the IDV data category and the various instruments it can contain — and the differences among IDIQ, multiple-award, multi-agency, GWAC, and Schedule vehicles — is the subject of IDV, IDIQ, MAC, GWAC, and Schedules.

Parent award and order

These two record types are defined by their relationship to each other. A parent award is a record that other records are placed against; in practice a parent is either an IDV or, occasionally, a standalone contract that hosts orders. An order — a task order for services or a delivery order for supplies — is a child record placed against a parent. Crucially, an order generally carries its own Procurement Instrument Identifier (PIID) while also identifying the PIID of the parent instrument, so that the data preserves the link between the order and the framework it was issued under.12 The result is a two-level hierarchy: the parent describes the framework and its boundaries, while each child order describes a specific buy and carries the obligations for that buy. The contractual nature of an order — that a task or delivery order is itself a binding contract within the parent framework — is developed in Indefinite-delivery contracts (FAR 16.5).

Transaction

A transaction is a single reported contract action. The original award is one transaction; each later modification is another, separate transaction against the same award. Every transaction carries its own federal action obligation — the change in obligated funds effected by that action — which may be positive, zero, or negative. This is the level at which spending is actually recorded: an award's obligated total is the sum of its transactions, not a single figure read off the award header. Because a modification is its own transaction with its own obligation, expenditure analysis that ignores modification transactions will understate spending; the nature and reach of modifications, including why a single modification can carry most of a program's value, are treated in Contract modifications.

Subaward

A subaward in the transparency data is a reported lower-tier award beneath a federal prime award. In the procurement context a subaward usually denotes a reported first-tier subcontract — a subcontract one level below the prime — and not a federal prime award in its own right. Subaward records are reported by the prime recipient rather than by a contracting officer, and since the retirement of FSRS that reporting is collected through SAM.gov.10 Because a subaward is a different kind of object from a prime transaction, subaward values must not be commingled with prime obligations when totaling federal spending. The two distinct meanings of "subaward," what the first-tier reporting captures and omits, and the move of that reporting to SAM.gov are the subject of Subawards, subcontracts, and first-tier reporting.

Identifiers and the parent–child relationship

The element that ties the record types together is the Procurement Instrument Identifier (PIID), the identifier each contract instrument carries under the contract-reporting rules of FAR Subpart 4.6.12 A standalone contract or an IDV has a PIID; an order placed against an IDV has its own PIID and additionally references the parent's PIID. Reading the two identifiers together is what lets an analyst place any order in its framework and distinguish a parent from its children.

The hierarchy has a strict reading rule. The ceiling reported on the parent IDV is a boundary on what the orders beneath it may total; the obligations that represent actual spending live on the transactions of the child orders. The parent value and the child values therefore occupy different levels of one structure and overlap, which is why they cannot be added together.

Period-of-performance fields and ordering periods

Award records also carry dates, and the date fields differ by record type in a way that parallels the parent–child distinction. An ordinary contract or order reports a current completion date — the end of the presently exercised performance — and an ultimate completion date — the end if all predetermined options are exercised. A procurement IDV, however, does not carry period-of-performance end dates in that sense: what bounds it is an ordering-period end date, or last date to order, and the performance of individual child orders may continue past that date. Reading an IDV's ordering deadline as if it were a performance end date, or expecting one expiration date to govern a vehicle and all of its orders at once, misreads the data model.13 How these several clocks are read as signals of future acquisition activity is developed in Recompetes and opportunity intelligence from awards data; the legal question of what may lawfully happen after an ordering period closes is treated in Indefinite-delivery contracts (FAR 16.5).

Current versus potential value fields

A single award record commonly exposes more than one dollar field, and the two most often confused are current value and potential value. As a matter of database representation, the current value reports the presently awarded portion of the award — the base plus any options already exercised — while the potential value adds unexercised options and other contingencies; a separate field set records the action and total obligations. The distinction is decisive: the current value is a contract-value field, not a commitment of funds, and it can differ from the amount obligated; only the obligation fields measure money the Government has actually committed.14 The current and potential fields sit side by side because an award legitimately carries both an awarded value to date and a not-yet-exercised ceiling at once, but neither is a spending measure. The exact meaning of either field depends on the specific field consulted, so the field definition, not the label alone, controls the interpretation. These fields are the data-system representation of the money concepts — estimated value, ceiling, minimum guarantee, obligation, and outlay — defined in Awards, ceilings, obligations, and spending; this chapter describes the fields, while that chapter defines what the underlying amounts mean.

The rule against double-counting

The single most important rule for working with this data follows directly from the parent–child model:

Never calculate federal spending by adding a parent IDV ceiling to the values of its child orders. For expenditure analysis, follow the obligation transactions and respect the parent–child relationship between the vehicle and its orders.

The rule exists because the parent ceiling and the child-order obligations sit on different levels of the same hierarchy. The ceiling is the maximum that orders placed under the IDV may total; the orders are the instruments through which money is actually obligated within that boundary. Summing the two adds a boundary to the very transactions the boundary was meant to bound, double-counting and inflating apparent spending — sometimes by orders of magnitude. The correct unit of expenditure analysis is the obligation recorded on the transactions of the orders (and on any modifications), never a ceiling, an estimate, or a potential value. The derivation of this rule from the underlying money concepts is given in Awards, ceilings, obligations, and spending; the present chapter states it as a property of the record model.

Action labels do not measure economic size

A related caution concerns transaction labels. The label on a transaction describes that action's relationship to an existing contract, not its economic magnitude. An action recorded as a modification can economically represent most of a major acquisition program — for instance, a multibillion-dollar production modification that exercises a large quantity under an existing contract. Conversely, an original award may be small. Reading the action type as a proxy for dollar size is therefore unreliable; the federal action obligation on the transaction, not its label, is the measure of size. Why a modification need not add scope or even money, and why it can nonetheless be enormous, are developed in Contract modifications.

A light public-data reading workflow

The full funding-traceability procedure — tracing a dollar down to its budget line item, Program Element, and accounting classification — is a separate subject, covered in CLINs, SLINs, and funding traceability. At the level of which records carry which numbers, however, a public-data reading of an award proceeds in a few steps:

  1. Identify the instruments. Record the prime contract PIID and, for an order, both the order's own PIID and the parent IDV's PIID, so the record's place in the parent–child hierarchy is fixed.
  2. Separate award from modifications. Treat the original award and each modification as the distinct transactions they are, rather than as a single figure.
  3. Record the obligation per transaction. Capture the federal action obligation for each transaction; their sum, not any header value, is the amount obligated.
  4. Retrieve the funding context. Identify the funding agency, the funding office, and the federal account associated with the obligations.

The USAspending API supports this reading directly: its award-funding and award-account endpoints return the federal accounts, the funding and awarding organizations, and the transaction-level obligations for an award, so that spending can be traced through the obligation transactions rather than estimated from a ceiling.15

Awarding agency versus funding agency

A final field distinction matters whenever the question is who paid. The awarding agency is the agency whose contracting office conducted the procurement and signed the award; the funding agency is the agency whose appropriation provided the money. These are separate fields because they are frequently different agencies: in interagency arrangements one agency may fund a requirement while another agency's contracting shop awards and administers the contract on its behalf. Treating "awarding agency" as a proxy for "who funded this" will misattribute spending in exactly those interagency cases, which is why the award-funding data exposes the funding organization separately from the awarding organization.15

Recompetes and opportunity intelligence from awards data

The preceding chapter describes how a federal award appears as a typed record; this chapter describes what those records, read together and read forward, can and cannot reveal about a future acquisition event. The subject sits on the data-classification, competition, and time dimensions at once, but as an inference layer over them rather than as another record type. Its governing distinction, and the most common error it guards against, is twofold: a recompete is not a data field — no system reports one — and the question "will a requirement be bought again?" must be kept separate from the question "could a particular firm win the resulting action?" Collapsing those two questions into a single "opportunity score" produces confident nonsense. This chapter is the analytical counterpart to the record model in How awards appear in federal data and to the pre-award lifecycle in The acquisition lifecycle, source selection, and contracting authority: it reads the historical record against forward demand signals to estimate where, when, and how an existing requirement may next be acquired.

A recompete is an inferred event, not a data field

No federal system publishes a field that says "this requirement will be recompeted on this date." A recompete — or, more generally, a follow-on acquisition event — is inferred by assembling an award's history and joining it to forward-looking signals of continued demand. The raw material comes from two distinct public data planes: the contract-award record, exposed through the SAM.gov Contract Awards data and the Federal Procurement Data System,16 and the opportunity-notice record, exposed through the SAM.gov contract-opportunities data.17 Neither plane labels an event as a recompete; the inference is the analyst's.

The cardinal rule of the analysis is to estimate two separate quantities and never to merge them:

  • Event probability — the likelihood that the requirement is bought again at all, on some timeline.
  • Addressability — the likelihood that a given firm can reach and win the resulting action.

A near-certain recompete on a vehicle a firm cannot access is not an opportunity for that firm; a perfectly addressable requirement that will never be rebought is not an opportunity at all. Holding the two apart is what keeps the analysis honest, and the scoring section below treats them as two axes rather than one number.

Award families, not flat rows

A single transaction row says little; the signal lives in the award family — the set of records that together describe one requirement over time. Assembling a family means grouping records by the underlying requirement rather than by identifier alone, and retaining the fields that carry timing, recurrence, and competitive posture:

  • PIID and referenced IDV PIID — to separate an order from its parent vehicle.
  • Modification number and modification reason — to distinguish option exercises, funding actions, extensions, and closeout.
  • Recipient UEI and CAGE, and ultimate parent — to normalize subsidiaries, mergers, and name changes.
  • Awarding office and funding office — to identify the buyer separately from the money's owner.
  • PSC, NAICS, description, and place of performance — to reconstruct the requirement.
  • Current completion date, ultimate completion date, and last date to order — the several clocks.

  • Base-and-exercised and base-and-all-options values — to gauge contractual scale (not spending).

  • Solicitation identifier — to join the award to past or future notices.

Multiple clocks, not one expiration

A requirement does not have a single expiration date. It has several clocks, and useful monitoring works in date bands rather than from one date:

  • Current completion date — the end of presently exercised performance, and the earliest meaningful continuation decision.
  • Next option date — when the Government must decide whether to continue.
  • Ultimate completion date — the end if every predetermined option is exercised.
  • Parent ordering-period end / last date to order — the deadline for placing new orders under a vehicle, after which child orders may still run.
  • Expected acquisition lead time — how long the buying organization needs to run the next acquisition.

As a capture heuristic — not a legal deadline — monitoring of complex, cleared, or integration-heavy requirements tends to begin roughly 18 to 30 months before ultimate completion, and of smaller or simpler buys roughly 9 to 18 months before. The throughline

Opportunity intelligence is an exercise in disciplined inference, and its discipline is a set of separations. Score two things, not one: whether a buy will happen, and whether a given firm can reach it. Build families, not rows; classify transactions, not just count them; reason in date bands, not from a single expiration; and match requirements by their substance, not by their identifiers. Above all, remember what the data is: public award records are delayed and incomplete, and notices show only their latest state, so the honest output of the method is a set of probabilities and lead times, never a certainty. Read that way — against the record model of the previous chapter and the lifecycle of the next — awards data becomes a forward-looking instrument without ceasing to be the backward-looking record it actually is.

Indefinite-delivery contracts (FAR 16.5)

FAR Subpart 16.5 is not a single contract type. It is the regulatory framework for indefinite-delivery contracts and the orders placed under them, and it answers two of the dimensions developed in The dimensions of a federal contract: the instrument dimension (what legal vehicle is used when the quantity or timing of a requirement is not known at award) and the ordering dimension (whether work is bought at the parent level or through later task or delivery orders). The most common error in reading this part of the vocabulary is to treat "indefinite-delivery" as the name of a contract — it is instead an umbrella over three distinct forms, a body of rules for how holders compete for orders, and a vocabulary of overlapping time periods. This chapter sets out the three forms, the order types, the single- versus multiple-award distinction, the fair-opportunity process and its dollar thresholds, the exceptions to fair opportunity, the several ordering clocks, and the restricted regime for protesting orders.

The three indefinite-delivery forms

FAR Subpart 16.5 recognizes three indefinite-delivery forms, distinguished by how much is fixed at award and how much is deferred to later orders.

  • A definite-quantity contract fixes the total quantity at award, but the timing or location of delivery may be determined through later orders. For example, the Government commits to buy exactly 1,000 generators, with delivery locations designated as needs arise. What is "indefinite" is the delivery schedule, not the amount.
  • A requirements contract is the Government's agreement that designated activities will purchase all of their actual covered requirements from one contractor during the ordering period. The precise quantity is unknown, and the stated estimate is not a guaranteed quantity. For example, one installation agrees to purchase all of its covered copier-maintenance requirements from one contractor for three years; if its actual need turns out to be larger or smaller than the estimate, the contractor must meet the actual need and is not owed the estimate.
  • An indefinite-quantity contract, universally abbreviated IDIQ, is used when the Government knows the general category of supplies or services and commits to a stated minimum but does not know the exact quantity or timing. The contractor must perform orders up to a stated maximum. For example, between $10,000 and $500 million of engineering services over five years. FAR 16.504 requires that the contract state a minimum greater than a nominal quantity and a stated maximum, so that the minimum represents a real purchase commitment and the maximum represents the contractual ceiling.116 The indefinite-quantity clause that establishes this minimum-and-maximum obligation is FAR 52.216-22.117

The IDIQ is by far the most common of the three in defense contracting, and the rest of this chapter is written principally about it; the requirements and definite-quantity forms borrow the same ordering vocabulary. The relationship of the IDIQ to the broader family of indefinite-delivery vehicles — and to the federal data category that holds them — is treated separately in IDV, IDIQ, MAC, GWAC, and Schedules.

Task orders versus delivery orders

Within the indefinite-delivery framework, work is acquired through orders, and the order type follows what the order buys. A task-order contract acquires services through orders called task orders; a delivery-order contract acquires supplies through orders called delivery orders. A single order is itself a binding contract within the parent framework, even though it is not a separate prime contract with the Government. Mixed vehicles can contain both supplies and services, but the terminology usually follows what an individual order acquires, so the same parent vehicle may issue task orders for the service portions of a requirement and delivery orders for the supply portions. The distinction is one of nomenclature and the kind of work being bought; it is independent of the pricing type carried on the order's contract line items, which is the subject of Pricing types and cost risk.

Single-award versus multiple-award IDIQ

The terms single-award and multiple-award describe how many primes hold the vehicle, not how the holders are paid or whether they competed. A single-award IDIQ gives one prime the vehicle, and later orders are placed with that one contractor. A multiple-award IDIQ gives several primes parallel contracts for the same scope; those holders ordinarily compete against one another for later orders. The holders of a multiple-award vehicle do not jointly hold one contract, and none of them owns the entire ceiling — each holds a separate prime contract under the common solicitation.

The agency-specific terms MAC IDIQ, MATOC (multiple-award task-order contract), MADOC (multiple-award delivery-order contract), and MACC (multiple-award construction contract) are variations on this same multiple-award architecture, not new pricing types or new instruments. They name the combination of "multiple award" with a particular order type or mission area. Because the single-award versus multiple-award choice is an award-structure question and not a competition question, a single-award vehicle is not the same thing as a sole-source award and may itself be the product of vigorous competition — a distinction developed in Single-award, single-source, and sole-source.

Fair opportunity and its thresholds

On a multiple-award vehicle, eligible holders ordinarily receive a fair opportunity to be considered for orders above the micro-purchase threshold. Fair opportunity is not the same as reopening full and open competition to the entire marketplace: the field is limited to the existing holders of the vehicle. The ordering contracting officer has broad discretion to streamline the process, and the order competition is generally not governed by the FAR Part 6 competition procedures, FAR Part 14 sealed-bidding procedures, or FAR Subpart 15.3 source-selection procedures that apply to awarding the parent vehicles themselves. Price or cost must still be considered in selecting the order awardee.

The intensity of the fair-opportunity process scales with the value of the order. As of the current thresholds, effective October 1, 2025, the micro-purchase threshold is $15,000 and the simplified acquisition threshold is $350,000.21 The tiers operate as follows:

Order value Fair-opportunity treatment
At or below the micro-purchase threshold ($15,000) Fair opportunity does not apply; the order may be placed directly.
Above the micro-purchase threshold through the simplified acquisition threshold ($350,000) The contracting officer may fairly consider holders using available information and need not necessarily request a new response from every holder.
Above the simplified acquisition threshold through $7.5 million Fair notice of the intent to make a purchase is ordinarily given to all holders capable of performing the work.
Above $7.5 million The notice, evaluation factors, documentation, and post-award process become more formal, including a more developed evaluation and a debriefing on request.

These dollar figures change on their own cycle and should be read as dated facts rather than as permanent features of the framework; the conceptual structure of escalating formality is what endures.

Exceptions to fair opportunity

The duty to provide fair opportunity is subject to a closed set of exceptions. A holder need not be given fair opportunity when:

  • the agency's need for the supplies or services is so urgent that providing fair opportunity would result in unacceptable delay;
  • only one awardee is capable of providing supplies or services that are unique or highly specialized;
  • the order must be issued on a sole-source basis as a logical follow-on to an order already issued under the contract that was placed competitively;
  • it is necessary to place an order to satisfy a minimum guarantee owed to a holder; or
  • a statute expressly requires or authorizes that the purchase be made from a specified source.

These determinations are documented through a justification for an exception to fair opportunity (JEFO). A JEFO is related to, but not identical with, the FAR Part 6 justification and approval (J&A) for other than full and open competition: the J&A justifies departing from full and open competition for an award, whereas the JEFO justifies departing from fair opportunity among existing holders for an order. Conflating the two documents is a common reading error; the broader vocabulary of single-source and sole-source justification is developed in Single-award, single-source, and sole-source.

Ordering period versus order period of performance

Indefinite-delivery contracts carry more than one clock, and the most common time-related error is to treat the life of the parent vehicle and the life of a particular order as the same thing. Four concepts must be kept distinct:

  • The ordering period is the window during which the Government may issue new orders under the parent vehicle.
  • The order period of performance is the window during which a particular order's work may be performed.
  • A parent option extends the period during which new orders can be issued — it lengthens the ordering period.
  • An order option extends or adds work under a particular child order — it lengthens or expands that one order's period of performance.

An order's performance may continue beyond the end of the parent ordering period when the parent terms so authorize; that does not ordinarily permit the Government to issue entirely new orders after the ordering period has ended. The practical consequences of this separation — including the question of what may and may not happen once an ordering period closes — are developed at length in Period of performance and scope, and the distinction between parent-level and order-level options is developed in Options and award terms.

Order protests

Protests against the placement of orders under indefinite-delivery contracts are restricted under the current revised FAR Part 16. An order protest generally may be brought only on the ground that an order increases the scope, period, or maximum value of the parent contract, or — for orders placed by the Department of Defense, the National Aeronautics and Space Administration, or the Coast Guard — on any ground when the order is valued above $35 million.22 Qualifying value-based order protests are heard by the Government Accountability Office (GAO). The general effect is that routine, in-scope orders below the agency-specific value threshold are largely insulated from protest, while the disputes that remain reviewable are those alleging that an order has burst the contractual boundaries of its parent or has crossed the high-value line.

IDV, IDIQ, MAC, GWAC, and Schedules

This chapter disambiguates a cluster of acronyms that are routinely collapsed into one another even though they live on different dimensions of an award. The unifying point is that IDV is a data classification, while IDIQ, GWAC, Schedule, BPA, and BOA name instruments or arrangements with different governing rules. The single most common error in reading federal contract data is to fuse the data label and the instrument into a single concept — to read "IDV" as though it were a contract type, or to assume that every umbrella vehicle is an IDIQ. The ordering mechanics that operate inside these vehicles — task versus delivery orders, fair opportunity and its exceptions, and the three indefinite-delivery forms (definite-quantity, requirements, and indefinite-quantity) — belong to Indefinite-delivery contracts and are not repeated here; this chapter is about telling the vehicles and the data categories apart.

IDV versus IDIQ

An Indefinite Delivery Vehicle (IDV) is the broad reporting and data category. It is the umbrella, used in federal contract reporting under FAR Subpart 4.6, for parent instruments against which orders or purchases may later be placed. An indefinite-delivery indefinite-quantity (IDIQ) contract is something narrower: one particular legally binding indefinite-quantity contract, containing a binding minimum and a stated maximum, with work bought through orders rather than at award. The relationship between the two is one of genus and species. Every reported IDIQ is an IDV, but not every IDV is an IDIQ.24

The reason the broader category is not exhausted by IDIQ is that an IDV may also include Federal Supply Schedules, blanket purchase agreements (BPAs), basic ordering agreements (BOAs), and other agreements that contain ordering provisions — only some of which are indefinite-quantity contracts. Treating "IDV" and "IDIQ" as synonyms therefore makes two distinct mistakes at once: it mistakes a data classification for an instrument, and it silently excludes the Schedules, BPAs, and BOAs that the IDV category is specifically built to encompass. The same parent-versus-child logic that the data systems use to keep an IDV's ceiling from being added to its orders' values is developed in How awards appear in federal data, and the underlying glossary entries are in The core vocabulary.

Multiple-award contract versus multi-agency contract

The acronym MAC carries two official and unrelated meanings, and conflating them is a frequent source of error because the two senses answer entirely different questions.

A multiple-award contract answers a question on the award-structure dimension: it is an arrangement in which more than one source receives an award for the same or similar scope. The defining facts are that each award is a separate prime contract and that no single holder owns the entire ceiling. After the parallel awards are made, specific requirements are competed among the holders through the fair-opportunity process described in Indefinite-delivery contracts. The characteristic misunderstanding is to imagine the awardees jointly holding one instrument, or to attribute the full vehicle ceiling to each company; both errors are addressed, on the money side, in Awards, ceilings, obligations, and spending.

A multi-agency contract answers a different question — who may order — and refers to a task- or delivery-order contract established by one agency that is usable by other agencies. Here the distinguishing feature is the breadth of authorized customers, not the number of holders. A single agency sets up the vehicle, and other agencies place orders against it under the vehicle's terms. The two senses are independent rather than mutually exclusive, so a single vehicle can be both multiple-award and multi-agency: established by one agency for governmentwide or interagency use, and held by several competing sources at once. Because the two meanings share three letters but sit on different dimensions, an analyst who does not specify which "MAC" is meant invites exactly the kind of cross-dimensional confusion this reference is built to prevent.

GWAC

A Governmentwide Acquisition Contract (GWAC) is a task- or delivery-order contract for information technology, established by one agency for governmentwide use under designated authority. Its defining attributes are therefore its subject matter (information technology) and its access (governmentwide), conferred by a specific delegation of authority to the establishing agency.

In practice, GWACs are commonly structured as multiple-award IDIQs, which is precisely why the term is easy to misfile. The label "GWAC" does not name a new pricing arrangement or a new ordering mechanic; the ordering still proceeds through task and delivery orders under the rules covered in Indefinite-delivery contracts, and the pricing of individual orders still belongs to the separate pricing dimension developed in Pricing types and cost risk. "GWAC" describes the vehicle's IT purpose and its governmentwide accessibility, layered on top of an instrument that is usually an IDIQ. The correct reading is additive: a GWAC is typically an IDIQ that has also been designated for IT and opened to the whole government, not a fourth species of contract that displaces the IDIQ category.

GSA Multiple Award Schedule

The GSA Multiple Award Schedule (MAS) — the GSA Schedule — consists of many individual schedule contracts for commercial products and services held by a large population of vendors. Agencies place orders against those schedule contracts under FAR Subpart 8.4 and the associated GSA rules, rather than under the ordinary indefinite-delivery procedures of FAR Subpart 16.5.

The Schedule should not be treated as merely another ordinary FAR 16.5 multiple-award contract, even though it shares the surface feature of many holders. The governing regime is different: ordering, competition, and pricing under the Schedules follow FAR Subpart 8.4 and GSA's own rules, and the current FAR Part 16 expressly recognizes that the Schedule authorities and rules take precedence over the generic indefinite-delivery procedures.25 This precedence is the practical reason the distinction matters. An action placed under the Schedules is analyzed against the Subpart 8.4 framework, not the Subpart 16.5 framework, so describing a Schedule order as if it were an ordinary FAR 16.5 task order misstates which rules apply. As of June 25, 2026, DoD contracting officers read the Part 16 text in the form rewritten by the Revolutionary FAR Overhaul, applied under a class deviation effective March 16, 2026; the recognition that Schedule rules take precedence is a feature of that overhauled Part 16.26

The three kinds of BPA

A blanket purchase agreement (BPA) is a repetitive-purchase arrangement, but "BPA" is not one thing. Three distinct concepts share the abbreviation, and they operate under different authorities and rules:

BPA type Authority What it is
FAR Part 13 BPA FAR Part 13 (simplified acquisition) A simplified "charge account" method for anticipated recurring purchases.
Schedule BPA FAR 8.405-3, established under one or more GSA Schedule contracts A blanket purchase agreement layered on top of existing Schedule contracts.
BPA under another authorized vehicle The governing vehicle and its applicable rules A BPA available when the underlying vehicle and the applicable rules permit it.

A FAR Part 13 BPA is the simplified-acquisition "charge account": a method for filling anticipated repetitive needs without writing a separate purchase document each time. A Schedule BPA is established under one or more GSA Schedule contracts and is governed by FAR 8.405-3, inheriting the Schedule framework rather than the simplified-acquisition framework.27 A BPA under another authorized vehicle exists only when that vehicle and its applicable rules allow it, and takes its rules from that vehicle. The recurring misconception is to assume that all BPAs operate under the same rules; they do not, and identifying the authority is the first step in any analysis.

Across all three types, the same structural point holds: a BPA generally establishes a purchasing arrangement, while the individual calls or orders placed against it create the actual purchases. The arrangement is the framework; the calls are the transactions. This is why a BPA is, by itself, frequently not a contract, and why its existence says little about how much has actually been bought — a point connected to the money concepts in Awards, ceilings, obligations, and spending and to the broader catalogue of arrangements that are not contract types in Common "not a contract type" traps.

BOA and basic agreement

Two further instruments are easy to mistake for contracts. A basic agreement is a written instrument of understanding that establishes the clauses to apply to future separate contracts entered into during its term. It does not itself contain the work; it pre-positions terms so that later contracts can be executed more efficiently.

A Basic Ordering Agreement (BOA) goes one step further. In addition to clauses, it establishes a description of the supplies or services to be provided, methods for pricing, and the mechanics for issuing and delivering against anticipated future orders. The crucial fact is that neither a basic agreement nor a BOA is itself a contract, and neither obligates the Government to buy anything or promises any future work.28 A contract comes into being only when an order is placed against the BOA and the parties agree on its terms.

The most consequential practical implication concerns money. Because a BOA is not a contract and carries no purchase commitment, a BOA "ceiling" is not an awarded contract amount and must not be read as one — it is neither obligated money nor a guarantee of orders. Treating a BOA ceiling as an award is a specific instance of the general "award amount" ambiguity that this reference repeatedly flags; the five distinct dollar figures that hide behind that phrase, and the rule against counting potential ceilings as spending, are developed in Awards, ceilings, obligations, and spending. For reporting purposes, BOAs nonetheless surface within the IDV data category alongside Schedules and BPAs, which is exactly why the data-classification-versus-instrument distinction matters: the same record that the data system files under "IDV" may be an instrument that is not, in law, a contract at all.

Vehicle on-ramps, off-ramps, and access

Holding a vehicle can be effectively binary: a firm that is not a holder generally cannot receive orders under a multiple-award vehicle regardless of its capability, because orders are competed among the existing holder set and a non-holder is simply not in it. An on-ramp is the mechanism by which a multiple-award vehicle adds new holders after initial award — through an open season, the creation of a new pool, or a re-solicitation — while an off-ramp removes non-performing or ineligible holders, so the holder population is not necessarily fixed for the life of the vehicle.29 For a non-holder, the realistic routes to participation are limited:

  • winning a seat at an on-ramp, when the vehicle opens one;
  • qualifying under a different vehicle that already covers the same scope; or
  • a teaming or subcontract route under an existing holder.

The full menu of entry choices for a non-holder — including the teaming and subcontract paths — is developed in Prime–subcontractor structure, privity, and flowdowns, and the question of whether a given vehicle is reachable at all feeds the "addressability" analysis in Recompetes and opportunity intelligence. A single-award vehicle is simply the extreme case: a closed holder set of one, with no on-ramp available to a non-holder at all.

The throughline

The vehicles and categories in this chapter resolve cleanly once each is assigned to its proper dimension. IDV is a classification used by the federal data systems; IDIQ, GWAC, Schedule, BPA, and BOA are instruments or arrangements, each with its own governing rules — an IDIQ under FAR Subpart 16.5, a Schedule under FAR Subpart 8.4, a Schedule BPA under FAR 8.405-3, a BOA as a non-contract ordering agreement, and a GWAC as an IT-designated, governmentwide IDIQ. The errors this chapter guards against are all the same error wearing different costumes: collapsing the data label into the instrument, or collapsing one instrument's rules into another's. The dimensional discipline that prevents it is set out in The dimensions of a federal contract, and the single-award-versus-sole-source distinction that often accompanies these vehicle questions is the subject of Single-award, single-source, and sole-source.

Pricing, commerciality, and cost risk

Pricing type is the dimension of a federal award that answers how the contractor is paid and which party bears the financial consequence when the actual cost of performance differs from what was expected. It is a separate axis from the instrument dimension, which governs how work is accessed: an indefinite-delivery indefinite-quantity (IDIQ) contract, a multiple-award contract, an individual task order, a standalone (definitive) contract, or a multiyear contract may each use one — or several — of the pricing arrangements described in this chapter. The arrangements group into four families: fixed-price, incentive, cost-reimbursement, and time-and-materials. Each family allocates cost risk differently, and the choice of family is governed by FAR Part 16 (applied by DoD under the current Revolutionary FAR Overhaul class deviation), not by the choice of vehicle.30

The vehicle-versus-pricing rule

The compact rule that organizes this chapter is that vehicle type determines how work is accessed, while pricing type determines how the contractor is paid and who bears cost risk. These are independent axes. An IDIQ may issue firm-fixed-price orders, cost-reimbursement orders, or both; a standalone contract or a multiyear contract may likewise draw on any pricing family. Nothing about the ordering structure constrains the payment arrangement, and nothing about the payment arrangement implies a particular ordering structure. This independence is the same point made from the instrument side in The dimensions of a federal contract, where treating IDIQ as though it were itself a pricing type is identified as one of the most common reading errors. A reader who keeps the two axes separate can correctly describe an award as, for example, "a multiple-award IDIQ issuing fixed-price-incentive task orders," with no contradiction between the labels.

The fixed-price family

In a firm-fixed-price (FFP) arrangement the contractor agrees to deliver the specified result for an agreed price that is not adjusted on the basis of the contractor's incurred cost. The contractor therefore bears most of the cost risk: if performance costs more than estimated, the contractor absorbs the difference, and if it costs less, the contractor keeps the difference. FFP places the maximum risk and the full responsibility for all costs and resulting profit (or loss) on the contractor, which is what makes it the default arrangement when the work and its cost are well understood.

A fixed-price contract with economic price adjustment (FP-EPA) is a fixed-price arrangement in which the otherwise-firm price may move up or down under defined indices or contingencies — for example, changes in established prices for specified materials or in published labor or commodity indices. The adjustment mechanism is limited to the economic changes the contract names, so cost risk is shared for those defined economic changes while the contractor continues to bear the ordinary performance risk.

A fixed-price incentive (FPI) contract sets a target cost, a target profit, a sharing formula, and a ceiling price. Actual cost is compared against the target cost, and the difference is divided between the parties according to the sharing formula, which adjusts the contractor's profit; the final price the Government pays is determined by that calculation but cannot exceed the ceiling price. Cost risk is therefore shared until the ceiling is reached, after which the contractor bears additional cost alone. The same family includes price redetermination, a fixed-price mechanism under which the price is established or revised at stated points (for example, after an initial period of performance), used when a firm price cannot be set for the entire effort at the outset; it is mentioned here only to place it within the family.

The cost-reimbursement family

Under a cost-reimbursement arrangement the Government reimburses the contractor's allowable incurred costs up to the applicable estimate or funding limit, rather than paying a price fixed in advance. Because payment follows actual allowable cost, the Government bears most of the cost risk; the contractor's exposure is principally to the fee it earns rather than to the costs it incurs, subject to the estimated cost and funding ceilings established in the contract. The family is distinguished internally by how the fee is determined.

  • Cost-plus-fixed-fee (CPFF) pays allowable costs plus a fee that is fixed in dollar terms at award. The fee does not vary with actual cost, so the Government bears most of the cost risk and the fee is normally fixed regardless of performance outcome.
  • Cost-plus-incentive-fee (CPIF) pays allowable costs plus a fee that is adjusted by an objective formula tied to the relationship between actual cost (or other measured outcomes) and a target. Cost risk is shared through the incentive formula, which raises the fee for performance better than target and lowers it for performance worse than target, within stated minimum and maximum fee limits.
  • Cost-plus-award-fee (CPAF) pays allowable costs plus a discretionary award fee determined by the Government's evaluation of the contractor's performance against stated criteria. The Government still bears most of the cost risk, but the award fee supplies a performance incentive through a subjective, periodically determined judgment rather than a formula.

Time-and-materials and labor-hour

A time-and-materials (T&M) arrangement pays the contractor at fixed hourly labor rates for hours worked, plus the actual cost of materials. Because the contractor is paid for the hours it expends rather than for a defined result, T&M carries significant Government risk: it provides no positive profit incentive to control hours, so it requires appropriate Government surveillance to ensure efficient performance. A labor-hour contract is a variant of T&M used where the contractor furnishes no materials — that is, T&M without the materials component — and it allocates cost risk the same way.

Hybrid pricing

A hybrid arrangement uses different pricing types for different elements of the same award, so that the cost-risk allocation depends on each element. Hybrid pricing is common: a single task order can carry a firm-fixed-price contract line-item number (CLIN) alongside a cost-reimbursement CLIN, with each line item priced under the family appropriate to its portion of the work. When a contract or order mixes arrangements in this way, the pricing type is identified at the line-item level rather than for the award as a whole, which is why contract line-item structure is the unit at which payment terms and funding are actually read. The mechanics of identifying pricing and funding line by line are developed in CLINs, SLINs, and funding traceability.

Part 16 selection guidance

FAR Part 16 expresses the choice among pricing types as a function of how confidently the work and its cost can be estimated, with cost risk shifting toward the Government as that confidence declines. Under the current text, the guidance directs:

  • Firm-fixed-price when risk is minimal or can be predicted with reasonable certainty — that is, when the requirement and its cost are well enough defined that the contractor can responsibly accept a fixed price;
  • Cost-reimbursement when requirements or costs cannot be estimated with sufficient accuracy to use a fixed price, so that fixing a price in advance would force the contractor either to inflate the price against uncertainty or to accept indeterminate risk; and
  • Time-and-materials only when the extent or duration of the work cannot be estimated with reasonable confidence at the time of award, and even then only with additional controls.

Because T&M provides no built-in incentive to control hours, FAR Part 16 attaches two further conditions to its use: the contract must include a ceiling price that the contractor exceeds at its own risk, and the Government must provide heightened surveillance of performance to give reasonable assurance that efficient methods and effective cost controls are being used.31 The labor-hour variant carries the same conditions. These requirements make T&M and labor-hour the arrangements of last resort within the selection hierarchy rather than ordinary choices.

Performance-incentive features alongside pricing

Several constructs that adjust a contractor's compensation for performance sit alongside the pricing type rather than constituting pricing types of their own. Incentive fee (as in the fixed-price-incentive and cost-plus-incentive-fee arrangements) and award fee (as in cost-plus-award-fee) are the fee-determination mechanisms described above; they modify how much the contractor earns on top of, or as part of, the underlying fixed-price or cost-reimbursement structure. A related construct, the award term, is not a fee at all but an additional period of performance that the contractor earns by meeting stated performance standards. An award term is therefore a performance-incentive feature expressed in time rather than money, and because it is a separately earned performance period it is treated with options and award terms rather than as a pricing family. The distinction matters for reading an award correctly: a CPAF order and an order with an award term may both reward good performance, but the first does so through fee and the second through additional ordering time.

How a price is substantiated: an orientation

The sections that follow are orientation-level, not an accounting manual: they sketch the machinery that surrounds the pricing arrangements above without attempting the detail an estimator or auditor would need. The unifying theme is the business-system infrastructure that a given pricing arrangement obliges a contractor to maintain. A pricing type does more than allocate cost risk; it dictates what a contractor must be able to substantiate, what records it must keep, and which audits and government reviews it must be prepared to pass. A firm-fixed-price commercial buy and a cost-reimbursement research contract sit at opposite ends of that obligation, and the distance between them is measured in business systems rather than in the price itself.

Price analysis versus cost analysis

The two principal methods of evaluating a proposed price differ in what they look at. Price analysis evaluates a proposed price against the market — competing quotes, published catalogs, prior prices, or other comparison points — without examining the individual cost elements that make up the price. Cost analysis examines the build-up of estimated costs: direct labor, materials, indirect rates, and profit, element by element. Cost analysis applies precisely when adequate price competition or commerciality is absent, so that the market cannot by itself establish that a price is fair and reasonable. The choice of method under FAR 15.404-1 therefore determines what a contractor must substantiate: a price the market validates, or a cost structure the contractor must expose and defend.32

Commercial-item pricing

Commercial products and services — bought under FAR Part 12 and defined at FAR 2.101 — are acquired on the basis of price reasonableness established from commercial and market pricing, generally without certified cost or pricing data under FAR 15.403-1. The commercial classification thus carries a direct pricing consequence: it routes the buy toward price analysis against the marketplace and away from the cost-build-up substantiation that noncommercial work attracts. This is why the commercial classification matters to pricing even though "commercial" is a classification, not a pricing type — a distinction catalogued among the cross-dimensional confusions in Common "not a contract type" traps.33

Indirect rates and wrap rates

A contractor's price is not built from direct labor alone. It builds from direct labor plus indirect rates — overhead, general and administrative (G&A), and fringe — which compound on top of the base labor cost into an effective wrap rate, the multiplier that turns a raw labor dollar into a billed dollar. The decisive orientation point is that the wrap rate is an artifact of the contractor's own cost structure, not of the contract: two firms bidding the same labor can carry very different wrap rates, and the same firm carries its wrap rate across the contracts it holds. The composition and administration of these indirect rates are governed by FAR 31.203 and the indirect-rate administration of FAR Part 42.34

Why cost-type work demands dependable timekeeping

Cost-reimbursement and time-and-materials or labor-hour arrangements reimburse incurred labor, which means the bill is only as trustworthy as the records of hours and costs behind it. Before a contractor can responsibly hold cost-type work, it must therefore maintain auditable timekeeping and an adequate accounting system that accumulates costs by contract and by CLIN — the criteria reflected in DFARS 252.242-7006 (Accounting System Administration) and the SF 1408 adequacy standard.35 This is the clearest illustration of the chapter's theme that the pricing arrangement drives the required infrastructure: the same business system that is optional background for a firm-fixed-price commercial buy becomes a precondition for cost-type award. The CLIN-level cost accumulation it depends on is developed in CLINs, SLINs, and funding traceability, and the invoicing that flows from it connects to the modification and invoicing mechanics in Contract modifications.

Certified (truthful) cost or pricing data

Above the applicable threshold and absent an exception, an offeror must submit and certify cost or pricing data as accurate, complete, and current — the requirement long known as the Truth in Negotiations Act and now expressed as "truthful cost or pricing data." The certification is consequential because it shifts risk onto the contractor after award: if the certified data was defective — inaccurate, incomplete, or out of date as of the agreed date — the Government may seek a price reduction in the amount by which the defect inflated the price, under FAR 15.403-4.36 Commerciality and adequate price competition are among the exceptions that remove the requirement, which is one reason the commercial classification and the competition posture matter so directly to a contractor's substantiation burden.

Cost Accounting Standards, DCAA, and contractor business systems

Three further pieces of infrastructure round out the orientation. The Cost Accounting Standards (CAS) — found in FAR Part 30 and codified at 48 C.F.R. Chapter 99 — govern how a covered contractor measures, assigns, and allocates costs, so that costs are accounted for consistently across its contracts. The Defense Contract Audit Agency (DCAA) performs the cost audits that test those measurements and the incurred costs a contractor bills. And DoD reviews a defined set of contractor business systems — accounting, estimating, purchasing, and others — for adequacy.37 These are the standing institutions behind the contract-specific obligations described above: CAS sets the measurement rules, DCAA audits against them, and the business-system reviews certify that the contractor's machinery is sound. The purchasing-system review in particular reaches the contractor's management of its own subcontracts, the subject of Prime–subcontractor structure, privity, and flowdowns.

Reading pricing across vehicles

Keeping the pricing axis distinct from the instrument axis resolves several recurring ambiguities in contract announcements and federal data. A description such as "multiple-award IDIQ" says nothing about how any order under it is priced; the orders may be FFP, cost-reimbursement, T&M, or hybrid, and different orders under the same parent may differ. Conversely, "firm-fixed-price" says nothing about the ordering structure — an FFP award may be a single standalone contract, an order under an IDIQ, or a multiyear production contract. Even a single-award or sole-source procurement, which is a fact about the number of holders and the presence of competition, leaves the pricing type entirely open. The pricing family of a major weapon-production contract is similarly a separate question from whether it is competed, single-award, or multiyear; the funding-and-commitment method of multiyear procurement can be paired with a fixed-price-incentive or other pricing arrangement as the program requires. In every case the reliable reading is to identify the instrument, the award structure, and the pricing type as three separate answers, and — where a contract mixes pricing arrangements — to descend to the CLIN level, because that is where the pricing type and the cost-risk allocation are actually fixed.

Options and award terms

An option is a unilateral right the Government reserves in a contract to acquire additional supplies or services, or to extend a period, on terms already established — a right, not an obligation. It belongs to the time dimension of a federal award: it answers how future periods or quantities are authorized, separately from the questions of how the contractor is paid (the pricing dimension) and how money is committed (the funding dimension covered in Awards, ceilings, obligations, and spending). The single most important property of an option is that, until it is exercised, it generally represents potential rather than committed work: an unexercised option is neither an obligation of funds nor a guarantee of any future order. This chapter sets out what an option is, the standard option clauses, the distinction between options held at the parent level and at the order level, the related but distinct concept of an award term, and the mechanics of when and how an option is exercised.

What an option is

Under the FAR an option gives the Government the right — exercisable at its discretion — to purchase additional quantities of a supply, to extend services for a limited time, to extend the overall term of a contract, or to extend the ordering period of an indefinite-delivery indefinite-quantity (IDIQ) vehicle. In every case the defining features are the same. The right is the Government's alone; the contractor cannot compel its exercise. The terms — price or pricing method, quantity, and duration — are fixed in advance when the contract is awarded, so that exercising an option does not reopen a negotiation. And the right is contingent: it lapses if it is not exercised within the agreed window.

Because an unexercised option is only a reserved right, it is easy to overstate. An option period that has been priced but not exercised adds nothing to what the Government has obligated and nothing to what it is committed to buy; it is one of the several distinct numbers that the phrase "award amount" can hide, alongside the estimated value, the vehicle ceiling, the minimum guarantee, the obligation, and the outlay. Treating the full value of every priced option as though it were committed spending overstates the real commitment in the same way that adding a parent vehicle's ceiling to its child orders does, an error developed in Awards, ceilings, obligations, and spending.

The standard option clauses

The FAR provides a small set of standard option clauses, each addressing a different kind of additional right. A contract may include more than one.

Clause Title What it reserves
FAR 52.217-6 Option for Increased Quantity The right to buy additional units of an item, priced in the contract
FAR 52.217-7 Option for Increased Quantity—Separately Priced Line Item The right to buy additional quantity carried as its own separately priced line item
FAR 52.217-8 Option to Extend Services The right to extend services for a short additional period (generally up to six months)
FAR 52.217-9 Option to Extend the Term of the Contract The right to extend the overall term of the contract by adding option periods

The quantity options (FAR 52.217-6 and FAR 52.217-7) operate on what is bought; the extension options (FAR 52.217-8 and FAR 52.217-9) operate on time. Because each option's price or pricing method is established in the clause, the line item, or the Schedule, an exercised option carries forward whatever pricing type the underlying line item used — a firm-fixed-price line item yields a firm-fixed-price option, and a cost-reimbursement line item yields a cost-reimbursement option.

Exercise of any of these clauses is not automatic. It normally requires that funds be available, that the requirement still exist, that the contractor have complied with the option terms, that notice be given in time, and that the contracting officer determine that exercising the option is in accordance with the contract and otherwise appropriate. Those preconditions are set out at FAR 17.207 and are developed below.38

Parent-level options versus order-level options

A persistent source of confusion is that options can sit at two different layers of an indefinite-delivery structure, and the two are not interchangeable.

A parent-level option extends the parent contract's ordering period or other parent-level authority. For example, a five-year IDIQ may carry a five-year optional ordering period, so that exercising the option keeps the vehicle open to new orders for an additional span. An order-level option extends or adds work under a single task order or delivery order. A task order might have a one-year base and four one-year options of its own, even though the parent IDIQ has a different ordering structure entirely. The two layers are governed by their own clauses, their own funded values, and their own exercise dates.

IDIQ parent contract
│   ordering period: 5-year base + 5-year option   ← parent-level option
│
├── Task Order 0001
│      1-year base + four 1-year options           ← order-level options
│
└── Task Order 0002
       1-year base + two 1-year options            ← order-level options

An order-level option is constrained by its parent. It must be authorized by the parent contract, fall within the parent's scope and maximum value, and be properly evaluated or otherwise supported. The relationship between a vehicle's ordering period and an order's own period of performance — including the question of whether an order option may be exercised after the parent's ordering period has closed — is treated in Period of performance and scope, and the indefinite-delivery framework that supplies the parent authority is described in Indefinite-delivery contracts. The practical rule is to never assume the parent's option and a child order's option are the same thing: exercising one does nothing to the other.

The award term as a distinct concept

An award term is an additional period of performance that the contractor earns by achieving specified performance results, rather than one the Government simply elects to take. It resembles an option operationally — it extends performance on pre-established terms — but its trigger is different: it rests on an award-term plan and defined performance criteria measured over the contract, so that strong performance is rewarded with additional time and weak performance forecloses it.

The award term is distinct on two sides. It is not an ordinary option, because an option is exercised at the Government's discretion subject to FAR 17.207 preconditions, whereas an award term is contingent on the contractor's measured results under the award-term plan. And it is not an award fee, which is monetary compensation paid for performance under a cost-plus-award-fee arrangement (one of the cost-reimbursement pricing arrangements discussed in Pricing types and cost risk); an award term pays in time, not money. Keeping the three apart — option, award term, award fee — avoids conflating a reserved right, an earned period, and a performance payment.

When exactly is an option exercised

An option does not float free in time. There is a date, or an exercise window, within which the Government must act, and outside of which the right is gone. The controlling information is normally found in a definite set of places: the option clause itself; the contract or order Schedule; the applicable contract line item (CLIN); and any prior modification that has changed the dates. A reader trying to determine whether an option is still live must check all four, because a modification can move a window that the original Schedule set.

The mechanics are clearest in FAR 52.217-9, Option to Extend the Term of the Contract, which is written with blanks that the solicitation fills in. Three blanks matter:

  • the period within which the Government must give the actual written notice that it is exercising the option;
  • the number of days of preliminary notice of the Government's intent to exercise; and
  • the maximum total duration of the contract, including all option periods.

The clause uses 60 days as the default preliminary-notice period unless the solicitation inserts a different number.39 The preliminary notice is a courtesy that lets a contractor anticipate continued performance, but it is expressly not an exercise of the option and does not commit the Government: a Government that sends the preliminary notice may still decline to exercise. The actual exercise is a separate, later act — typically a unilateral modification or other written notice signed by the contracting officer within the option-exercise window. Unilateral exercise is possible precisely because the terms were fixed at award; the modification mechanics are described in Contract modifications.

Before exercising, the contracting officer must make the pre-exercise determinations required by FAR 17.207. In substance the contracting officer must determine that funds are available; that the requirement still exists; that exercising the option is the most advantageous method of fulfilling that requirement (for example, advantageous compared with a new competition); that the contractor's performance has been acceptable; and that the option was properly evaluated and priced, or is otherwise determinable under the original contract terms.38 Only a warranted contracting officer may sign the exercise, a point of authority developed in Contracting authority and who makes the award. The use of option periods on a single award is also conceptually distinct from a multiyear commitment, in which the Government commits across program years up front; that contrast is the subject of Multiyear procurement and major weapon production.

What happens when a deadline is missed

Because the option right is bounded by its window, timing is not a formality. A late purported unilateral option exercise is generally defective: the Government cannot unilaterally exercise a right it failed to exercise within the agreed window, and an attempt to do so after the window has closed does not bind the contractor on the option terms. The right has simply lapsed.

The parties can sometimes respond by entering a bilateral agreement to continue performance, but such an agreement is not a retroactive option exercise. It is a newly negotiated modification, and it must independently satisfy the other dimensions that make any contract action valid: it must respect competition requirements, stay within scope, be executed by a contracting officer with authority, comply with fiscal law and funding availability, and observe any limitations imposed by the parent vehicle. This is a direct application of the principle that being in scope is necessary but never sufficient — an action must also be timely, competed or justified, funded, and properly authorized — set out in Period of performance and scope.

Two related misconceptions are worth retiring. An option does not exercise itself merely because money was budgeted for the period; budgeting is not obligation, and an appropriation set aside in anticipation does not constitute the contracting officer's written exercise. Nor does an option exercise itself because the contractor kept working: continued performance after a window closes does not convert a lapsed option into an exercised one. In both cases what exists is, at most, the basis for a negotiated modification that must satisfy the validity tests on its own — not a "magical" retroactive exercise of the option.

Period of performance and scope

The plain statement that "the contract expired" is almost always too imprecise to be useful, because a single defense acquisition runs several independent clocks at once, each governing a different question and each producing a different consequence when it ends. This chapter develops two of the dimensions set out in The dimensions of a federal contract: the time dimension, which asks how the windows for ordering, performing, and delivering are authorized and when they close, and a validity question that sits across the competition and time dimensions, namely whether a contemplated order or modification is in scope. The two are connected by a recurring fact pattern — the parent indefinite-delivery indefinite-quantity (IDIQ) contract whose ordering period has closed while child orders remain alive — and by a single governing principle stated at the end: being in scope is necessary but never sufficient to make a contract action lawful.

The several independent clocks

Treating an acquisition as having one expiration date conflates distinct events that fall on different dates and carry different legal effects. The following clocks can all run on the same vehicle.

Clock What it governs Consequence when it ends
Parent IDIQ ordering period When the Government may issue new task or delivery orders No new orders may be issued
Parent option-exercise window When the Government may extend the parent ordering period If not timely exercised, the unilateral right generally lapses
Order period of performance When services under a particular order are scheduled to be performed Performance becomes due, but the order does not vanish automatically
Order delivery schedule When supplies must be delivered Delivery becomes due; administration, acceptance, payment, and claims may continue
Order option window When the Government may activate a future order period or quantity The option must be exercised according to the order's terms
Ultimate delivery date The latest date the contractor can be required to deliver under orders issued during the parent ordering period Performance generally cannot be pushed beyond that date without additional authority
Appropriation availability When particular funds may incur new obligations Expired funds generally cannot finance a new need, although they may remain usable to liquidate or adjust valid existing obligations
Contract closeout Administrative completion after final performance, acceptance, payment, audits, and claims Records are closed; this can occur long after the performance date

These clocks are anchored in the structure that current FAR Part 16 requires, which under the DoD class deviation reflects the Revolutionary FAR Overhaul rewrite.40 An IDIQ must state its ordering period, its options, its minimum and maximum, its general scope, the activities authorized to order, and the last date by which delivery may be required. Each order, in turn, must separately contain its own date, line items, performance or delivery schedule, statement of work, and accounting and appropriation data. Because the parent and the order each carry their own time and funding terms, a question such as "may this work still be done?" has no single answer until the relevant clock is identified. The distinction between the parent ordering period and the order period of performance, and between a parent-level option and an order-level option, is introduced in Indefinite-delivery contracts; the mechanics and deadlines of exercising any option belong to Options and award terms.

A timeline example

A concrete sequence shows how the parent ordering clock and an order's own clocks operate independently.

Parent IDIQ ordering period .......... Oct 1, 2022  –  Sep 30, 2027
Ultimate date for performance/delivery ............... Sep 30, 2030

Task Order 0042 issued .............................. Sep 15, 2027
  Task Order 0042 base period ...... Oct 1, 2027  –  Sep 30, 2028
  Task Order 0042 option period .... Oct 1, 2028  –  Sep 30, 2029

Task Order 0042 was validly issued on September 15, 2027, before the parent ordering period closed on September 30, 2027. Its performance may continue afterward because FAR 52.216-22 provides that an order issued during the effective period of the indefinite-quantity contract may be completed within the time specified in the order, and the parent contract continues to govern that order even though no new orders may be placed under it.41 Several practical results follow. A new task order ordinarily could not be issued on October 1, 2027, because the ordering period had ended the day before. Task Order 0042 may nonetheless run through September 30, 2028 on its base period. Its option might be exercisable in August or September 2028 even though the parent ordering period has long since closed — but only if the parent contract and the order expressly support that arrangement, the option was part of the timely issued order, and the parent's scope, maximum value, and ultimate delivery date are all respected. What the Government cannot do is simply keep adding unrelated work through 2030: that date is the ultimate completion date, the outer boundary on finishing work already properly ordered, not a free additional ordering period.

The "zombie IDIQ" question

The situation in the timeline — a parent IDIQ whose ordering period has expired while one or more child orders remain active — is sometimes described informally as a "zombie" IDIQ, but there is no formal legal category by that name. It is ordinary contract administration. A parent vehicle can be closed to new orders yet continue to govern valid orders issued while it was open, provided those orders were within the parent's scope, issued during the stated ordering period, and within the parent's maximum value. Difficulty arises only when a party tries to treat the surviving orders as a reason to do something that the closed ordering period no longer permits.

The following table separates the actions that are ordinarily available after the ordering period closes from those that ordinarily are not.

Proposed action Usually possible?
Accept completed work Yes
Pay invoices Yes
Deobligate excess funds Yes
Add incremental funding for already-ordered work Potentially
Pay a valid equitable adjustment or claim Potentially
Extend the schedule so the contractor can finish the same work Potentially

Add a new system, site, quantity, or service period Usually no

Continue recurring services indefinitely through modifications No

The pattern in the table is that administration of work already properly bought continues, while anything that amounts to buying new work after the ordering window has closed does not. The "potentially" entries turn on the specific contract terms and on funding, which is why the legal test cannot be reduced to whether money is involved.

Why "adding money" is not the legal test

A common shortcut treats any modification that adds money as the same kind of action, but the dollar figure is not the test; the question is what the added obligation actually purchases. Consider five hypothetical modifications that each add $5 million to an existing order: (1) funding another increment of the cost already estimated for work that is within the existing order; (2) paying an equitable adjustment caused by a Government-directed change; (3) exercising an option that was evaluated as part of the order; (4) settling a claim arising from past performance; and (5) adding an entirely new work package. The first four may be lawful, depending on the contract documents and the availability of funds, because they pay for, adjust, or complete work already within the deal. The fifth may be an out-of-scope procurement even though it uses the same contractor, the same program, the same contracting office, and the same technology. The converse also holds: a modification can be out of scope even though it adds no money at all — for example, by substantially changing the nature of the required work, or by replacing one deliverable with a materially different one. The treatment of modifications generally, including bilateral and unilateral changes and why a modification need not add work or money, is the subject of Contract modifications, governed under the DoD deviation by the rewritten FAR Part 43.42

The two-envelope scope analysis

When the contemplated action sits under an IDIQ, the scope question is not asked once but twice, because the work must fit inside two nested boundaries. A child-order modification generally must satisfy both the parent IDIQ scope and the child order scope: the parent's scope contains the order's scope, which in turn must contain the proposed modification. Picturing the relationship as nested envelopes makes the trap visible — being within the parent IDIQ's broad scope is not necessarily enough. If the added work was not reasonably within the competed scope of the existing order, the Government may need to conduct another order competition among the eligible holders or otherwise justify a noncompetitive action, even though the work would have fit comfortably within the parent vehicle as a whole.

The scope factors

Scope is not determined merely by matching a Product Service Code, a North American Industry Classification System (NAICS) code, a program name, or a broad phrase such as "engineering support"; two actions can share all of those labels and still differ in scope. The factors that bear on whether added work is within scope include the change in the type or purpose of the work; the change in quantity or required outcome; the change in period of performance; the magnitude of the price increase; any geographic or organizational expansion; the skills and resources required; whether the original solicitation warned offerors about the possible change; whether the original competitors reasonably could have anticipated it; and whether the change would have attracted a materially different field of competitors. Air Force acquisition rules at DAFFARS 5343.102-90 direct contracting officers to document scope determinations and to consider expressly the changes in the work, the performance period, and the cost, together with whether the change was reasonably anticipated and how it affects the field of competition.43

Who decides whether it is in scope

Operationally, the contracting officer makes or adopts the scope determination. The program office and engineers supply the technical facts about what is changing, while agency counsel, competition advocates, and other reviewers may advise or concur, but only a contracting officer acting within delegated authority may execute the modification. That label is not final in an absolute sense. The Government Accountability Office (GAO) or the Court of Federal Claims may review an allegedly out-of-scope order or modification in a bid protest; a board of contract appeals or the Court of Federal Claims may address scope in a contract dispute; and auditors or inspectors general may examine the transaction afterward. Current FAR Part 16 expressly preserves an order protest on the ground that an order increased the parent contract's scope, period, or maximum value — one of the narrow grounds on which order placements remain protestable, as described in Indefinite-delivery contracts. The several distinct meanings of contracting "authority," and the limits of a warranted contracting officer's power, are developed in Contracting authority and who makes the award.

Governing principle: necessary but not sufficient

The clocks and the scope envelopes converge on a single rule that recurs throughout this reference: "in scope" is necessary, but not sufficient. A contract action that fits within both the parent and the order scope may still be unlawful for an independent reason. To be valid, the action must also be timely — taken while the relevant ordering or option clock is open — within the maximum ceiling, properly competed or justified where competition is required, funded from a legally available appropriation, and executed by a contracting officer acting within delegated authority. Each of those is a separate dimension, and a defect in any one of them is enough to defeat the action no matter how clearly the work falls within the contract's scope. The matched distinction between number-of-holders and absence-of-competition that underlies the competition requirement is developed in Single-award, single-source, and sole-source.

Multiyear procurement and major weapon production

Multiyear procurement (MYP) is a funding-and-commitment method by which the Government buys requirements for more than one program year under a single contract without exercising a separate option for each future year. It belongs to the funding dimension of an award — the axis that asks when and how money is obligated and committed — and not to the instrument, pricing, or competition dimensions described in The dimensions of a federal contract. Because major weapons such as aircraft, missiles, ships, and submarines are frequently bought in defined production lots rather than in uncertain quantities, the production contracts that carry MYP are also a leading example of awards that are not indefinite-delivery vehicles at all, which makes this dimension the natural place to separate the funding method from the instrument and from the structure of the supplier base.

What multiyear procurement is

A multiyear contract purchases requirements for more than one — but generally not more than five — program years without requiring the Government to exercise a separate option for every future program year. The defining feature is the up-front commitment: more than one year's requirement is contractually included at the initial award, rather than held in reserve as options to be decided annually. Performance in later years may still be contingent on appropriations being enacted, and to protect the contractor against an up-front commitment that the budget later does not fund, the contract may provide a cancellation payment — and an associated cancellation ceiling — if those appropriations are not made. These features are governed by FAR Subpart 17.1.44 The "generally not more than five program years" span is a structural limit of the method and is recorded as such throughout this reference.

For DoD, multiyear procurement carries additional machinery beyond the FAR baseline. A defense MYP usually involves a present-value cost comparison demonstrating savings against annual contracting, particular budget treatment, statutory findings, congressional authorization or notification where applicable, and explicit controls over cancellation liability and advance procurement. These DoD-specific requirements are set out in DFARS Subpart 217.1, which implements the statutory multiyear and advance-procurement provisions of 10 U.S.C.45

Multiyear procurement versus a base contract with options

The cleanest way to see what MYP is, is to contrast it with the more common structure of an annual base contract carrying options. Both span several years, but they commit the Government very differently. Under an options structure the future-year work is not yet committed: the Government may decline to exercise an option, and the contractor usually has no recovery for the work in an option that is never exercised. Under a multiyear contract, more than one year's requirement is part of the basic deal from the outset, no annual option exercise is needed for the basic multiyear quantities, and the contractor's exposure to an up-front commitment is offset by cancellation protection.

Issue Annual contract with options Multiyear contract
Future-year work at initial award Not yet committed; the Government may decline options More than one year's requirement is contractually included
Annual decision The Government must exercise each option No annual option exercise is needed for the basic multiyear quantities
Appropriations risk The Government can decline an option Later performance may be contingent on appropriations
Contractor protection Usually no recovery for an unexercised option May include cancellation charges or a cancellation ceiling
Typical use Recurring services and ordinary acquisitions Stable, known production or service requirements where multiyear commitment creates value
Production economics Limited commitment to future lots May support economic order quantities, stable production rates, long-lead purchases, and amortization of nonrecurring costs

The last row is the economic reason the method exists. Because a multiyear commitment gives a contractor and its suppliers confidence that several lots will actually be built, it can support buying materials in economical quantities, holding production rates stable, purchasing long-lead items in advance, and amortizing nonrecurring costs across the committed quantity — efficiencies that a year-by-year options buy, with its limited commitment to future lots, cannot reliably unlock.

Related but distinct concepts

Several concepts travel alongside multiyear procurement and are frequently conflated with it, but each is a separate idea:

  • Economic order quantity (EOQ) is the practice of purchasing components or materials in economical quantities across future production lots. It is a buying efficiency that a multiyear commitment can enable, not the commitment itself.
  • Advance procurement is the purchase of long-lead components before the main end-item procurement year, so that material is on hand when assembly begins. DoD controls over advance procurement are part of the DFARS multiyear machinery, but advance procurement can also occur outside a formal MYP.
  • Block buy contracting is another multiyear quantity-commitment approach with its own statutory treatment, distinct from MYP under FAR Subpart 17.1.
  • Incremental funding is the practice of adding funds to a contract or order over time. Incremental funding alone does not make a contract a multiyear procurement; the two answer different questions, as The dimensions of a federal contract notes, and incremental funding is treated in the funding-traceability material at CLINs, SLINs, and funding traceability.
  • A multiple-year contract is an ordinary descriptive phrase, often used for a base period plus options that happens to run across several years. It is not the same as the defined multiyear method, and the resemblance of the words is a frequent source of error of the kind catalogued in Common "not a contract type" traps.

Multiyear procurement is not a pricing type

The single most important classification point is that multiyear procurement is principally a commitment, budgeting, and production-economics method, not a pricing type. A defense MYP very commonly uses a fixed-price arrangement — frequently fixed-price incentive — but the multiyear commitment and the pricing arrangement are separate classifications on separate axes. The pricing dimension, developed in Pricing types and cost risk, asks how the contractor is paid and who bears cost risk; the multiyear method asks how many years of requirement are committed and how that commitment is budgeted and protected. A contract can be multiyear and firm-fixed-price, multiyear and fixed-price-incentive, or in principle multiyear with other pricing, just as the instrument and pricing dimensions are independent of each other.

Why major weapon production is often not an IDIQ

Reporting on weapon programs sometimes assumes that any large, recurring defense buy must be an indefinite-delivery indefinite-quantity (IDIQ) vehicle. For major production, that assumption is usually wrong. Defined production lots of aircraft, missiles, ships, and submarines are frequently purchased through standalone definitive contracts, multiyear procurement contracts, production options, lot contracts, and contract modifications that add authorized production quantities — none of which is an indefinite-delivery vehicle.

The reason is structural. An IDIQ exists to handle requirements whose quantity is genuinely uncertain at award, which is why FAR requires a stated minimum and maximum for that form; the indefinite-delivery framework is the subject of Indefinite-delivery contracts. For a production lot, the Government usually knows the number of end items it is funding — a specific number of airframes, missiles, or ships — so an indefinite-quantity arrangement is less natural than it is for the uncertain demand of engineering support. Buying specified quantities is exactly what a definitive contract, a multiyear contract, or a quantity-adding modification does well. The corollary is that the supporting work for the same weapon system — engineering services, repair, spare parts, or technical assistance, where future demand is genuinely uncertain — may still be acquired through IDIQs or basic ordering agreements (BOAs). Two awards on the same program can therefore sit on different points of the instrument axis: a definitive production contract for the end items and an IDIQ for the sustainment around them.

The Virginia-class submarine example

The construction of Virginia-class nuclear-powered attack submarines is a worked case in which all the dimensions diverge, and it is frequently mis-described. The shorthand that Virginia-class boats are bought through "a sole-source IDIQ that always goes to Electric Boat" collapses at least four separate dimensions into one phrase and is inaccurate on each. The prime is General Dynamics Electric Boat (GDEB) — a General Dynamics shipyard, not General Electric — and the instrument is not an IDIQ.

The industrial base and the teaming structure

Only two U.S. shipbuilders currently construct nuclear-powered submarines: General Dynamics Electric Boat and HII Newport News Shipbuilding. That extraordinarily limited industrial base makes ordinary marketplace competition unrealistic for complete nuclear-submarine construction.46 Within that base, the Virginia program runs on a long-standing teaming arrangement: GDEB is the prime contractor under the current teaming structure, Newport News Shipbuilding is the major subcontractor and co-builder, both shipyards construct major submarine sections, and both have delivered completed Virginia-class submarines. The Navy describes the arrangement as a teaming arrangement between GDEB and Newport News, not as Electric Boat performing the entire program alone — a prime-and-subcontractor relationship of the kind analyzed in Prime–subcontractor structure, privity, and flowdowns.

The 2019 Block V action, by dimension

The December 2, 2019 Block V action is best read by separating its dimensions rather than forcing it into a single label:

Virginia-class Block V (announced Dec. 2, 2019)
│
├── Instrument ............ existing definitive production contract
├── Action .............. $22.2 billion contract modification
├── Pricing type ........ fixed-price incentive
├── Funding / commitment . multiyear procurement
├── Quantity ............ nine submarines
├── Option .............. one additional submarine
├── Prime ............... General Dynamics Electric Boat

The action was a $22.2 billion modification to an existing definitive production contract, priced fixed-price-incentive, structured as a multiyear procurement, for nine submarines plus an option for one more.47 It was not an IDIQ: the Navy was buying specified submarines in a defined production block, not reserving an uncertain quantity. A modification of this magnitude is unremarkable on its own axis — the point that a modification can be enormous, and need not be a new contract, is developed in Contract modifications.

The 2025 addition

In April 2025 the Navy again used contract modifications, this time to add two FY2024 Virginia-class submarines together with related shipyard investments. The announced actions involved both GDEB and Newport News contracts, with more than $9.4 billion obligated at award from Shipbuilding and Conversion, Navy funds.48 As in 2019, the vehicle was a modification adding specified production quantities, not an indefinite-delivery order.

Why Electric Boat is the prime — and the right competition baseline

That GDEB holds the prime role is a matter of industrial-base reality rather than the absence of a competition that could otherwise have occurred. The relevant factors include GDEB's role as design authority and lead yard; its nuclear-submarine-specific facilities and workforce; its existing design, production, testing, and integration systems; the practical impossibility of quickly creating another qualified nuclear-submarine prime; the Navy's deliberate allocation of work between the two existing nuclear-capable shipyards; and the need to maintain the submarine industrial base. The economically meaningful competition for complete nuclear-submarine construction is therefore not comparable to a normal information-technology services solicitation with twenty capable bidders. The common FAR Part 6 basis for limited competition captures this: only one responsible source — or, for DoD, one or a limited number of responsible sources — can satisfy the requirement.49 This is a competition-dimension judgment, and it is distinct from the single-award and sole-source labels analyzed in Single-award, single-source, and sole-source; a limited industrial base is not the same thing as a one-holder vehicle.

A more accurate description

Read across its dimensions, the program is described accurately as follows: Virginia-class submarines are acquired through large definitive, multiyear production contracts and modifications, with GDEB as prime and Newport News as a major subcontractor and co-builder within a two-shipyard nuclear-submarine industrial base. Laid out by axis, the same action separates cleanly — instrument: definitive production contract; action: contract modification; commitment method: multiyear procurement; pricing type: fixed-price incentive; competition: limited industrial-base environment; prime structure: GDEB prime, HII–Newport News major subcontractor; deliverables: specified submarines. The example shows why the dimensions should be tracked separately: every one of these answers a different question, and no single phrase such as "sole-source IDIQ" can carry all of them at once.

Prime contracting, subcontracting, teaming, and first-tier reporting

Most defense work is performed not by a single company but by a chain of them: a federal agency contracts with a prime contractor, the prime contracts with subcontractors, and those subcontractors may in turn contract with firms below them. This chapter develops the performance chain, the legal relationship, and part of the data-classification dimensions introduced in The dimensions of a federal contract: who actually performs the work, who holds a contract with whom, and how the lower tiers appear in public data. Its organizing legal idea is privity of contract — the Government's contractual relationship normally runs only to the prime — from which follow the rules on responsibility, payment, and claims; how prime-contract clauses "flow down"; how a firm that does not hold a vehicle can still enter the chain; what rights in technical data and software pass with the work; and how first-tier subcontracts are reported in public spending data. Two errors recur and the chapter guards against both: treating a named subcontractor as a direct federal contractor, and treating the single word subaward — which carries two distinct legal meanings and labels an instrument that is not legally an "award" at all — as though it named one thing.

The contracting chain

A federal acquisition typically forms a tiered chain. The agency awards a prime contract or task order; the prime forms first-tier subcontracts; and first-tier subcontractors may form second-tier subcontracts. Running alongside that vertical chain, the prime also makes ordinary supplier and vendor purchases that support performance. The structure can be drawn as follows.

Federal agency
│
└── Prime contract or task order  ← Government privity stops here
       │
       ├── First-tier subcontract
       │      │
       │      └── Second-tier subcontract
       │
       └── Supplier / vendor purchase order

The term subcontract is defined broadly: under FAR 44.101 it includes any contract or purchase order entered into by a subcontractor to furnish supplies or services for the performance of a prime contract or a higher-tier subcontract, so the definition reaches well beyond the first tier and captures purchase orders, not only formal contracts.50 As the core vocabulary notes, a subcontract is a real contract, but one between private parties rather than with the Government.

Privity of contract and its four consequences

Privity of contract is the direct contractual relationship between two parties. In the normal arrangement the Government has privity with the prime contractor and not with any subcontractor, even a subcontractor performing the bulk of the work. Four consequences follow, and together they explain much of how the chain behaves.

  • Responsibility runs to the prime. The prime is responsible for delivering the Government's requirement. Performance failures by a subcontractor are, as between the prime and the Government, the prime's problem to manage and cure; the agency looks to the prime, not to the firm that actually fell short.
  • Payment runs through the prime. The prime normally pays its subcontractors out of what it receives, rather than the Government paying subcontractors directly. The flow of money tracks the contractual chain.
  • Claims run against the prime. A subcontractor ordinarily submits contractual claims against the prime, not directly against the Government, because it has no contract with the Government on which to sue. Mechanisms by which a prime sponsors a subcontractor's claim against the Government exist but are the exception, not the rule.
  • Consent does not create a prime relationship. Government approval of, or consent to, a subcontract does not ordinarily turn the subcontractor into a prime contractor. Consent (discussed below) is a control over the prime's purchasing decisions; it is not the award of a new prime contract and does not establish Government privity with the subcontractor.

These consequences are why the same work can be described as performed "by" a well-known subcontractor while the contract, the obligation, and the delivery responsibility all sit with a prime the public may never hear of. The distinction also underlies the warning in the core vocabulary against treating a named subcontractor as a direct federal contractor.

When subcontractors enter

Subcontractors do not all appear at one moment. They can enter the structure at six distinct points across the life of an acquisition, and the point of entry affects what, if anything, the subcontractor is promised.

  1. Before the prime solicitation. Companies position themselves by establishing teaming agreements or nondisclosure agreements in anticipation of a requirement, before the Government has issued a solicitation.
  2. In the prime proposal. The offeror identifies key subcontractors and describes the proposed workshare, capabilities, labor, and costs as part of its proposal to the Government.
  3. At parent-vehicle award. On award of a parent vehicle — for example an indefinite-delivery indefinite-quantity (IDIQ) contract — the prime establishes long-term subcontracts intended to support future orders rather than any single deliverable.
  4. During an order competition. When competing for a specific task or delivery order, the prime adds specialists assembled specifically for that order. Order-level competition under indefinite-delivery vehicles is described in Indefinite-delivery contracts.
  5. During performance. As the work proceeds, the prime purchases materials, services, or specialized work that the contract requires.
  6. Through lower tiers. First-tier subcontractors themselves purchase work from other firms, extending the chain downward to second and lower tiers.

A recurring misconception attaches to the second point. A company named as a "team member" in a proposal is not automatically guaranteed a subcontract. Whether that company actually receives work depends on the private teaming agreement or subcontract agreement creating an obligation; being listed in a winning proposal, by itself, does not. This matters when a parent vehicle is later used to place orders, because the long-term subcontracts formed at award (the third point) and the order-specific specialists added later (the fourth point) may be entirely different firms.

Teaming and the entry choices for a non-holder

The points of entry above also describe the routes by which a firm that cannot, or does not, win a prime contract on its own still reaches federal work. A firm that does not hold a relevant vehicle has, in practice, a bounded menu of choices:

  • Win as a prime on an open-market competition or by qualifying for the applicable vehicle.
  • Join a vehicle through an on-ramp — taking a holder's seat when a multiple-award vehicle adds holders, as described in IDV, IDIQ, MAC, GWAC, and Schedules.
  • Team with an existing holder, contributing to the holder's proposal and performance under a teaming arrangement.
  • Subcontract to the likely prime, supplying a defined scope one tier below the prime contract.
  • Sell a separable component or capability that the Government or a prime can adopt without displacing the wider integration.

Two cautions govern this menu. First, a teaming arrangement is a private contract: a contractor team arrangement or teaming agreement binds the parties to each other on the terms they negotiate, and — as the six points of entry establish — being named on a winning team does not by itself guarantee a subcontract or a fixed share of the work. Second, vehicle access can be the binding constraint rather than capability: where a requirement will be competed only among the holders of a particular multiple-award contract, a non-holder must secure a seat, qualify under another vehicle, or take one of the teaming or subcontract routes above before technical merit can matter at all. How heavily that access constraint weighs on a given pursuit is the addressability question developed in Recompetes and opportunity intelligence from awards data; the limiting case of a closed holder set is the single-award vehicle treated in Single-award, single-source, and sole-source.

Flowdown clauses

Some clauses in a prime contract must be included in applicable subcontracts. Such clauses are called flowdowns. The mechanism allows the Government to extend selected obligations down the chain even though it has no privity with the lower-tier firms: the obligation is carried by the prime's contract with its subcontractor, which the prime is directed to write in.

A central and frequently missed point is that not every prime-contract clause automatically flows down. Whether a particular clause must appear in a given subcontract depends on several variables:

  • the value of the subcontract;
  • the tier of the subcontract (first-tier, second-tier, and so on);
  • whether the subcontract is for a commercial product or commercial service;
  • the place of performance;
  • the type of data or system access involved;
  • whether classified or controlled information is implicated;
  • domestic-source rules; and
  • the exact prescription or wording of the clause itself, which states where and how far it flows.

Because applicability turns on these factors, the set of clauses that flows into a $20,000 commercial purchase order differs sharply from the set that flows into a large, non-commercial, first-tier subcontract with system access. In DoD subcontracting, flowdowns commonly implicate cybersecurity and safeguarding requirements, data rights, specialty metals, Buy American and Berry Amendment domestic-source rules, counterfeit-parts controls, audit rights, cost or pricing data, and contractor business-system requirements. Several of these obligations ride along with the kinds of long-running production contracts treated in Multiyear procurement and major weapon production and survive into contract modifications.

Government rights in technical data and software

One flowdown subject is consequential enough to treat on its own: rights in technical data and computer software. The Government does not automatically own what a contractor develops; what it obtains is a license of defined scope, and the scope turns on who funded the development. The framework distinguishes technical data (recorded information of a scientific or technical nature) from computer software, and grades the Government's license — at orientation level — from unlimited rights (typically where development was funded entirely by the Government), through government-purpose rights (mixed funding, for a period), to limited rights in technical data and restricted rights in software (typically where development was funded entirely at private expense).51

Three practical points follow. First, background intellectual property — what a firm brings to the work, developed at its own expense — is treated differently from the deliverables produced under the contract, and the line between them is set by the contract and the funding source, not by who physically created the item. Second, assertions and markings matter procedurally: an offeror is generally required to identify, in or with its proposal, the technical data and software for which it asserts restrictions on the Government's rights, and a failure to assert in the required form can forfeit the restriction or even render an offer ineligible. Third, data rights shape an incumbent's replaceability: if the Government holds only limited or restricted rights in the data needed to recompete a requirement, a would-be successor cannot simply be handed the incumbent's drawings or software, which raises the transition barrier analyzed as part of addressability in Recompetes and opportunity intelligence from awards data. The same logic runs down the chain: an overly broad prime or teaming agreement can capture a subcontractor's background IP or its asserted rights, so the data-rights terms of a teaming arrangement deserve the same scrutiny as its workshare. The distinctive data-rights features of SBIR/STTR and Other Transaction work are noted in Alternative acquisition pathways and "not a contract type" traps.

What a procurement "subaward" legally is

For a federal procurement contract, the instrument that the transparency systems group under the heading subaward is legally a first-tier subcontract award. It is not a federal prime contract. It is a private contract between the prime contractor and the subcontractor, and the Government generally is not a party to it. The legal chain is best read as a sequence of distinct relationships:

Federal agency
   │   prime contract  (Government ── prime contractor)
   ▼
Prime contractor
   │   first-tier subcontract  (prime ── subcontractor; Government not a party)
   ▼
Subcontractor

Because the FAR definition of subcontract reaches purchase orders and their modifications,50 the population of "subcontracts" under a prime contract is large and varied; only a subset of those subcontracts is ever reported under the transparency rules, a point developed below. The defining structural fact is that the subcontractor is not a direct federal contractor: the Government generally has privity of contract only with the prime, as the privity discussion above establishes. The general vocabulary distinction between a subcontract and a subaward is drawn in The core vocabulary.

The subcontract carries its own pricing arrangement

A first-tier subcontract has its own pricing arrangement, and that arrangement need not match the pricing type of the prime contract above it. The prime contractor and the subcontractor negotiate the subcontract's pricing between themselves, and because the Government generally is not a party to the subcontract, it is generally not a party to that pricing decision. The pricing arrangement on a subcontract may be any of the forms used in the wider acquisition system — firm-fixed-price, cost-reimbursement, time-and-materials, labor-hour, fixed-price incentive, a purchase order, a long-term supplier agreement, or a negotiated commercial agreement — and the choice is made on the merits of the supply relationship rather than dictated by the prime's own pricing type. The full taxonomy of pricing arrangements and the cost risk each allocates is the subject of Pricing, commerciality, and cost risk.

The practical consequence is that the prime and the subcontract do not need to use the same pricing type, so a complete classification of a subcontract names both its own arrangement and the arrangement of the prime it supports. A correct classification might read: a first-tier, firm-fixed-price subcontract supporting a cost-plus-incentive-fee prime contract. The two pricing dimensions are independent. Reading a subcontract's pricing off the prime's pricing type — or assuming a fixed-price prime is performed entirely through fixed-price subcontracts — conflates two separately negotiated arrangements and is a frequent error.

Four distinct subcontracting-compliance systems

A frequent error is to collapse "subcontracting compliance" into a single concept. There are at least four separate systems, each with its own authority, trigger, and purpose; they should never be merged.

First-tier subaward reporting

The first system is public-transparency reporting of first-tier subawards under FAR 52.204-10 and related authorities.52 It exists so that subcontract awards, not just prime awards, appear in public spending data. The reporting system was formerly the FFATA Subaward Reporting System (FSRS), which was retired on March 8, 2025; first-tier subaward reporting then moved to SAM.gov.53 This system is about disclosure, not goals or performance shares, and is treated in detail in the reporting sections below.

Small-business subcontracting plans

The second system is the small-business subcontracting plan, the FAR Part 19 framework — specifically FAR Subpart 19.7 — for establishing and reporting small-business subcontracting goals.54 A plan is generally required when subcontracting possibilities exist, the prime is not itself treated as a small business for the requirement, and the value exceeds the applicable threshold (generally above $900,000, or $2 million for construction). The former eSRS reporting capability for these plans moved into SAM.gov in 2026. This is a goal-and-reporting system distinct from transparency reporting, and it is developed fully in Small business in defense contracting.

Consent to subcontract and purchasing-system review

The third system is consent to subcontract and purchasing-system review under FAR Part 44. Certain subcontracts require the contracting officer's consent before the prime may proceed, with the requirement depending on the contract type, the value, and whether the prime holds an approved purchasing system. A Contractor Purchasing System Review (CPSR) examines how the prime manages its purchases and subcontracts; a prime with an approved purchasing system faces consent requirements different from one without. As noted above, consent is a control over the prime's purchasing process and does not create Government privity with the subcontractor.

Limitations on subcontracting

The fourth system is limitations on subcontracting, which apply to small-business set-asides and may require the small-business prime to perform a specified portion of the work itself.55 Its purpose is to ensure that a set-aside actually benefits the small-business awardee rather than passing the work through to other firms. This is separate both from subcontracting-plan goals (the second system) and from first-tier transparency reporting (the first system): it constrains how much of the prime's own work may be subcontracted, rather than reporting subawards or setting aspirational goals. It is treated in full in Small business in defense contracting.

Keeping these four apart is essential to reading a defense subcontract correctly. Transparency reporting discloses who received subawards; subcontracting plans set and report small-business goals; consent and CPSR govern whether and how the prime may subcontract at all; and limitations on subcontracting cap how much a set-aside prime may push outside its own walls. A single subcontract can sit inside several of these systems at once, in the same way that a single award occupies several dimensions at once, but each system answers a different question.

What first-tier reporting captures

For procurement contracts the applicable reporting rule is the clause at FAR 52.204-10, which implements the Federal Funding Accountability and Transparency Act (FFATA, Pub. L. 109-282), the statute behind first-tier subaward transparency reporting.52 The rule focuses on first-tier subcontracts — those awarded directly by the prime contractor for the performance of the prime contract. It does not reach into the lower tiers of the supply chain, and it excludes certain general supplier arrangements that benefit multiple contracts or that are charged through general and administrative (G&A) or other indirect costs rather than booked directly to the one prime contract. The reporting question, in other words, is not "did the prime spend money with another company," but "did the prime award a first-tier subcontract, directly chargeable to this prime contract, of a kind the rule requires to be reported."

The following table illustrates how that test sorts a set of representative transactions. The right-hand column states whether each is normally reported as a first-tier subcontract.

Transaction Normally a reported first-tier subcontract?
Prime hires a company to design a subsystem for one federal contract Yes, if reporting requirements apply
Prime buys specially manufactured components for that prime contract Generally yes
Prime's major shipbuilding teammate performs 30% of the program Yes
Prime buys ordinary office supplies used across the company Generally no
First-tier subcontractor hires a second-tier subcontractor Not normally reported as a first-tier award by the prime
Prime pays its employees No

Three patterns in the table are worth naming. First, work that is specific to the one prime contract — a designed subsystem, specially manufactured components, a teammate performing a substantial share of the program — is the heart of what first-tier reporting captures. Second, general inputs that are not specific to the contract, such as ordinary office supplies charged through indirect or G&A pools, generally fall outside first-tier reporting, as does paying the prime's own employees, which is not a subcontract at all. Third, the rule is explicitly first-tier: when a first-tier subcontractor in turn hires a second-tier subcontractor, that lower-tier award is not normally reported by the prime as a first-tier award. The reach of the rule is one level down from the prime, not the full depth of the supplier chain.

The retirement of FSRS and the move to SAM.gov

The reporting just described was historically collected through the FFATA Subaward Reporting System (FSRS), the system primes used to file first-tier subaward and subcontract reports. FSRS was retired on March 8, 2025, and the reporting function moved to SAM.gov, the System for Award Management.53 The migration did not change the underlying statutory obligation under FFATA or the substance of the FAR 52.204-10 reporting test; it changed the system through which the reports are filed and surfaced. The reporting obligation also survives the broader regulatory transition described elsewhere in this reference: the Department of Defense class deviation adopting the Revolutionary FAR Overhaul, effective March 16, 2026, rewrites FAR Part 16 and related parts but does not displace the first-tier subcontract reporting implemented under FAR 52.204-10.56 The place of the resulting subaward record among the other federal data record types — and the rule that subaward values must not be commingled with prime obligations when totaling federal spending — is developed in How awards appear in federal data.

In hosting the reporting, SAM.gov draws a clearer line than the legacy system between two kinds of report:

   │
   ├── Subcontract Reports  ── associated with procurement contracts
   │                           (a first-tier subcontract under a prime contract)
   │
   └── Subaward Reports     ── associated with grants and other

A Subcontract Report is associated with a procurement contract — that is, with a first-tier subcontract under a federal prime contract. A Subaward Report is associated with grants and other financial-assistance awards. The distinction matters because it maps directly onto the two legal meanings of subaward described next.

The two meanings of "subaward"

The single word subaward is used for two different legal instruments, and keeping them apart resolves much of the confusion that surrounds the term.

In the procurement context, the chain runs from a federal prime contract to a first-tier subcontract:

Federal prime contract
   └── first-tier subcontract   (legal instrument: a subcontract)

Here the legal instrument is a subcontract, even though USAspending and the legacy transparency systems sometimes group it under the broad heading subaward. The label is a transparency-data convention; the underlying instrument is governed by the FAR subcontract definition at FAR 44.101 and reported under FAR 52.204-10. Treating the subcontractor as a direct federal contractor because the data calls its subcontract a "subaward" is precisely the error the procurement meaning is meant to prevent.

In the financial-assistance context, the chain runs from a federal grant to an assistance subaward:

Federal grant (to a prime recipient)
   └── assistance subaward      (to a subrecipient)

Here a subaward is a transfer of assistance from a prime recipient to a subrecipient. It is not a FAR procurement contract; it is a downstream flow of grant assistance, governed by the assistance framework rather than by the FAR's subcontracting rules. The instrument, the parties, and the governing rules all differ from the procurement case.

The recommended usage for the procurement meaning, which this reference adopts throughout, states the relationship plainly: a procurement subaward is a transparency-data label for a reported first-tier subcontract under a federal prime contract. Read that way, the term names a report about an instrument rather than a new federal award, and it never implies that the subcontractor holds a prime contract with the Government.

The throughline

The reliable way to read this part of the structure is to keep three legal layers and four compliance systems apart. The instrument is a subcontract, not a federal prime contract, so the subcontractor is not a direct federal contractor and the Government generally lacks privity with it. The subcontract's pricing arrangement is negotiated between prime and subcontractor and is independent of the prime's pricing type, so a fixed-price subcontract can sit beneath a cost-reimbursement prime. And the transparency label — subaward in legacy systems, now resolved into SAM.gov's Subcontract Reports for procurement and Subaward Reports for financial assistance — is a reporting convention layered on top, capturing only first-tier subcontracts and excluding lower-tier awards and indirect or general supplier costs. Around those layers sit the four compliance systems — first-tier reporting, small-business subcontracting plans, consent and purchasing-system review, and limitations on subcontracting — each answering a different question, together with the teaming, entry, and data-rights choices that determine who joins the chain and on what terms. A relationship correctly assigned to its instrument, its pricing, its reporting layer, and its compliance systems is rarely misread; the words subcontract and subaward borrowed loosely across all of them almost always are.

Small business in defense contracting

In the competition and performance-chain dimensions introduced in The dimensions of a federal contract, "small business" is not a fixed attribute that a company carries from one award to the next. A firm is small or large for a particular procurement and its assigned North American Industry Classification System (NAICS) code, so the same company can be small for one acquisition and large for the next without anything about the company changing. This chapter sets out the per-procurement size-determination sequence, reproduces the principal socioeconomic categories, explains the small-business "rule of two" and the current dollar thresholds, describes the ways small-business participation occurs on multiple-award contracts, catalogs the recurring traps (affiliation, the ostensible subcontractor, limitations on subcontracting, the nonmanufacturer rule, recertification, and the prime-versus-subcontract-credit confusion), and closes on what the phrase "small-business dominance" of a defense sector does and does not mean.

Size is determined per procurement and per NAICS

A company does not hold a single, portable "small" status. Size is assigned acquisition by acquisition through a sequence that begins with the contracting officer and ends with potential later challenge.

  1. The contracting officer assigns a NAICS code based on the principal purpose of the acquisition.
  2. The Small Business Administration (SBA) associates that NAICS code with a size standard.
  3. The standard is normally expressed as average annual receipts or number of employees.
  4. The company must generally include relevant affiliates in its size calculation.
  5. The company represents or certifies its status.
  6. Size and socioeconomic status can later be challenged or require recertification.

The measurement windows are specific. For receipts-based standards, SBA generally uses the latest five complete fiscal years; for employee-based standards, the average number of employees over the latest 24 calendar months.57 Because each NAICS code carries its own threshold, the consequences for a single firm can look contradictory until the per-procurement rule is applied. The same contractor might be small for a receipts-based consulting NAICS, large for another NAICS with a lower receipts threshold, small when it wins a parent multiple-award contract (MAC), no longer small after an acquisition several years later, or required to recertify for a particular order. None of those outcomes conflicts with the others, because each is a statement about a different procurement, NAICS code, or point in time, in the same way that the dimensional model treats an award's other attributes as independent axes.

The rule of two, the thresholds, and MAC participation modes

The principal mechanism that channels work to small businesses is the small-business rule of two. In simplified terms, above the micro-purchase threshold and through the simplified acquisition threshold, acquisitions are generally set aside for small businesses unless the contracting officer does not reasonably expect competitive offers from at least two responsible small businesses at fair-market prices, quality, and delivery. Above the simplified acquisition threshold, a total small-business set-aside is generally made when there is a reasonable expectation of at least two responsible small-business offers and award at a fair market price, under FAR 19.502-2.60

The dollar boundaries that frame the rule are dated facts that change on their own cycle. The current general thresholds, effective October 1, 2025, are a micro-purchase threshold of $15,000 and a simplified acquisition threshold of $350,000.61 These are the general figures only; there are exceptions, specialized thresholds, and different procedures for contingencies, overseas buying, humanitarian operations, and particular statutory programs.

On multiple-award contracts, small-business participation is not a single switch but one of several structural modes, several of which can be combined on one vehicle. Participation can occur through:

│
├── An entirely small-business MAC
├── A partial set-aside
├── A small-business reserve
├── Separate socioeconomic pools (e.g., 8(a), HUBZone, SDVOSB, WOSB)
├── An order-level set-aside

These modes connect directly to the ordering mechanics in Indefinite-delivery contracts: whether a small firm sees work under a MAC can depend on whether the vehicle is set aside, reserved, or pooled, and on whether a given order is itself set aside or competed unrestricted among all holders.

Small-business traps

A set of recurring structural traps determines whether a nominally small firm is in fact eligible, and whether the work it performs counts the way the parties assume. Each is a distinct rule.

Affiliation

SBA may aggregate the size of companies under common ownership, control, management, or other affiliation rules under 13 C.F.R. § 121.103, so that a nominally small entity is treated as large once its affiliates are counted.62 Affiliation is the reason size is computed at the level of a corporate family rather than a single legal entity, and it is the most common way a firm that "feels" small fails the size standard.

Ostensible subcontractor

A small prime may lose eligibility under the ostensible subcontractor rule if a non-similarly-situated subcontractor performs the primary and vital requirements of the contract or if the prime is unusually reliant on that subcontractor. The rule does not forbid using subcontractors to strengthen a proposal — SBA permits a subcontractor's past performance and experience to be cited — but it targets excessive reliance or the effective transfer of the core requirement to a firm that is not itself small.

Limitations on subcontracting

For covered set-asides, the prime generally may not pay non-similarly-situated subcontractors more than the applicable share of the amount paid by the Government, under FAR 52.219-14: 50% for services; 50% for supplies (excluding specified material costs); 85% for general construction (excluding materials); or 75% for specialty-trade construction (excluding materials).63 A subcontract to a similarly situated entity can receive more favorable treatment — generally meaning the subcontractor is itself small under the applicable NAICS and possesses the same required socioeconomic status. Limitations on subcontracting are one of the four distinct subcontracting-compliance systems distinguished in Prime–subcontractor structure, privity, and flowdowns; they constrain how much of the prime's own work may flow outward, which is a separate question from subcontracting-plan goals or first-tier transparency reporting.

Nonmanufacturer rule

For a supply set-aside, a small reseller ordinarily must provide a product manufactured by a qualifying small domestic manufacturer unless SBA has issued an applicable waiver or another exception applies — the nonmanufacturer rule.64 The rule prevents a supply set-aside from being satisfied by reselling a large or foreign manufacturer's product without an authorized exception.

Recertification

A firm's eligibility — and whether the agency may count the award toward small-business goals — can diverge from its original status after a merger, acquisition, long-term contract milestone, option, or order-level recertification request. Under current SBA rules at 13 C.F.R. § 125.12, an entity may remain eligible for some unrestricted orders while becoming ineligible for a particular set-aside order or no longer countable toward agency goals.65 Recertification is therefore the point at which eligibility and goal-credit, normally aligned, come apart; it is closely related to the option-exercise and long-term-vehicle mechanics treated in Subawards, subcontracts, and first-tier reporting and in Single-award, single-source, and sole-source.

Prime award versus subcontract credit

A large-business prime award does not become a small-business prime award merely because a portion of the work — say, 40% — is subcontracted to small firms. Prime-contract small-business dollars and small-business subcontracting dollars are separate measurements that are tallied and goaled independently. Conflating them is the error behind many claims that a particular large award "is" a small-business award; the distinction parallels the broader caution in Common "not a contract type" traps against reading one dimension's label as if it answered a different dimension's question.

What "small-business dominance" of a sector does and does not mean

Whether small businesses "dominate" a defense sector depends entirely on the denominator, and at least four different measurements are in circulation:

Four denominators for "small-business share"
│
├── (1) Percentage of award actions
├── (2) Percentage of prime-contract obligations
├── (3) Percentage of subcontract dollars
└── (4) Percentage within a particular NAICS or PSC

Small businesses do not dominate total DoD prime-contract dollars. DoD's FY2025 goals were 23.17% for prime-contracting dollars and 30% for subcontracting dollars — goals expressed as minority shares of the whole, not majorities.66

The most-cited sector figures must be read with their denominator attached. GAO found that professional, scientific, and technical services; manufacturing; and construction together accounted for 77% of all DoD obligations to small businesses in FY2020; within professional services, the major categories included architectural and engineering services, computer systems design, and scientific research and development.67 That figure does not mean small businesses received 77% of all DoD spending in those sectors. It means those sectors received 77% of the dollars DoD awarded to small businesses — a share of the small-business total, not a share of the sector. For a defined group of DoD service contracts, GAO separately found that about 27% of obligations from FY2017 through FY2022 went to small businesses.68

Read with the denominators kept straight, the practical picture is consistent. Small firms tend to have substantial opportunities in construction and installation repair; architecture and engineering; information-technology and cybersecurity support; scientific and technical services; research, development, and prototyping; training; base operations; maintenance and logistics; niche components and manufacturing; and staff augmentation and professional support. Large companies generally dominate the prime-integration role for major aircraft, ships, missiles, nuclear systems, and satellites, while small businesses frequently participate underneath those awards as suppliers and subcontractors — the performance-chain pattern described in Prime–subcontractor structure, privity, and flowdowns. GAO has likewise observed that major acquisition programs typically award to large businesses and often operate with limited competition. "Small-business dominance," then, is rarely a claim about who receives the most defense dollars in a sector; it is usually a claim about where, within the smaller pool of dollars reserved for or won by small firms, those dollars concentrate.

Post-award administration, modifications, and closeout

A contract modification is a written change to the terms of an existing contract or order. It can be tiny or enormous, funded or unfunded, administrative or substantive, and it is defined entirely by its relationship to an already-awarded instrument rather than by its dollar size (FAR 2.101).69 A modification belongs to no single dimension of an award: a given modification might add money, change time, alter pricing, revise scope, or merely correct an address, and the same instrument may be modified many times over its life. This chapter sets out what a modification is, the typical kinds, the bilateral-versus-unilateral distinction and the rule that only a contracting officer can modify the Government's contract, what is not a modification (most importantly, a new order under an indefinite-delivery indefinite-quantity (IDIQ) parent), why a modification need not add work or money, and why a modification can nonetheless be one of the largest actions in an acquisition program.

What a modification is

Under FAR 2.101 a modification is any written change in the terms of a contract. The term is deliberately broad: it spans corrections of clerical errors, the obligation or deobligation of funds, changes to quantity or price, revisions to the statement of work, and partial or complete terminations. Because the category is defined by what it does to an existing instrument and not by magnitude, two modifications that look nothing alike — a one-line correction to a paying-office address and a multibillion-dollar addition of production work — are the same kind of contract action in the federal data systems. That breadth is the root of much misreading of contract announcements and spending data, a point developed in How awards appear in federal data.

A modification operates on an existing award, so it inherits and is bounded by that award's other dimensions. It must stay within the instrument's stated scope, fall within any ceiling, be timely, be funded from a legally available appropriation, and be executed by an official with authority. Being within scope is necessary but never sufficient to make a modification lawful — the same validity test that governs every contract action, set out in Period of performance and scope.

Typical kinds of modification

Modifications recur in a recognizable set of forms. A single modification may combine several of these at once:

  • add or remove funding (obligate additional money, or deobligate unused funds);
  • exercise an option, the mechanics of which are described in Options and award terms;
  • change quantity (increase or decrease the units ordered);
  • change price or estimated cost;
  • add, delete, or revise a contract line-item number (CLIN), the structural units treated in CLINs, SLINs, and funding traceability;
  • change a delivery date or period of performance;
  • revise the statement of work;
  • direct a change under the contract's Changes clause;
  • negotiate an equitable adjustment following a Government-directed change;
  • change accounting or payment information;
  • terminate some or all of the contract (for convenience or for default); and
  • definitize an initially unpriced action, converting it to firm terms.

Each of these touches a different axis of the underlying award — funding, time, pricing, scope, or the line-item structure — which is why the bare label "modification" conveys almost nothing about what actually changed.

Bilateral versus unilateral

A modification can be bilateral or unilateral, and the difference lies in who signs it (FAR Subpart 43.1).70

A bilateral modification — also called a supplemental agreement — is signed by both the contracting officer and the contractor. It is used where the change requires the contractor's assent, typically to reflect a negotiated agreement: definitizing an unpriced action, negotiating an equitable adjustment, or otherwise altering terms the contractor is not already obligated to accept.

A unilateral modification is signed only by the contracting officer. It is used where the Government may act on its own under authority the contract already grants — to exercise an option, make an administrative change (such as correcting a paying-office address), issue an authorized change order under the Changes clause, or give a termination notice. The Government can act unilaterally in these cases precisely because the underlying right or authority was fixed when the contract was awarded; the unilateral exercise of an option, for instance, works because the option's terms were established at award, as explained in Options and award terms.

Only a contracting officer can modify the contract

Whether a modification is bilateral or unilateral, the Government's signature must come from a warranted contracting officer. Only an authorized contracting officer can modify the Government's contract (FAR 43.102).71 A program manager, a contracting officer's representative (COR), an engineer, or any other government employee cannot legally change the contract merely by sending instructions, expressing a preference, or directing the contractor in the field. Apparent direction from someone without contracting authority does not bind the Government and can expose the contractor to performing uncompensated or unauthorized work; the limits of who holds that authority are the subject of Contracting authority and who makes the award. The Revolutionary FAR Overhaul rewrite of the modification rules retains this core allocation of authority to the contracting officer.72

What is not a modification

Not every change in the contracting relationship is a modification of an existing instrument. The most important case is the new order under an IDIQ. A new task order or delivery order placed against an IDIQ parent is generally a new order under the parent, not a modification to the parent IDIQ. FAR Part 43 expressly distinguishes orders that do not otherwise change the parent contract's terms from modifications. Placing an order draws on the ordering authority the parent already established (the indefinite-delivery framework is described in Indefinite-delivery contracts); it does not rewrite the parent's terms.

The distinction matters because each order, once issued, is itself a contract action that can be modified on its own. A modification to a task order changes that order — not necessarily the parent IDIQ:

Parent IDIQ contract
│   (placing an order is NOT a modification of the parent)
│
├── Task Order 0001
│      ├── Modification 01 — add funding      ← modifies the ORDER
│      └── Modification 02 — extend schedule  ← modifies the ORDER
│
└── Task Order 0002

Here, Modification 01 and Modification 02 change Task Order 0001; they do not necessarily change the parent IDIQ. Keeping the layers separate — order versus parent — is essential to reading the action correctly, and it parallels the parent-level-versus-order-level distinction drawn for options in Options and award terms and for the core terms in The core vocabulary.

Why a modification need not add work or money

A modification does not inherently mean "more work," and it does not even necessarily mean more money. The category includes actions that remove money, add nothing of substance, or change only an administrative detail. The following table shows how loosely "modification" correlates with either adding funds or adding work:

Modification Adds money? Adds work?
Correct the paying-office address No No
Deobligate unused funds Reduces money No
Add the next increment of funding for already ordered cost-type work Yes No
Exercise an option Yes, usually Yes
Pay an equitable adjustment for an earlier Government change Yes Not necessarily
Add a new deliverable Usually Yes
Extend schedule due to contractor delay Maybe not No
Add another year of recurring services Usually Yes

Two rows reward attention. Adding the next increment of funding for cost-type work that was already ordered puts money on the contract without adding any new work — the work was already authorized, and the modification merely obligates the next tranche against it, a pattern tied to incremental funding and traced in Awards, ceilings, obligations, and spending. Conversely, paying an equitable adjustment adds money to compensate the contractor for an earlier Government-directed change, not necessarily to buy additional deliverables. The key question is therefore not just whether money was added; it is which contractual obligation was changed or created.

A modification can be enormous

Because a modification is defined by its relationship to an existing contract and not by its dollar value, a single modification can represent most of a major acquisition program. The clearest example is the Navy's 2019 Virginia-class Block V action: a roughly $22.2 billion fixed-price-incentive, multiyear modification to an existing contract that added construction of nine submarines and included an option for a tenth (DoD daily "Contracts" announcement, December 2, 2019).73 In structural terms this single action exercised the pricing dimension (fixed-price-incentive), the funding-and-commitment dimension (multiyear procurement), and the options dimension at the same time, while being recorded as a modification.

This is precisely why procurement databases can mislead. An action labeled "modification" carries no implication that it is small or routine; economically it may be the bulk of a flagship program. A reader who treats "modification" as a synonym for a minor adjustment — or who tries to reconstruct program spending without distinguishing the substance of each modification — will badly misjudge where the money is. As with the ambiguity of "award amount," the safe practice is to look past the label to the obligations actually recorded, the discipline developed in How awards appear in federal data.

Post-award kickoff and the administration phase

Award begins the work; it does not end it. A signed contract or order marks the point at which performance, billing, oversight, and eventual closeout commence, and modifications are only one of the instruments that operate during the long administration phase that follows. It is common, soon after award, for the parties to hold a post-award orientation — often a kickoff conference — to align expectations, walk through the statement of work and deliverables, confirm reporting and invoicing mechanics, and identify the officials who will administer the contract on each side. FAR Subpart 42.5 treats post-award orientation as a tool for reaching a clear and mutual understanding of contract requirements and for resolving potential problems before they arise; it is a means of administration, not a renegotiation, and it changes nothing in the contract.74 From that point the contract is administered against its terms: the Government monitors performance, inspects and accepts work, pays invoices, records contractor performance, processes any changes through the contracting officer, and ultimately closes the file. The sections that follow trace that arc, with modifications — already treated above — sitting at its center.

The administering roles

Contract administration is carried out by a defined set of roles whose authority is deliberately uneven. The central distinction is between officials who can legally bind or change the Government's contract and those who cannot, and that distinction is the post-award expression of the same authority rule developed for the award itself in Contracting authority and who makes the award.

  • The procuring contracting officer (PCO) is the warranted contracting officer who awards the contract and ordinarily retains authority over its substantive terms, including modifications, unless that authority is delegated.
  • The administrative contracting officer (ACO) is a warranted contracting officer to whom contract administration functions are assigned, often at a different office than the one that awarded the contract; the ACO performs the administration functions enumerated at FAR 42.302 and can bind the Government within the scope of that assignment.
  • The contracting officer's representative (COR) is a Government employee designated in writing by the contracting officer to perform specified technical or administrative monitoring functions. A COR's authority is limited to what the written designation grants and, under FAR 1.604, does not include authority to make any commitment or change that affects price, quality, quantity, delivery, or other terms of the contract.75
  • The Defense Contract Management Agency (DCMA) is the DoD component that performs contract administration services on many DoD contracts; its personnel include ACOs and supporting specialists who administer contracts delegated to the agency.
  • The program office owns the requirement and the mission outcome and is the source of much technical direction, but program officials are not contracting officers and cannot, by virtue of program authority, change the contract.

The allocation is summarized below. The decisive column is the last one.

Contract-administration roles and authority
Role          | Primary function                                  | Can change the contract?
PCO           | Awards the contract; retains substantive terms    | Yes — warranted CO
ACO           | Performs assigned administration functions        | Yes — within the delegated scope
COR           | Monitors technical/administrative performance     | No — designation excludes term changes
DCMA          | Provides delegated contract-administration svcs   | Through its warranted COs only
Program office| Owns the requirement and mission outcome          | No — not a contracting officer

The governing rule is unchanged from the award context: a contract action that binds or changes the Government must be executed by a contracting officer acting within the scope of a warrant (FAR 1.602), the administration functions are organized under FAR 42.1 and following, and the COR's limited delegated authority is fixed by FAR 1.604.75 A COR, an engineer, or a program manager who issues direction outside that framework cannot change the contract, however senior — the reason informal direction is a recurring source of disputes, taken up below.

Deliverables and CDRLs

What a contract requires the contractor to furnish divides into two broad classes that are administered differently. Deliverable supplies and services are the end items and performance the contract buys — the hardware, the software, the maintenance, the studies. Data deliverables are the documents and data the contractor must submit about that work — drawings, test reports, manuals, status reports, and similar items. In DoD contracts the data deliverables are specified on a Contract Data Requirements List (CDRL), recorded on DD Form 1423, with each CDRL line item typically tied to an authorizing data item description (DID) that defines the content and format of the required submission.76 A CDRL line item generally references the work it documents, frequently a line item of the kind described in CLINs, SLINs, and funding traceability, and a technical-data line item may itself be carried as not-separately-priced. Distinguishing the supply or service being bought from the data being delivered about it matters because acceptance, payment, and rights attach differently to each, and because a contract can be fully performed in its hardware while data deliverables remain outstanding.

Inspection and acceptance

Before the Government pays for and takes responsibility for what a contract produces, it ordinarily inspects the supplies or services and then formally accepts them. FAR Part 46 establishes the quality-assurance framework: inspection determines whether the deliverable conforms to contract requirements, and acceptance is the Government's act of assuming ownership of, or approving, conforming supplies or services.77 Acceptance carries legal consequences and is not a mere formality. Depending on the contract terms, acceptance can mark the passage of title to the Government, start a warranty period, and — subject to the contract's inspection clause and exceptions such as latent defects, fraud, or gross mistakes amounting to fraud — be conclusive, limiting the Government's later ability to reject the item. Because acceptance fixes these consequences, the question of when and by whom acceptance occurs is itself an administration question, and the authority to accept, like other binding acts, runs to the contracting officer or a duly authorized representative rather than to any official who happens to receive the work.

Invoicing and payment

A contractor is paid by submitting invoices against the contract, and for DoD that submission is predominantly electronic. Invoices and receiving reports are processed through Wide Area Workflow (WAWF), the invoicing and acceptance module of the Procurement Integrated Enterprise Environment (PIEE), which routes a submission among the contractor, the acceptor, and the paying office.78 Electronic submission is not merely a convenience: DFARS 252.232-7003 requires contractors to submit payment requests and receiving reports in electronic form, ordinarily through WAWF, with narrow exceptions.78 Payment timing is governed by the Prompt Payment Act, under which the Government must pay proper invoices within the applicable period — generally 30 days after receipt of a proper invoice — or owe interest, with the FAR's Prompt Payment clauses implementing the rule. The mechanics of which line item and which line of accounting an invoice draws against are the funding-traceability subject of CLINs, SLINs, and funding traceability: an invoice is paid against specific CLINs and the accounting data attached to them, which is why a clean line-item and ACRN structure is what makes orderly payment possible.

Contractor performance information (CPARS)

The Government keeps an official record of how contractors perform, and that record is not public. The Contractor Performance Assessment Reporting System (CPARS) is the governmentwide system in which agencies prepare past-performance evaluations on contracts and orders; under FAR Subpart 42.15 these evaluations assess contractor performance and are used to inform future source-selection decisions.79 CPARS information is controlled and source-selection sensitive: completed evaluations feed the past-performance records used in later competitions, and access is restricted to authorized Government and contractor users, with the assessed contractor able to review and comment on its own report but the file as a whole shielded from public disclosure. A public analyst therefore cannot see a contractor's full CPARS file; the official performance record sits behind an access wall even though the underlying awards are visible in public data. This asymmetry is why past performance functions as a signal in Recompetes and opportunity intelligence without ever being fully observable from outside, and it is the administration-phase counterpart to the responsibility and past-performance judgments that drive the award decisions described in Contracting authority and who makes the award.

Constructive changes and unauthorized direction

Because only a contracting officer can change the contract, direction from anyone else is a recurring source of trouble. A constructive change arises when the contractor is effectively required to perform work beyond the contract's requirements — not through a formal modification, but through informal direction, an interpretation imposed by a Government official, or conduct that has the practical effect of altering the work — without a proper change order having been issued. Direction from a COR, an inspector, an engineer, or a program official who lacks contracting authority can create exactly this situation: the work changes in fact, but no authorized instrument records it. The doctrine cuts in two directions at once. It can entitle the contractor to relief, because the Government received changed performance, while also confirming that the informal direction was unauthorized and that the only proper way to direct a change is through the contracting officer under the contract's Changes clause. The defensive posture follows directly: a contractor that receives change-like direction from someone other than the contracting officer ordinarily seeks written confirmation from the contracting officer before treating the contract as changed, because apparent direction that does not come from a warranted official does not bind the Government — the same authority limit drawn in Contracting authority and who makes the award.

Claims and equitable adjustments

When a change — formal or constructive — affects the cost or time of performance, the contractor's remedy is monetary and runs through a defined sequence. A request for equitable adjustment (REA) is a request that the contract price or schedule be adjusted to account for the effect of a Government-directed or constructive change, typically grounded in the contract's Changes clause (FAR 52.243).80 An REA that the parties cannot resolve by negotiation may be converted into, or asserted as, a claim under the Contract Disputes Act, which the contractor submits to the contracting officer for a written contracting officer's final decision; claims above the statutory certification threshold must be certified.80 The contracting officer's final decision is the pivot of the FAR Part 33 disputes process, and it is appealable — to an agency board of contract appeals or to the U.S. Court of Federal Claims.80 The distinction between an REA and a claim is consequential: a claim triggers Contract Disputes Act mechanics, including the contracting officer's decision deadline, the running of interest from receipt of a proper claim, and the appeal route, whereas an REA is a negotiation posture that has not yet entered the formal dispute track. Either way the action is resolved by the contracting officer, not by the program office or the COR, which is why a constructive change asserted against informal direction must still be reduced to a request directed at a warranted official.

Termination and closeout

A contract may end before performance is complete, and it always ends with an administrative wind-down. The Government may terminate for convenience when it is in the Government's interest, settling with the contractor for work performed and allowable costs rather than for breach, or terminate for default (for cause) when the contractor fails to perform; the two are governed by FAR Part 49, and they differ sharply in their financial and reputational consequences for the contractor.81 A termination is itself executed by the contracting officer, ordinarily as a modification of the kind catalogued above. Whether a contract runs to full performance or is terminated, it then enters closeout: under FAR 4.804 the contracting office verifies that the contract is physically complete, that final payments and any deobligations have been made, that required reports and clearances are in place, and that the file can be administratively closed.81 Closeout is the formal end of the administration phase, and it can lag performance by months or years — final invoices, deobligation of unspent balances, indirect-cost rate settlements, and audits all occur after the work itself is finished. That lag is the precise mechanism behind the administrative-activity trap described in Recompetes and opportunity intelligence: a deobligation, a final invoice, or a closeout audit appearing in the data long after performance ended is not evidence of live work, but the ordinary tail of administration and closeout playing out on a contract whose substantive effort is already over.

CLINs, funding traceability, and program transition

A contract line item number (CLIN) is the structural unit that organizes what a contract or order buys, and the line-item structure is also where the contractual work most often meets the appropriation that pays for it. This chapter describes what a CLIN organizes, the distinction between a CLIN and its subline items (SLINs), how the Accounting Classification Reference Number (ACRN) connects a line item to a line of accounting, how line items behave on an indefinite-delivery contract, and how far a single obligated dollar can be traced from public award data toward a Department of Defense (DoD) Program Element or budget line. Line items belong to no single one of the dimensions set out in The dimensions of a federal contract; they are the level at which several dimensions — pricing, time, delivery, and above all funding — are recorded together for one piece of the work.

What a CLIN is and what it organizes

A CLIN is a separately identified portion of a contract or order used to define what is being purchased and to organize its associated terms. A single line item collects, for that portion of the work, its description; quantity; price or estimated cost; fee; pricing type; funding; delivery schedule; period of performance; place of performance; inspection and acceptance; and payment terms. A CLIN is not a separate contract; it is a structural unit inside a contract or order, and the contract or order as a whole remains the legal instrument.

A worked example shows how one contract can hold several different kinds of work at once:

One shipbuilding contract
│
├── CLIN 0001 — Submarine detailed design
│       Pricing: Cost-plus-fixed-fee
│       Quantity: 1 lot
│       POP: Oct. 1, 2026 – Sep. 30, 2028
│       Funding: RDT&E, Navy
│
├── CLIN 0002 — Long-lead propulsion material
│       Pricing: Firm-fixed-price
│       Quantity: 2 sets
│       Delivery: June 30, 2029
│       Funding: Shipbuilding and Conversion, Navy
│
└── CLIN 0003 — Technical data
        Pricing: Not separately priced
        Delivery: per CDRL

Because each line item carries its own pricing arrangement, a single contract can be a hybrid contract: a firm-fixed-price CLIN, a cost-reimbursement CLIN, a time-and-materials CLIN, and a not-separately-priced data CLIN can all coexist in the same contract. This is the line-item mechanism behind the rule, developed in Pricing types and cost risk, that a vehicle and its pricing arrangement are independent: the contract is one instrument, but its CLINs can sit on different points of the cost-risk spectrum.

The FAR requires line items to identify, among other things, what is being purchased, the product or service code (PSC), the accounting classification, the quantity, pricing information, and the applicable delivery schedule or period of performance; it also requires the pricing type to be identified at the line-item level when a contract mixes pricing arrangements.82 These required data elements are what make the CLIN, rather than the contract as a whole, the natural unit at which to read what a contract actually does.

CLIN versus SLIN

A SLIN is a subline item underneath a CLIN, used to break a single line item into smaller administrative pieces without creating a new CLIN. DoD normally constructs CLINs from four numeric characters — 0001, 0002, 0003 — and forms SLINs by extending the parent CLIN number. The form of the extension signals what kind of subline item it is:

Subline item type Numbering form Example
CLIN (base line item) four numeric characters 0001, 0002, 0003
Separately deliverable SLIN parent CLIN + two letters 0001AA, 0001AB, 0001AC
Informational SLIN parent CLIN + two numeric characters 000101, 000102, 000103

DoD uses SLINs to separate different deliveries, destinations, funding sources, performance periods, or other administrative characteristics within one line item.83 The distinction between the two SLIN forms matters: a separately deliverable SLIN (the alphabetic form) identifies a separately deliverable or separately priced piece of the line item, whereas an informational SLIN (the numeric form) subdivides the line item for accounting or administrative purposes without changing what is delivered.

Two examples show the typical uses. A line item can be split across time:

CLIN 0001 — Engineering support
├── SLIN 0001AA — Base year
├── SLIN 0001AB — Option year 1
└── SLIN 0001AC — Option year 2

Or a single, integrated effort can be split only for funding, while remaining one deliverable:

CLIN 0002 — One integrated development effort
├── SLIN 000201 — Army funding (informational)
├── SLIN 000202 — Navy funding (informational)
└── SLIN 000203 — Air Force funding (informational)

In the second case the work is not divided — there is one development effort — but the funds come from three sources, so informational SLINs record the split without implying three separate deliverables.

CLINs, SLINs, and the ACRN: connecting work to a line of accounting

The CLIN or SLIN is frequently the point at which an award's contractual work intersects with the line of accounting that funds it. In DoD instruments this connection is made through an ACRN, a two-character code that ties a CLIN or SLIN to one or more accounting citations; the system is intended to permit traceability from funding to contract actions.84 An ACRN functions as a short pointer: rather than repeating a full accounting classification beside every line item, the instrument assigns each line of accounting an ACRN and then references that code where the funds are applied.

A simple mapping illustrates the pointer:

CLIN 0001 → ACRN AA → FY2026 RDT&E, Navy
CLIN 0002 → ACRN AB → FY2025 Shipbuilding and Conversion, Navy
CLIN 0003 → ACRN AC → FY2026 O&M, Navy

Public award data may expose only the high-level account behind such a mapping; the signed contract schedule and the ACRN data provide the more granular link from a specific line item to a specific appropriation, fiscal year, and account. This is the same obligation-tracing problem framed in Awards, ceilings, obligations, and spending: the obligation that matters lives on a line item, and the ACRN is what attaches that line item to the appropriation it draws on.

CLINs on an IDIQ

On an indefinite-delivery indefinite-quantity (IDIQ) contract, line items appear at two levels, and the two should not be confused. At the parent indefinite-delivery vehicle (IDV) level, CLINs may describe only broad categories of work — for example, CLIN 0001 systems engineering services, CLIN 0002 software development, and CLIN 0003 travel and other direct costs — without committing specific quantities, prices, or funding. The actual work is specified on the child order, whose order CLINs state the real quantity, price, funding, delivery schedule, and period of performance for that order.

The relationship between the two levels is now explicit in regulation: under the rewritten FAR Part 16 that DoD applies through the current class deviation, an order must include both its own line-item information and the corresponding line item from the parent contract.85 An order CLIN therefore carries the order's own terms while remaining traceable to the parent category it draws from. This is consistent with the broader treatment of indefinite-delivery vehicles in Indefinite-delivery contracts (FAR 16.5): the parent vehicle is a framework, and the obligations that fund real work are recorded on the orders placed under it, line item by line item.

Can an award be tied to a Program Element or budget line?

There is usually no standardized public contract-award field that proves a one-to-one connection between an award and a DoD Program Element or a P-1 budget line item. The connection can sometimes be established from the underlying documents, but it is rarely available as a clean field in public data, and the reason is structural: budget documents and contract data are built on different hierarchies for different purposes.

The DoD budget-exhibit families

DoD publishes its budget justification in several exhibit families, one per appropriation area, because the appropriation areas are not arranged as a single universal hierarchy.

Appropriation area Common public budget exhibit
Research, development, test, and evaluation (RDT&E) R-1 Program Element, with more detailed R-2 / R-2A material
Procurement P-1 procurement line item and supporting exhibits
Operation and Maintenance O-1
Military Construction C-1

These are published as separate budget-document families precisely because they do not form one common structure.86 An RDT&E effort is identified by a Program Element on an R-1; a procurement buy is identified by a line item on a P-1; the two numbering systems do not interoperate, and neither maps cleanly onto a contract's CLINs.

What a Treasury Account Symbol does and does not identify

A Treasury Account Symbol (TAS) identifies the high-level federal account: the agency responsible for the account, the appropriation's period of availability, and the particular fund or account classification.87 A TAS does not ordinarily identify a specific R-1 Program Element, a P-1 line item, an R-2 project, an O-1 subactivity group, a weapon-system lot, a contract CLIN, or an internal cost center. The granularity gap is large: one TAS may contain dozens or hundreds of program elements, budget lines, activities, and projects. Knowing the TAS therefore locates the appropriation but not the program within it.

What USAspending can expose

At the award or transaction level, USAspending can expose the federal account or accounts; transaction-level obligation amounts; the awarding agency and office; the funding agency and office; award and order identifiers; action dates; program activities; object classes; and other award attributes. The USAspending API specifically supports award-account and award-funding queries that return federal accounts, funding and awarding organizations, and transaction obligations.88 However, a DATA Act program activity is not automatically the same thing as a DoD R-1 Program Element or P-1 line item; they are separate reporting structures, and although the names may occasionally align or permit a strong inference, the alignment is not guaranteed. The data systems behind these fields are described further in How awards appear in federal data.

Why the mapping is difficult

The mapping from an award to a budget line resists a simple text match for several compounding reasons. A single order can contain several CLINs; several fiscal years of funds; multiple appropriations and multiple TAS values; several internal lines of accounting; both RDT&E and procurement funding; funding from more than one component or agency; and subsequent deobligations or upward adjustments. The parent IDIQ may carry no meaningful program-level funding at all, with the actual funds appearing only on child orders and their modifications. More fundamentally, budget documents describe formulation and congressional justification, while contract data describe execution; reprogrammings, transfers, working-capital-fund purchases, interagency acquisitions, and changing execution plans can all weaken a simple text match between a budget exhibit and an award. The same execution-versus-plan gap appears in Multiyear procurement and major weapon production, where advance procurement and economic-order-quantity buys spread one program's funding across appropriations and years.

The document that can close the gap

The instrument that can close the gap is a signed task order or modification, which should contain accounting and appropriation data organized through an ACRN. In DoD instruments the ACRN is effectively a pointer to a full line of accounting that may include the appropriation and fiscal year; the program or budget activity; the Program Element or procurement line; the object class; the organization or cost center; the project or subproject; the funding document number; and other service-specific accounting fields. The public version of such a document may redact or omit portions, but the unredacted award file and the accounting system normally contain a more exact funding trail. The modification dimension of this is treated in Contract modifications: because obligations and their accounting data are frequently added by modification, the funding trail of a mature award lives across its modifications, not only in the original signed instrument.

Tracing where the money came from

Tracing a dollar should begin from the individual contract action or order, not merely from the parent IDV, because the obligation and its accounting data live on the action. The public-data workflow proceeds in nine steps:

  1. Identify the prime contract procurement instrument identifier (PIID) and, when applicable, the task or delivery order number.
  2. Separate the original award from each modification or transaction.
  3. Record the federal action obligation for each transaction.
  4. Retrieve the funding agency, funding office, federal account, and TAS.
  5. Determine the appropriation family and fiscal-year availability.
  6. Review the associated program activity, award description, PSC, NAICS code, and funding office.
  7. Match that information against the appropriate R-1, P-1, O-1, C-1, or other budget exhibit.
  8. Obtain the signed order and funding modifications to find the ACRNs and lines of accounting.
  9. Use the purchase request, Military Interdepartmental Purchase Request (MIPR), funds-certification document, or internal accounting crosswalk when an exact Program Element or budget-line match is required.

The steps are ordered by increasing difficulty and decreasing public availability: the early steps draw on public transaction data, while the later steps require the signed award file or the accounting system.

Confidence levels of funding conclusions

How firmly a funding conclusion can be stated depends on which of these steps it relies on. The following table pairs typical conclusions with the confidence they can be asserted at from public data.

Interagency acquisitions require special care, because the funding agency may provide the money while a different awarding agency conducts the procurement. In that case the awarding office named on the action is not the source of the funds, and the funding agency, funding office, and federal account fields — not the awarding fields — identify where the money came from. This is one more reason the obligation transaction, and ultimately the ACRN on the signed instrument, is the reliable unit for tracing a dollar, consistent with the question of who actually holds authority over an action treated in Contracting authority and who makes the award.

Purpose, time, and amount

Tracing a dollar to its line of accounting answers where funds came from; it does not by itself answer whether those funds could lawfully be applied to the work in question. That second question is governed by federal fiscal law, which rests on three statutory pillars. An appropriation may be used only for its authorized purpose: the purpose statute provides that appropriations are to be applied only to the objects for which they were made, except as otherwise provided by law (31 U.S.C. 1301(a)). It may be obligated only within its period of availability — its time: an annual appropriation is generally available for obligation only during the fiscal year for which it was enacted, and a multiyear appropriation only across the years specified. And it may be obligated and expended only up to the amount appropriated: the Antideficiency Act prohibits obligations or expenditures in excess of, or in advance of, available appropriations (31 U.S.C. 1341), and a violation carries administrative and potential penal consequences for the responsible official. The authoritative treatment of these doctrines is GAO's Principles of Federal Appropriations Law — the "Red Book" — which is the standard reference an analyst consults when an obligation's legality, rather than merely its accounting, is at issue.89 These three constraints explain a recurring puzzle in awards data: a program can have an appropriation in hand and still be unable to spend it on a given task, because the funds are the wrong purpose, the wrong year, or already exhausted.

The bona fide need rule

The time constraint has a sharp corollary in the bona fide need rule: a fiscal-year appropriation may be obligated only to meet a legitimate — bona fide — need arising in, or continuing through, the appropriation's period of availability (31 U.S.C. 1502(a)).90 An agency may not use a current year's funds to buy goods or services for a need that does not arise until a later year, nor (absent specific authority) reach back to satisfy a current need with a prior year's expired balances. The rule is what prevents an appropriation's period of availability from being circumvented by buying ahead or banking funds, and it is the doctrine behind many apparent timing anomalies in obligation data: an action's fiscal-year funding must correspond to the year in which the need genuinely exists. For supplies, the need is generally tied to when the items are required; for services, the analysis turns on whether the work is severable or nonseverable, treated next.

Severable versus nonseverable work

Whether a single year's appropriation can fund work that spans fiscal years depends on whether the work is severable or nonseverable. Severable services are continuing and recurring in nature — the kind of effort that produces value period by period, such as routine maintenance, guard services, or ongoing technical support — and each increment is useful on its own. Nonseverable work produces a single end product or accomplishes one whole task — a study delivered as a finished report, a prototype, a discrete construction project — whose value is realized only on completion. The distinction controls funding. Nonseverable work must be fully funded in the appropriation current at the time of award, because the entire undertaking represents a single bona fide need of that year. Severable services, by contrast, may within limits cross fiscal years on a single year's appropriation: a severable-services contract or order may be funded from an appropriation current at the time of award even though performance extends into the next fiscal year, provided the total period does not exceed twelve months (10 U.S.C. 3133).91 This is why two superficially similar service awards can carry very different funding patterns — one incrementally funded across years, the other obligated in full at award — depending entirely on which side of the severability line the work falls.

Expired versus cancelled funds

An appropriation's period of availability marks the end of its life for new commitments, not its disappearance. After the period of availability closes, an appropriation expires: it is no longer available to incur new obligations, but for five additional fiscal years it remains available to record, adjust, and liquidate existing obligations — for example, to pay invoices on already-obligated work or to make permissible upward adjustments to prior obligations (31 U.S.C. 1553). At the end of that five-year period the account is cancelled (closed): the balance is withdrawn, and the appropriation is thereafter unavailable for any purpose, whether new obligations or adjustments to old ones (31 U.S.C. 1552).92 The practical consequence for awards analysis is that the apparent state of a years-old contract depends on this clock. A modification that adjusts an obligation against an expired-but-not-cancelled appropriation is permissible; the same adjustment against a cancelled appropriation is not and must instead be charged to current funds, subject to statutory limits. The funding trail of a mature award, which lives across its modifications as Contract modifications develops, therefore cannot be read without knowing where each appropriation sits on the expired-to-cancelled timeline.

Continuing resolutions

The three pillars assume that full-year appropriations exist. When they do not — when a fiscal year begins before Congress has enacted some or all of the regular appropriations acts — agencies operate under a continuing resolution (CR), a temporary appropriation that maintains funding for a defined interval. A CR characteristically funds activities at or near the prior year's rate and carries restrictions that bear directly on acquisition: it generally bars new-start programs (efforts not funded in the prior year), limits production-rate increases beyond the prior year's level, and constrains actions whose execution depends on appropriations not yet enacted. The effect is that an action which is otherwise ready to award — a new program's first contract, or a rate increase on an existing one — may have to wait until full-year appropriations are enacted, even though the requirement is validated and the contractor is available. CR-driven delay is thus a distinct, calendar-driven reason a funded-in-concept action does not appear in obligation data when expected, separate from any deficiency in the requirement itself.

Color of money and program transition

Defense appropriations are not fungible: they come in distinct "colors," each a separate account with its own purpose and period of availability, and the purpose statute makes those boundaries binding. The principal colors are Research, Development, Test & Evaluation (RDT&E) — the Navy's, for instance, carried in the "3600" appropriation — used to develop and demonstrate capability; Procurement, used to buy production quantities of fielded systems; and Operation & Maintenance (O&M) — the "3400" appropriation — used to operate and sustain them. Because each color may be applied only to its own purpose, prototype or RDT&E funding does not automatically pay for production. Moving a capability from development into fielding requires a deliberate transition onto procurement or O&M money of the right year — a step that is budgetary, not merely technical or contractual.

This is the gap between an endorsed requirement and a fundable one. A program can have enthusiastic operational users, a successful prototype, and a validated need, and still lack executable funding, because no appropriation of the right color and the right year is available to pay for the next phase. The endorsement establishes that the work is wanted; only an appropriation of the correct color and availability makes it lawful to obligate for that work. The transition decision — and the budget cycle that must produce the procurement or O&M line to support it — is where many otherwise-successful efforts stall:

Single-award, single-source, and sole-source

Three terms that sound interchangeable describe entirely different things, and confusing them is one of the most common errors in reading defense contract awards. The terms single-award and multiple-award describe the number of holders a vehicle has; the term sole-source describes the absence of competition. These are independent dimensions in the sense developed in The dimensions of a federal contract: the award-structure dimension answers "how many companies hold the vehicle," while the competition dimension answers "who was allowed to compete." Because the two questions are orthogonal, a one-holder vehicle can be the product of vigorous competition, and a fully competed multiple-award vehicle can still produce an order directed to a single holder. This chapter separates the concepts, sets out the four combinations they generate, recommends a disciplined vocabulary, and states the two regulatory rules that attach to single-award and noncompetitive actions: the preference for multiple awards and the written determination required for a single-award task- or delivery-order vehicle over $150 million.

Two independent dimensions

The whole subject reduces to keeping two axes apart. The award-structure axis counts holders: a single-award IDIQ is held by one prime, while a multiple-award IDIQ is held by several primes who hold parallel contracts for the same scope, as developed in Indefinite-delivery contracts. The competition axis describes how the award was reached: a competed award results from full and open competition (or competition within a set-aside pool), while a sole-source award is negotiated with one contractor without full and open competition, under a statutory authority or authorized exception. Neither axis determines the other. The number of holders a vehicle ends up with says nothing about whether the public was invited to compete for it, and the presence or absence of competition says nothing about how many holders eventually result.

A further term, single-source, sits awkwardly between the two and is best avoided. The FAR sometimes uses "single source" when discussing large single-award vehicles, but the phrase reads as though it were a synonym for "sole-source," which it is not. Because single-source invites exactly the collapse this chapter warns against, the unambiguous label for a one-holder vehicle is single-award IDIQ.

Once the two axes are held apart, four cases follow, and each has a different correct description. The structured illustrations below show how the same word — "one winner," "several winners" — can sit on either side of the competition line.

Recommended terminology reservations

Because each of these words belongs to a single dimension, the clearest usage reserves each term for the question it actually answers. Four reservations keep the vocabulary disciplined:

  • Single-award and multiple-award are reserved for the number of vehicle holders — an award-structure fact.
  • Sole-source is reserved for the absence of competition — a competition fact, equally applicable to a whole vehicle or to a single order placed on a sole-source basis.
  • IDIQ is reserved for the delivery and ordering structure — the instrument, as set out in IDV, IDIQ, MAC, GWAC, and Schedules.
  • FFP, FPI, CPFF, and the other pricing labels are reserved for the pricing arrangement — how the contractor is paid and who bears cost risk, the subject of Pricing types and cost risk.

Held to these reservations, a description such as "a competed single-award firm-fixed-price IDIQ" parses cleanly: competed names the competition, single-award the holder count, firm-fixed-price the pricing, and IDIQ the instrument, with no term doing two jobs. The term single-source is set aside entirely, because its resemblance to "sole-source" makes it ambiguous; single-award IDIQ says the same thing without inviting the error. The same discipline underlies the warning in Common "not a contract type" traps that "sole source" names a competition posture rather than a kind of contract.

The multiple-award preference and the $150 million determination

Two regulatory rules attach specifically to these distinctions, and both belong to the competition and award-structure dimensions rather than to the instrument or pricing dimensions.

First, the current FAR Part 16, as rewritten under the Revolutionary FAR Overhaul, prefers multiple awards when making multiple awards is practicable.95 The preference is a default toward distributing a vehicle among several competing holders rather than concentrating it in one; it operates on the award-structure axis and is independent of how any individual order is later priced or competed.

Second, a task- or delivery-order contract over $150 million — including all options — that is awarded to a single source requires a specific written determination before award.96 This determination is a creature of the award-structure dimension: it is triggered by the choice to use one holder for a high-value ordering vehicle, not by any lack of competition in reaching that holder. It is therefore separate from, and additional to, any FAR Part 6 justification that a noncompetitive award would independently require. A single-award vehicle over the threshold needs the written determination even if it was fully and openly competed; a sole-source vehicle below the threshold needs the FAR Part 6 justification but not this determination; and a high-value sole-source single-award vehicle needs both. As of the article date, June 25, 2026, DoD contracting officers apply the overhauled Part 16 — including the multiple-award preference and the single-source determination requirement — under a class deviation effective March 16, 2026.97

The FAR Part 6 basis for a noncompetitive award

When an award is in fact made without full and open competition, the competition dimension requires its own justification under FAR Part 6, which is distinct from the award-structure determination just described. The most common basis is that only one responsible source — or, for the Department of Defense under a class deviation, one or a limited number of responsible sources — can satisfy the agency's requirement, the authority at FAR 6.302-1.98 This basis explains many sole-source actions in defense, where the practical impossibility of qualifying an additional source quickly, or a deliberately limited industrial base, leaves one or a few firms able to perform; the Virginia-class submarine program treated in Multiyear procurement and major weapon production is a worked example of a limited-industrial-base judgment that is distinct from any one-holder vehicle.

The justification for a sole-source award under FAR 6.302-1 should not be confused with the justification for an exception to fair opportunity that supports a sole-source order under a multiple-award vehicle, the document described in Indefinite-delivery contracts. The first departs from full and open competition for an award; the second departs from fair opportunity among existing holders for an order. Both produce something correctly called a sole-source action, but they live at different levels of the structure — one at the parent vehicle, one at the order — which is exactly why the holder-count and competition axes must be tracked separately. The same separation matters for small-business set-asides and recertification, where a single holder's eligibility can shift over a vehicle's life, as developed in Small business in defense contracting.

The acquisition lifecycle, source selection, and contracting authority

The word authority does more work in defense contracting than almost any other, and it names several unrelated concepts that are routinely collapsed into one. A program's statutory authorization, an agency's appropriation, the budget-process term contract authority, an official's delegated power to bind the Government, and the power to place orders under an existing vehicle are five different things, only some of which are forms of budget authority and only one of which describes the person who signs a contract. This chapter follows an award from the mission need that precedes any solicitation through the source-selection decision and into the post-award protest window; separates the several meanings of "authority"; identifies who actually makes the award decision in the two most common procedures — a FAR Part 15 negotiated source selection and a FAR Subpart 16.5 multiple-award order — describes the supporting cast around the decision; and explains how far the individual decision-maker can be traced from public data. It develops the second analytical warning introduced earlier in this reference: that being within an instrument's scope is necessary but never sufficient, because a lawful contract action must also be executed by an official who holds the authority to take it.

The pre-award acquisition lifecycle

A contract award is the late, visible terminus of a long internal process, and most of the decisions that determine the eventual award — the requirement itself, the vehicle, any set-aside, the assigned NAICS code, and the evaluation strategy — are settled well before a final solicitation is published. The chain runs from an articulated mission need, through acquisition planning and market research, into a sequence of pre-solicitation exchanges with industry, and only then into the formal solicitation, evaluation, and selection that the public award record reflects. The stages, with their principal regulatory anchors, are these:

  • a mission need — an articulated operational gap or demand, typically owned by an end user or capability sponsor rather than by a contracting organization;
  • a requirement owner / requiring activity — the program or organization that converts the need into a defined requirement and sponsors its funding;
  • acquisition planning under FAR Part 7 — the development of an acquisition strategy and a written acquisition plan that fix, among other things, the contracting approach, competition strategy, and milestones;118
  • market research under FAR Part 10 — the analysis of the available industrial base and of commercial capability that informs whether and how to compete the requirement;119
  • a sources sought notice or request for information (RFI) — a published notice inviting interested firms to identify themselves and their capabilities, often the first public signal of a forthcoming requirement;
  • an industry day and one-on-one exchanges — structured and individual pre-solicitation communications in which the Government describes the emerging requirement and industry responds;
  • a draft solicitation — a draft request for quotations (RFQ) or request for proposals (RFP) released for comment, exposing the proposed requirement, vehicle, set-aside, NAICS code, and evaluation factors before they are fixed;
  • the final RFQ / RFP — the binding solicitation against which offers are submitted;
  • evaluation and discussions — the assessment of offers against the stated factors and, where conducted, exchanges with offerors in the competitive range;
  • the source-selection decision — the best-value (or other stated-basis) selection of the awardee;
  • award — the signed contract or order that binds the Government;
  • a debriefing under FAR 15.506 — the post-award explanation provided to unsuccessful (and, on request, successful) offerors;120 and
  • a protest under FAR Subpart 33.1 or before the Government Accountability Office (GAO) — the formal challenge to the conduct or outcome of the procurement.121

FAR 15.201 expressly encourages exchanges of information with industry before the receipt of proposals, including presolicitation notices, draft solicitations, requests for information, presolicitation and preproposal conferences, industry days, and one-on-one meetings, so long as the exchanges are consistent with procurement integrity requirements.99 The analytical consequence is that the leverage available to a prospective offeror is front-loaded. Responding to a sources-sought notice or commenting on a draft solicitation can be more strategically consequential than responding to the final solicitation, because by the time the final RFP issues, the requirement, the contract vehicle, any set-aside, the assigned NAICS code, and the evaluation strategy have usually already been shaped — frequently in direct response to what industry said during the earlier exchanges. The same pre-solicitation notices are the demand signals consumed for opportunity intelligence in Recompetes and opportunity intelligence from awards data; read forward they are notices an offeror can act on, and read backward they are the published record of how a requirement took shape. Alternative paths — an Other Transaction, a Broad Agency Announcement, or a Commercial Solutions Opening — substitute their own pre-award sequences for parts of this chain, as catalogued in Alternative acquisition pathways and "not a contract type" traps.

Five conditions an award must satisfy

The lifecycle above can be compressed into a neutral analytical model of what must be simultaneously true for a contract award to actually occur. An award is not produced by enthusiasm, by a budget figure, or by a single official's preference; it requires the convergence of five distinct conditions, each owned by a different actor and governed by a different body of rule. The conditions are:

# Condition What it requires Who supplies it
1 A real end-user requirement An articulated mission demand, not merely interest The end user / capability sponsor
2 An owning organization A program or requiring activity that holds the requirement The program office / requiring activity
3 Available, correct appropriation Funds that are available and properly chargeable to the requirement The comptroller / resource manager
4 A lawful acquisition path A FAR contract, an order, an Other Transaction, an SBIR Phase III, or a subcontract The contracting authority
5 Demonstrable delivery capability Integration, testing, and schedule sufficient to perform The offeror

Several corollaries follow directly, and each corrects a common misreading of how awards arise. A verbal endorsement from an operator satisfies only the first condition; mission interest is necessary but does not by itself create a requirement, a budget, or a contract. A contracting officer cannot manufacture the requirement or the budget — the contracting authority supplies the fourth condition (a lawful path) but neither the demand of the first nor the appropriation of the third. A program manager ordinarily cannot bind the Government; program approval supplies the second condition but not the signature that effects an award, which remains the contracting officer's, as developed below and in Post-award administration, modifications, and closeout. And a budget line does not by itself award a contract; an appropriation satisfies the third condition but must still be joined to a requirement, a lawful path, and a capable performer. The model parallels two distinctions drawn elsewhere in this reference: the split between event-probability and addressability that underlies the two-score recompete model in Recompetes and opportunity intelligence from awards data, where a likely event and an addressable opportunity are independent facts; and the executable-funding discipline of CLINs, SLINs, and funding traceability, where money must be not merely appropriated but available and correctly chargeable on a line item before it can be obligated.

The several meanings of "authority"

Because the same word is attached to budget law, program law, procurement law, and individual delegations, the first task in reading any contracting statement is to ask which authority is meant. The following table separates the senses commonly confused.

Term Meaning
Contracting officer authority Delegated authority — usually evidenced by a warrant — to bind the United States within stated limits
Ordering authority Authority granted by a vehicle and a delegation to place task or delivery orders against it
Budgetary contract authority A form of budget authority allowing obligations to be incurred in advance of appropriations
Program authorization Statutory authority for a program, frequently in an authorization act such as the National Defense Authorization Act (NDAA)
Appropriation or other budget authority Legal authority to incur obligations and usually to make payments for specified purposes
Awarding/contracting agency The organization conducting the procurement; not a form of budget authority

Two distinctions in this table cause the most trouble. The first separates the budget-law sense from the procurement sense of the bare phrase "contract authority." The specialized budget term contract authority is a form of budget authority that permits an agency to enter into binding obligations in advance of an appropriation, with later liquidating appropriations required to pay them.100 It is a feature of how Congress structures spending power, and it is a different concept from the ordinary power of a contracting officer to sign a procurement contract that is already funded. The second distinction separates ordering authority from contracting-officer authority: the power to place an order against an existing vehicle is conferred by the vehicle and a delegation, and is narrower than, though dependent on, the general warrant power to bind the Government. The two intersect when an ordering contracting officer issues a task order, treated below and in Indefinite-delivery contracts.

Contracting-officer authority is delegated and bounded

A contracting officer may enter into, administer, modify, or terminate contracts, and make related determinations and findings, only to the extent of the authority delegated to that officer (FAR 1.602-1).101 That delegation is usually evidenced by a warrant — a certificate of appointment stating any limits, such as a dollar ceiling or a restriction to particular kinds of actions. The consequence runs throughout this reference: no matter how senior the official or how strongly a program wants a result, only a warranted contracting officer acting within the scope of the warrant can bind the United States. The same rule is why only a contracting officer can sign a contract modification on the Government's behalf, and why apparent direction from an unwarranted official does not obligate the Government. Authority in this sense is therefore both delegated — it flows down from the agency head through appointment — and bounded — it stops at the limits the warrant states.

Who makes the award decision

The phrase "who made the award" has two distinct answers that are easy to conflate: who selected the awardee, and who signed the resulting contract. The selection decision and the binding signature can rest with different people, and which roles exist at all depends on the acquisition procedure.

A FAR Part 15 negotiated procurement

In a competitive negotiated procurement under FAR Part 15, the decision is deliberately divided among roles:

  • the evaluation team evaluates proposals against the solicitation's stated criteria;
  • the Source Selection Authority (SSA) makes the selection decision — the choice of which proposal represents the best value to the Government;
  • the contracting officer controls procurement communications with offerors and awards the contract; and
  • the contracting officer is the default SSA unless another official is appointed to that role.

The selection rests with the SSA, who must exercise independent judgment based on the factors and significant subfactors in the solicitation, even when relying on the reports, ratings, and analyses prepared by the evaluation team (FAR 15.303).102 The SSA's role is thus not to ratify the evaluators' tally but to make a reasoned, documented best-value decision that the evaluation record supports. Separately, the person who signs the contract and thereby legally binds the Government is the authorized contracting officer. When no separate SSA is appointed, the contracting officer occupies both roles, but the two functions remain conceptually distinct: selecting the awardee and binding the Government are different acts, and in larger acquisitions they are usually performed by different people.

A FAR 16.5 multiple-award order

The picture changes for an order placed against an existing multiple-award vehicle. For a FAR Subpart 16.5 task or delivery order, the ordering contracting officer generally has broad discretion to design streamlined order-placement procedures appropriate to the requirement.103 The formal source-selection procedures of FAR Subpart 15.3 — including the appointment of an SSA and the apparatus of a full source-selection plan — do not automatically apply to a fair-opportunity order competition, although the vehicle's terms or the individual order solicitation may adopt similar procedures by choice. In other words, the multiple-award structure was already established when the parent vehicle was awarded; placing an order draws on the ordering authority the parent created rather than reopening a Part 15 selection, so the decision-maker for an order is ordinarily the ordering contracting officer exercising fair-opportunity discretion rather than a separately appointed SSA. The codified-versus-deviation citation note is material here: under the current DoD class deviation adopting the Revolutionary FAR Overhaul, fair-opportunity procedures sit primarily at FAR 16.507, whereas still-codified FAR places many of them at FAR 16.505.103

The supporting roles

A source selection or an order decision is the product of many participants, only two of whom can actually decide the award or bind the Government. The following table sets out the typical division of labor.

Participant Typical role
Program or requiring office Defines the need and the technical requirement
Technical evaluation team Evaluates technical submissions
Cost/price analysts Assess price, cost, and realism where applicable
Legal counsel Advises on process, scope, protests, and documentation
Small-business specialist / SBA Advises or acts on set-aside and program issues
Resource manager Certifies that funds are available and properly chargeable

The resource manager's certification connects directly to the funding dimension: an award cannot be obligated unless funds are available and properly chargeable to the requirement, the discipline traced through CLINs, SLINs, and funding traceability. The small-business specialist's role connects to the set-aside and program decisions described in Single-award, single-source, and sole-source. A common error is to assume that the most senior or most visible official is the one who decided the award. A program manager, a program executive officer (PEO), or a Milestone Decision Authority may approve the underlying program or its acquisition strategy, but that approval does not by itself make any of those officials the SSA or the contracting officer. The acquisition-strategy approval and the source-selection decision are different acts performed under different authorities, and the signature that binds the Government remains the contracting officer's.

What public data shows about competition

Because the published solicitation sits late in the lifecycle, the public record systematically understates how much of a competition was already settled before any document appeared on a contract-opportunities portal. Three properties of the available public data follow from this timing, and each bounds what an analyst can responsibly infer about competition.

First, the published solicitation is relatively late. By the time a final RFQ or RFP is visible, the shaping decisions of the pre-award lifecycle — requirement, vehicle, set-aside, NAICS code, and evaluation strategy — are largely fixed, so the solicitation reveals the outcome of the shaping window rather than the deliberations within it. The earlier sources-sought notices, RFIs, and draft solicitations are the more revealing artifacts, which is why they are treated as demand signals in Recompetes and opportunity intelligence from awards data.

Second, forecasts and long-range estimates are nonbinding planning clues. Agency acquisition forecasts and the long-range acquisition estimates contemplated by FAR 5.404 are explicitly planning information; they signal anticipated requirements but commit the Government to nothing, and a forecast entry is not a solicitation, a funded requirement, or a promise to compete.104 An analyst can read them as evidence of intent and approximate timing, not as evidence that an award will occur as described or on the stated schedule.

Third, CPARS is controlled and not public. The Contractor Performance Assessment Reporting System (CPARS) — the Government's record of contractor past performance — is source-selection-sensitive and access-controlled, not a public dataset, so the performance history that materially informs many best-value decisions cannot be reconstructed from open sources.105 CPARS is the past-performance feed described in Post-award administration, modifications, and closeout, and its controlled status is a principal reason the evidentiary basis for a selection is harder to see than the fact of the award.

These three limits sharpen the same evidentiary asymmetry developed below for the decision-maker: the public record is reliable about organizations and the fact of an award and unreliable about the deliberative inputs and the named individuals behind it. The awarding and funding office are traceable from public data in a way the individual decision-maker, the source-selection rationale, and the underlying past-performance record are not.

How far the decision-maker can be traced publicly

The recurring theme of this reference — that public data trace the account and office far better than the exact budget line and the named individual — applies sharply to authority. From a signed contract or modification and the federal data systems, one can usually identify the awarding agency, the awarding office, the funding agency, the funding office, and sometimes the contracting officer named on the instrument. What is generally not a standard field is the identity of the SSA or of the individual evaluators: that information is not carried in USAspending and instead may surface only in a source-selection decision document, a debriefing, a GAO or court protest record, the contract file, a congressional record, or a FOIA production.106

The cleanest way to state the limit is comparative. Public data usually let an analyst trace the funding account better than the exact budget line, and the awarding office better than the individual decision-maker. Recovering an exact Program Element or budget-line item, or attaching a name to the source-selection decision, normally requires the underlying procurement and accounting documents rather than the public award record. This is the same evidentiary asymmetry drawn for budget lines in CLINs, SLINs, and funding traceability: the structures the data systems are designed to report — organizations, accounts, transactions — are visible, while the structures they are not designed to report — internal deliberative roles and the finest budget granularity — generally are not. For the reader of a contract announcement, the practical rule is to treat the awarding office and funding account as knowable from public data, and to treat the named SSA and the exact budget line as facts that ordinarily must be sourced from the procurement file itself.

Alternative acquisition pathways and "not a contract type" traps

A recurring source of error in reading United States defense contracting is the habit of treating any prominent acronym as though it named a type of contract or a pricing arrangement. Many of the field's most visible labels do neither. They describe a program or statutory authority, a solicitation method, a customer or funding context, a competition rule, an acquisition classification, or a structural unit of a contract — each of which answers a different one of the dimensions set out in The dimensions of a federal contract. This closing chapter collects the terms most often misfiled as contract or pricing types, maps each to the dimension it actually belongs to, and gives short notes on the ones that cause the most trouble. The governing principle, stated at the end, is the one this reference applies throughout: always say which dimension a term belongs to, and the confusion dissolves.

The remaining sections expand the entries that are most often mistaken for contract or pricing types.

Programs and authorities: SBIR, STTR, and OTA

SBIR (Small Business Innovation Research) and STTR (Small Business Technology Transfer) are research and small-business innovation programs, not contract or pricing types. A given SBIR award is funded and competed under the program's rules, but the resulting instrument is still classified on the ordinary dimensions — it has an instrument form, a pricing type, a period of performance, and so on. Describing an award as "an SBIR" identifies the program that produced it, in much the same way "small business" identifies a competition-and-eligibility status rather than a contract type, as developed in Small business in defense contracting.

OTA — Other Transaction authority — is the most consequential entry in this chapter, because it is the one most often misread as a kind of contract. OTA is a statutory authority to enter into transactions that are generally outside the standard FAR procurement-contract framework. An "Other Transaction" is therefore not a FAR contract type at all, and the pricing-type and ordering vocabulary built on the FAR — firm-fixed-price, IDIQ, fair opportunity — does not map onto it in the ordinary way. The correct statement is dimensional: OTA answers the question under what authority is the Government acting, and the answer "an Other Transaction" places the instrument outside, rather than within, the body of rules that the rest of this reference describes. Calling an Other Transaction a "contract type" both misstates its legal nature and invites the assumption that the standard FAR mechanics apply to it.

Solicitation methods: BAA and CSO

A Broad Agency Announcement (BAA) is a competitive solicitation method, used especially to solicit research proposals and basic and applied science. A Commercial Solutions Opening (CSO) is likewise a solicitation method, oriented toward acquiring innovative commercial items. Neither term describes how the contractor is paid or what ordering structure the award uses. Both answer the question how was this requirement solicited and competed — a method dimension that is logically prior to, and independent of, the instrument and pricing that the resulting award carries. A BAA or CSO can lead to a fixed-price award, a cost-reimbursement award, or, in some cases, an Other Transaction; the solicitation method does not determine the pricing type any more than the choice to advertise a job determines the salary. Treating "BAA" or "CSO" as a contract type collapses the solicitation-method dimension into the instrument and pricing dimensions that it sits above.

Customer and funding context: FMS

Foreign Military Sales (FMS) is a customer and funding context: the United States Government sells defense articles and services to a foreign government, and the foreign customer's funds (or authorized financing) pay for them. FMS is neither an instrument nor a pricing type. The underlying acquisition still uses ordinary contract instruments and ordinary pricing arrangements; what "FMS" tells the reader is who the ultimate customer is and whose money funds the work, which is a context distinct from how the contract is structured or priced. Reading "FMS" as a contract type obscures the fact that an FMS case is implemented through the same kinds of contracts as a domestic acquisition.

Vehicle designation: BIC

Best-in-Class (BIC) is a governmentwide designation applied to a contract vehicle that has been recognized as a preferred solutionwide option. It is a label attached to a vehicle, not a new instrument or pricing type and not a property of any individual order. Like the additive reading of GWAC and Schedule developed in IDV, IDIQ, MAC, GWAC, and Schedules, the correct reading of BIC is layered: a designated vehicle is still whatever instrument it already was — typically a multiple-award IDIQ or a Schedule — with a governmentwide designation added on top. "BIC" describes the vehicle's standing, not its mechanics.

Competition rules: set-aside and sole source

A set-aside is a value on the competition dimension: it restricts who may compete for a requirement, most commonly reserving it for small businesses or a socioeconomic category. A sole-source award is also a competition-dimension concept: it describes a requirement that is awarded without competition, to a single source, generally on a documented justification such as only one responsible source under FAR 6.302-1.107 Neither term names an instrument or a pricing type. Crucially, the competition dimension is independent of the award-structure dimension — the number of vehicle holders — so that single-award (one holder) is not the same thing as sole source (no competition), and a single-award contract can be the product of vigorous competition. That distinction is the subject of Single-award, single-source, and sole-source. Set-asides as an eligibility mechanism, and the rule of two that drives them, are developed in Small business in defense contracting. Reading "set-aside" or "sole source" as a contract type fuses the competition question with the instrument and pricing questions that answer entirely different things.

Acquisition classification: commercial product or service

A commercial product or commercial service is an acquisition classification under FAR Part 12, not a pricing type. The two are defined terms in FAR 2.101, where they describe what is being bought rather than how it is priced.108 The classification governs which streamlined procedures and clauses apply and reflects a determination that the item or service is of a type sold or offered in the commercial marketplace. A commercial-item acquisition still carries an ordinary pricing type — most often firm-fixed-price, but not necessarily — and an ordinary instrument. "Commercial" answers the question how is this item classified for purposes of applicable procedures, which is distinct from how the contractor is paid. Treating the commercial classification as though it were itself the pricing type conflates the FAR Part 12 classification dimension with the pricing-and-risk dimension treated in Pricing types and cost risk.

Definitization status: UCA and letter contract

An undefinitized contract action (UCA), the broad form of which is the letter contract, is a preliminary or undefinitized contractual arrangement that authorizes the contractor to begin work before the final terms — most importantly the price or estimated cost — have been negotiated. It is not a distinct pricing type; it is a status on the way to a definitized pricing type. The arrangement exists precisely because the parties have agreed to start work before agreeing on price, and it is later definitized into firm terms through a modification, a process noted among the kinds of modification in Contract modifications. The correct dimensional statement is that a UCA or letter contract describes whether the price has yet been settled, not how the contractor will ultimately be paid. Reading a UCA as a pricing type misses that its defining feature is the absence, for the moment, of a settled price.

Structural unit and contract action: CLIN and modification

A Contract Line Item Number (CLIN) is a structural unit of a contract — a numbered line that organizes what is being bought, how it is funded, when it is delivered, and how it is priced. A single contract or order routinely carries several CLINs, and different CLINs on the same order may even carry different pricing types, as the worked example in The dimensions of a federal contract shows. A CLIN is therefore a unit within a contract, not a contract or a pricing type itself; the CLIN structure, its sub-line items, and funding traceability are the subject of CLINs, SLINs, and funding traceability.

A modification is a contract action — a written change to an existing contract or order — and is not inherently a new contract. It is defined by its relationship to an already-awarded instrument rather than by its size, so a modification can be administrative or substantive, funded or unfunded, tiny or among the largest actions in a program, as Contract modifications develops. The recurring error is to read a large modification as if it were a new award, or to read the word "modification" as conveying something about pricing; it conveys only that an existing instrument was changed.

Making the innovation pathways operational

The four innovation labels in the map above — SBIR/STTR, Other Transactions, the Broad Agency Announcement, and the Commercial Solutions Opening — are the entries most likely to be mistaken not only for contract types but for interchangeable on-ramps. They are not interchangeable. Each is a distinct route with its own way for a firm to enter, its own range of resulting instruments, its own mechanism for moving from an early effort to follow-on work, its own answer to whether that follow-on must be competed again, and its own footprint in public award data. The map is enough to file each label under the right dimension; the four facets below describe how each pathway actually operates, which is what determines where a prospective offeror enters and how the resulting action appears — or fails to appear — to an outside analyst.

The pathways share a common shape: an early, lower-dollar effort (a study, a prototype, or a research project) followed, if it succeeds, by a transition decision and then by some larger follow-on. What differs is the legal machinery at each step. Other Transactions (OT): research and prototype authorities, and the production follow-on

An Other Transaction is not a FAR contract, as the map records; operationally, the distinction that matters most is between the two statutory authorities and what each enables downstream. A research OT is entered under 10 U.S.C. 4021 for basic, applied, and advanced research projects, while a prototype OT is entered under 10 U.S.C. 4022 for prototype projects directly relevant to enhancing the mission effectiveness of military personnel and supporting systems.109 A firm frequently enters either through a consortium that manages a portfolio of projects for a sponsoring agency, or through a direct solicitation; the resulting instrument is an OT agreement negotiated outside the standard FAR procurement-contract framework. The operationally decisive feature is the follow-on: a competitively awarded prototype OT that is completed successfully can support a follow-on production action — itself an OT — without further competition, provided the original solicitation and resulting agreement expressly contemplated the production follow-on. That authority, implemented for DoD at DFARS 206.001-70, is what allows a prototype effort to mature into production without a fresh open competition, and it is why "competitively awarded" and "completed successfully" are not incidental adjectives but the statutory preconditions for the production transition.109

SBIR / STTR: the three-phase structure and the Phase III follow-on

The SBIR and STTR programs, authorized under 15 U.S.C. 638, are operationally a three-phase progression rather than a single award. A firm enters by winning a competitive proposal against a published topic: Phase I establishes technical feasibility; Phase II develops the technology, typically to a prototype; and Phase III is the commercialization or transition stage in which the technology derived from the Phase I/II work is brought into use, including by another agency or program.110 The transition mechanism is the defining operational fact: a Phase III award derives from the earlier SBIR/STTR work, may be awarded sole-source, and need not be re-competed, because the competition that justifies it occurred at the Phase I/II stage. A Phase III action is not confined to a single instrument form — it can be a FAR contract or another instrument — so the same authority can surface as an ordinary contract action even though no new competition preceded it. For an outside analyst, a Phase III award entered by a prior SBIR awardee is the pathway's least visible step precisely because it can look like any other sole-source action.

Broad Agency Announcement (BAA): a research solicitation method

A Broad Agency Announcement is a solicitation method under FAR 35.016, used for basic and applied research and that part of advanced technology development not directed at a specific system or hardware solution.111 A firm enters by submitting a white paper or proposal against a standing announcement that may remain open for an extended period. The operational character that distinguishes a BAA from ordinary source selection is its evaluation: proposals undergo peer or scientific review against the announcement's stated criteria, rather than head-to-head price competition among offerors for a single defined requirement. A BAA results in FAR contracts (or, where appropriate, assistance instruments), and it is best understood as a method for soliciting research, not as a follow-on engine: any later production or scale-up is a separate action taken under whatever authority then applies, not an automatic continuation of the BAA award.

Commercial Solutions Opening (CSO): a competitive method for innovative commercial items

A Commercial Solutions Opening is a competitive solicitation method oriented toward acquiring innovative commercial items, technologies, and services.112 A firm enters by responding to the CSO with a proposed solution, which is evaluated on a competitive basis under the opening's criteria. The operationally important point is that a CSO is instrument-agnostic at the output: it can result in a FAR contract or an Other Transaction, and the follow-on consequences track whichever instrument is chosen — a FAR award carries ordinary FAR follow-on mechanics, while an OT award can carry the prototype-to-production follow-on described above when the CSO and resulting agreement contemplated it. Because the CSO is a method rather than an instrument, reading it as a contract type obscures the more useful question of which instrument it produced and therefore which follow-on rules attach.

How these routes relate to competition posture and to recompete intelligence

These statutory routes are properly read as a competition-dimension fact, but a distinct one. A Phase III award and a production OT following a successful prototype both proceed lawfully without ordinary open competition — yet they are not instances of the general sole-source posture that Single-award, single-source, and sole-source develops. That chapter owns the FAR Part 6 sole-source concept, in which an award departs from full and open competition on a documented justification; the innovation follow-ons here instead rest on their own statutory authorities (15 U.S.C. 638 for Phase III; 10 U.S.C. 4022 with DFARS 206.001-70 for the production OT), under which the absence of a fresh competition is built into the authority rather than justified as an exception to it. The two should be cross-read, not merged: both yield an action with no head-to-head competition, but on different legal bases.

The same feature creates a specific data trap. Because a Phase III award or a production-OT follow-on lawfully proceeds without a new competition, it will not appear as an open competition in opportunity data — there is no fresh solicitation to observe. An analyst scanning for competed opportunities will therefore systematically miss work that flows through these routes, which is the innovation-follow-on trap treated in Recompetes and opportunity intelligence: the follow-on materializes as a sole-source-looking action (or, for an OT, as uneven and sometimes sparse reporting) rather than as a contestable opening. Finally, these pathways differ from ordinary FAR contracting in their data-rights terms — SBIR/STTR data rights and OT intellectual-property provisions are negotiated on bases distinct from the standard FAR clauses — a difference developed in Prime–subcontractor structure, privity, and flowdowns.

The governing principle

Every term in this chapter is frequently misread as a "type of contract" or a pricing arrangement when in fact each answers a different dimensional question. SBIR, STTR, and OTA answer which program or authority; BAA and CSO answer which solicitation method; FMS answers which customer and funding context; BIC answers which designation a vehicle carries; set-aside and sole source answer which competition rule; commercial product or service answers which acquisition classification; a UCA or letter contract answers whether the price has been definitized; a CLIN answers which structural unit; and a modification answers what kind of action was taken. The operating rule of this entire reference, set out first in The core vocabulary, is that a term should always be paired with the dimension it belongs to. A term correctly assigned to its dimension is rarely misread; a term borrowed across dimensions — pricing read onto a program, a solicitation method read as an instrument, a context read as a contract — almost always is. Map each label to its dimension, and the apparent tangle of acronyms resolves into a set of independent, individually answerable questions about the same award.

The one dimension genuinely concerned with pricing arrangements — the choice among fixed-price, incentive, cost-reimbursement, and time-and-materials contract types — is the subject of FAR Part 16. As of the article date (June 25, 2026), DoD contracting officers apply the Revolutionary FAR Overhaul rewrite of FAR Part 16 under a class deviation effective March 16, 2026.113 That deviation governs how pricing arrangements are selected; it does not turn any of the programs, authorities, solicitation methods, customer contexts, designations, competition rules, classifications, or structural units cataloged in this chapter into a pricing type.114
