# Why these slides matter most right now

The boss question that these three slides answer -- Q2, "what's in scope vs
out of scope and how do you know?" -- is THE pivotal question of the walk-in.
Every other slide depends on the $7.1B TAM being a defensible number. If these
three slides aren't tight, nothing downstream lands.

The v1.3 draft attempted this with three separate arguments:
  - Slide 3 (waterfall): reduce $56.6B "total pool" to $7.1B via subtractions
  - Slide 9 (appendix): compose $7.1B into ~10 federal appropriations
  - Slide 10 (appendix): hedge with Frame A vs Frame B apportionment

Two problems with v1.3:
  (a) The $56.6B "total pool" on Slide 3 is a frankenpool that mixes FPDS
      obligations with budget-implied labor and reactor procurement. The
      tie-out is suspiciously exact because the pool is defined as the sum
      of the deductions.
  (b) The $9,536M "implied public-yard labor" on Slide 3 has three weak
      joints (wrong top, wrong bottom, wrong residual-interpretation) and
      cannot be defended with a workbook cell.

v1.4 response (structural):
  - Slide 3 reframes as a 2x2 matrix. The $7.1B is the intersection of two
    independent axes, not the residual of a subtraction chain. Adjacent cells
    (public-yard labor, newbuild PSC 1905, reactor PSC 4470, unmeasured
    in-house RDT&E) are shown explicitly, not subtracted.
  - Slide 4 (promoted from appendix) drops the $7.4B pre-exclusion universe
    and rebases all appropriation figures to $7,067M. The 49 / 51 direct vs
    imputed TAS attribution split moves from footnote to foreground.
  - Slide 10 trims to a single appendix slide with decision-linked framing.

v1.5 response (strategic framing, layered on v1.4 structure):
  - Slide 3 reframes from "intersection of two measurement systems" to
    "awards-primary methodology, budget materials as reconciliation." A new
    callout card ("Why awards-first, not budget-first") makes the four
    methodology advantages explicit on-slide.
  - Slide 4 reframes from "here's where the $7.1B comes from on the budget
    side" (descriptive) to "the appropriation composition is the diagnostic
    that proves awards-first is the right base" (diagnostic). The OPN 35%
    finding moves from a descriptive callout to the lede and foreground
    takeaway card.

# RHS COMMENTARY -- FOUR CALLOUT CARDS

**[FOREGROUND] Why awards-first, not budget-first**
Awards data tags every transaction with customer-assigned PSC, vendor, vessel, and period -- and scopes to contractor-addressable $ automatically. Budget books carry no comparable tagging; budget-first sizing would mix in civilian federal payroll and miss OPN-funded MRO (see Slide 4).

**Top-right -- Public-yard activity (budget-side only)**
FY25 OMN Ship Maintenance (SAG 1B4B) transferred $7.5B via RRN customer orders to the four public naval shipyards: Norfolk ($1.79B), Portsmouth ($1.47B), Puget Sound ($2.74B), Pearl Harbor ($1.49B). Total public-yard activity is bounded at $12-15B once SCN-funded CVN RCOH / SSN EOH / inactivation work, OPN ship-alt installs, and RDT&E customer orders are added. Civilian federal workforce executes this work; none of it generates FPDS contract records. This cell is where the "submarine MRO looks understated" story lives -- most nuclear depot work on SSNs, SSBNs, and CVNs is performed in-house at Portsmouth, Puget Sound, and Pearl Harbor. A budget-first methodology would either include this $ (sizing a non-contractor-addressable market) or exclude it on judgment (inheriting the exclusion boundary from awards data anyway).

**Bottom-left -- Newbuild and reactor PSCs (FPDS-visible, non-MRO by PSC)**
PSC 1905 is predominantly ship construction, but is known to contain MRO -- the two largest examples in FY2025 are Electric Boat's $831M Fleet Directed Work (Special Hull Treatment Seam Split Repairs on Virginia-class SSNs) and HII's $424M USS Boise SSN-764 Engineered Overhaul at Newport News, both coded 1905 rather than J998 / J999. Under a work-type-first classification, an estimated ~$1.5-2.0B of PSC 1905 would migrate into the MRO TAM -- concentrated on submarines (~$1.25B, 5% of the sub-tagged PSC 1905) and aircraft carriers (~$320M, 22% of the carrier-tagged PSC 1905). This is the load-bearing reason the $7.1B Services TAM is a floor, not a ceiling, on submarine and carrier MRO activity visible in FPDS. PSC 4470 is reactor-component procurement (not sustainment) -- new cores, long-lead forgings, and reactor OEM spares.

# TITLE
Appropriation Sourcing | OPN funds 35% of MRO, nearly matching OMN -- MRO is not a single-appropriation market

# LEDE
The clearest evidence that awards data is the right measurement base is the appropriation composition that the awards data recovers. OPN -- a procurement appropriation -- funds 35% of MRO obligations, nearly matching OMN's 37%. The remaining ~28% spans RDT&E Defense-Wide (11%), USCG, Air Force, Army, SCN, and smaller buckets. No single appropriation is "the maintenance line"; a budget-first methodology would have to correctly split MRO across ~10 appropriations using rules that the data itself shows to be non-obvious. Awards data recovers this apportionment by aggregating TAS attributions from individual contract awards, rather than inferring it from budget structure.

# JOB OF THIS SLIDE
Close the loop on Slide 3's methodology argument. Slide 3 asserts that awards data is the primary measurement base; Slide 4 provides the diagnostic proof. Reading the two slides back-to-back, the boss should conclude that a budget-first methodology -- attempting to tag line items as "MRO" vs "non-MRO" from OMN / OPN / RDT&E justification books -- would almost certainly have either undercounted MRO (by missing OPN-funded replacement equipment) or overcounted it (by including public-yard labor or newbuild MRO-adjacents). Awards data sidesteps both failure modes because work type is customer-tagged at the contract level.

# RHS CALLOUT CARDS -- TAKEAWAYS (in order of emphasis)

**[FOREGROUND] OPN funds ~35% of MRO -- MRO is a multi-appropriation market**
A procurement appropriation -- OPN -- funds nearly as much MRO as OMN does. This is visible in the Navy's own OMN Exhibit OP-5 SAG 1B4B, which partitions FY24 availabilities as 44 of 68 OMN-funded and 24 OPN-funded. Two distinct OPN streams flow into MRO: (1) OPN BA-01 Line Item 1000 Ship Maintenance, Repair and Modernization ($2.4B FY26 request) funds private-contracted availability labor in CPF and FFC; (2) OPN BA-7 Command Support Equipment and BA-8 Spares fund equipment installed and parts consumed during availabilities. Awards data recovers the full MRO composition because PSC, vendor, vessel, and work description jointly identify the activity regardless of appropriation color. A budget-first methodology is not impossible -- but it requires the analyst to know which OPN BAs to include and how to unbundle BA-01 ship-maintenance $ from PSC 1905 shipbuilding coding, which the awards tagging sidesteps entirely.

**49% of MRO $ are directly TAS-attributed; 51% are imputed from peer PSCs**
Direct attribution uses USAspending's /awards/funding/ endpoint (Treasury File C) to tie each award to its specific Treasury Account Symbol. Where the endpoint returns no match, the award inherits the appropriation mix observed in the directly-attributed peers within the same PSC bucket. Rollup totals are stable across the direct and imputed split; per-award precision is lower for the imputed subset.

**Navy OMN + OPN = 72% of MRO obligations; MRO is a Navy story, not a DoD-wide one**
USCG is only 4% of obligations. Air Force, Army, SCN, and DoD-wide appropriations together are ~13%. Sizing the Navy MRO market primarily from Navy appropriations is correct; but note again that "Navy appropriations" here means both OMN (maintenance color) and OPN (procurement color), not just OMN.

**Within MRO PSCs, OPN concentrates in BA-7 Command Support Equipment (62%) and BA-8 Spares (32%)**
BA-7 funds installation and modernization electronics, C4ISR, and combat-system integration performed during availabilities. BA-8 funds spares and repair parts (including VIRGINIA-class CNO availability materials). The large OPN Ship Maintenance program itself -- BA-01 Line Item 1000 ($2.4B FY26 request, legal basis PL 116-93) -- funds the private-contracted availability labor in CPF and FFC but does not flow through the 68 MRO PSCs in our TAM; it is likely coded to PSC 1905 in FPDS, which would make it part of the Slide 3 bottom-left "PSC 1905 embedded MRO" cell rather than the MRO TAM cell. The $1.5B BA-7 share visible in MRO PSCs is equipment-installs-during-availabilities -- itself the hardest bucket for a budget-first methodology to find, because it is a procurement BA funding work performed during a maintenance availability.

**RDT&E Defense-Wide contributes 11%**
Almost entirely Trident II, Strategic Systems Programs (SSP), and Space and Missile Defense Command (SMDC) sustainment on J-series PSCs. Draper MK7 Trident ($318M FY2025) is the single-largest MRO PIID and is funded here. An eleventh-hour budget-first methodology would likely have missed this entirely by not tracing J-series PSC work back to the DW-funded SSP portfolio.

**SCN contributes approximately $40M on MRO PSCs**
De minimis at the PSC level. Nuclear-platform MRO bundles under PSC 1905 shipbuilding rather than onto MRO PSCs; see Slide 3 for the full scope reconciliation.

# BREADCRUMB
Appendix / TAM Framing

# TITLE
TAM Framing | The $7.1B contracting TAM apportions to a ~$3.3-3.8B range of FY2025-delivered contractor revenue under period-of-performance sensitivity

# LEDE
The $7,067M MRO TAM measures contract obligations -- the flow of contract dollars transacted in FY2025. Contractor revenue earned in FY2025 is a different number, obtained by apportioning each award's obligation over its period of performance; that estimate falls in a $3.3-3.8B range depending on apportionment method. The deck uses the obligation number (Frame A) throughout; this appendix exists to support readers who model specific public comps on an earned-revenue basis (Frame B).

# RHS CALLOUT -- DECISION-LINKED FRAMING NOTE

Frame A measures the flow of contract dollars that the market will transact -- the number relevant to market-entry, capability-build, and acquisition decisions. Frame B approximates existing-contractor revenue -- the number relevant to modeling specific public comps on an earned-revenue basis. This deck is sizing the former. Readers modeling the latter for a specific contractor should multiply their relevant segment $ by that segment's apportionment rate (above), not by the blended 47.5% -- the 19-71% spread across segments makes the blended rate unsafe for single-segment modeling.
