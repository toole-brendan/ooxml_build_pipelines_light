The Navy's official Ship Work Breakdown Structure (SWBS) — now called the Expanded Ship Work Breakdown Structure (ESWBS) — is documented in several key sources.

Primary Official Sources
MIL-STD-881 (current version: 881F) — This is the DoD-wide standard for Work Breakdown Structures across all defense materiel items, including a dedicated appendix for Ship Systems WBS (Appendix E). It defines Level 1–3 WBS elements for ships, covering hull structure, propulsion, electric plant, command and surveillance, auxiliary systems, and more. You can access it through the ASSIST-QuickSearch database at 
quicksearch.dla.mil
.

NAVSEA ESWBS Publication — The Navy's own Expanded Ship Work Breakdown Structure is maintained by NAVSEA and is referenced in standards like MIL-STD-1399, which relates interface considerations to ESWBS 3-digit elements. This is the Navy-specific, more detailed structure used throughout a ship's entire life cycle for cost, weight, specifications, design, production, and maintenance.

ESWBS Group Structure
The ESWBS organizes a ship into 10 major functional groups using a 3-digit numbering system:

ESWBS Group	Description
000	General Guidance and Administration
100	Hull Structure
200	Propulsion Plant
300	Electric Plant
400	Command and Surveillance
500	Auxiliary Systems
600	Outfit and Furnishings
700	Armament
800	Integration/Engineering
900	Ship Assembly and Support Services
How to Access
MIL-STD-881 is publicly available through the 
ASSIST-QuickSearch portal
 maintained by the Defense Logistics Agency (DLA).

The full ESWBS (with detailed sub-group breakdowns) is a NAVSEA-controlled document. If you have access to NAVSEA's technical data repositories or a CAC-enabled login, you can retrieve it there. It's also sometimes referenced in contract specifications and ship design documents.

A partial ESWBS spreadsheet listing has been shared on sites like 
Boat Design Net
, which may give you a working-level view of the detailed 5-digit codes.

Scribd hosts community-uploaded versions of the SWBS breakdown that can be useful for reference, though they may not reflect the latest revision.

For your defense work, the MIL-STD-881 appendix is the best unclassified starting point, and the NAVSEA ESWBS document provides the full Navy-specific detail you'd need for cost modeling or specification work.

How They Relate
MIL-STD-881 Appendix E covers Sea Systems and provides a WBS for ship programs, but it uses the standard MIL-STD-881 hierarchical format (Level 1, Level 2, Level 3 elements) rather than the 3-digit numeric group codes. The Level 2 and 3 elements in Appendix E do align conceptually with the ESWBS groups (e.g., Hull Structure, Propulsion Plant, Electric Plant, etc.), but the numbering convention is different.

According to an NDIA briefing on MIL-STD-881C, Appendix E for Sea Systems noted that the "Ships WBS remains key lower level breakdown" — meaning the ESWBS serves as the detailed lower-level structure beneath the MIL-STD-881 framework.
